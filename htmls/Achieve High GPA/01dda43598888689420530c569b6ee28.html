Getting a 4.0 GPA

Edit
  • History
Comments (1) Share

I fundamentally believe that nearly everyone is capable of getting a 4.0 GPA. Instead, 1-3% of students get a 4.0 GPA. Why do I believe that we are all capable? I believe we are capable because the key to getting a high GPA is largely based on good planning, efficient study habits and strong test taking ability, moreso than individual capability and innate knowledge. There are five key steps that I have outlined below, that one should follow when trying to maximize their GPA. The steps are evaluate, plan, study, take test, correct. I will detail the five steps below. 

Evaluate:    

The first thing to do when trying to achieve a high GPA is to pick the right courses. Now, what are the “right” courses? The first thing you should consider when trying to pick the right courses is your general interest towards these courses. Many students take easier courses that they have little interest in, and actually do worse than they would have if they take a harder course they are actually interested in. So choose a course that interests you! Secondly, consider the difficulty of the course. You can gauge the difficulty by asking anyone on the Facebook group for that course, look at the ASSU calendar and finally look at course evaluations through ROSI. The two tabs that you should focus on the most in the course evaluation results are “assignments allowed demonstration of learning” and “course workload”. You want a high number in the former, and generally a lower number in the latter. Also, consider looking at ratemyprofessor.com to look at the professors teaching style. If it sounds like many students are satisfied with the professor than that is a positive sign, whereas if the professor is not well-liked, than it may be better to choose another course. Furthermore, try to get a copy of the course’s syllabus, either through a Facebook group or by searching in Google. The syllabus can be used to determine whether the course is either essay-based or test-based. That is not to say a course cannot be both essay and test based, but generally most classes favor essays or tests for 70%+ of the course.   

Now, I am going to expose you to one of the biggest fallacies that UofT students fall victim to. The fallacy of essay based courses being easier than test based courses. This is only a half-truth. For instance, essay based courses are typically easier to get between 50-78% in, but test-based courses are considerably easier to get 85%+ in. This is due to the innate marking scheme that is required for essays and tests. Essays are mostly subjective, whereas tests are mostly objective. Subjectivity is always a gamble when it comes to GPA. You may write a paper that is worth 40% and the TA may love it and give you an 88% on the assignment, or the same paper may receive a 68% from another TA. Now, obviously the university takes precautions to make sure that grading is standard across TAs, but the guidelines and restrictions the professor gives to the TAs pertaining to marking is very fallible, and way too easy to ignore. In the end, essay-based courses are more of a gamble; you can write mediocre papers and get exceptional marks, write exceptional papers and get mediocre marks, or fall somewhere in between. When you add in the fact that you take 10 courses per year, you may not want to take the gamble of whether a certain TA likes your paper or not. Tests, on the other hand, are generally more objective. That means that if you thoroughly know the content of the course, it is indeed realistic to get above a 90 on a test-- the same cannot be said about a paper. 

Next, look at whether you can gain bonus marks or not in the course. I urge everyone reading this article to attain every bonus mark possible in every one of their courses. So often students neglect to get maximum bonus marks (if they are available) and are 1-2% off a higher GPA bracket. So, in the end, think to yourself whether attending a conference is worth an extra percent overall, and potentially the difference between a 3.3 to a 3.7, or 3.7 to a 4.0 in the course. Consider whether there are group projects as well. Some people love group projects, and some people despise them, so remember to check if collaboration is required for assignments. On that note, if you hate presentations, choose a course that does not have any presentation component.    

Make sure to also look at the previous exams through the exam repository. Read through them, and try to determine the level of subjectivity present in the test, and whether the test is in fact fair. At UofT, you will take tests that are not representative of the course material you learned, so try to make sure the test is completely on topic and fair. If you know anyone who has taken the course, try to gauge how well they did in the course, as this can be loosely correlated to how well you will do (if you have the same learning style). If someone who had a similar learning style to you did poorly in the class, then you should choose another course if you can. 

Lastly, evaluate your personal academic goals honestly. Truly ask yourself what grade you want in every course and ask yourself how willing you are to attain this grade. Most students say they want to 4.0 a course, but are unwilling to study more than 6 hours the day before the test. If that is you, then you have to plan how your attitude and levels of effort can allow you to succeed in a course. It is much easier to achieve a high GPA if you truly have a reason to achieve it, such as for med/law/grad school, or even to appease your family. So, truly ask yourself what your true goals are, and think to yourself how hard you are willing to make them happen.  

Plan:

Now that you have evaluated your courses and decided what courses to take, it is now time to plan how you will succeed in the course. How students enact their plans of action differ, but there a few key things students should do. First, you should create a central deadline calendar for all of your courses. This means that you should simply plot all of your assignments on a calendar. You can do this on a physical calendar, a digital calendar, or my personal favorite, on a Stickies program on the computer. The point of this is to stay organized. Once this deadline calendar is made, you can easily spot the busy intersections between assignments. A busy intersection may be “test on Tuesday afternoon, essay on Wednesday morning, and test on Wednesday night.” It is essential to identify the dates that school becomes very busy for you. Most students falter on this step, and end up sacrificing the mark of one of their courses for a higher mark in another course, primarily due to time constraints. This is completely avoidable. Basically, you should plan globally, meaning you should identify and know when tests are, and also where your extracurriculars fall in conjunction with your academic deadlines. There is nothing worse than having two tests soon, and finding out that you have committed 8 hours to a research event, or really anything that takes away time from your essential study time. 

Understand how much assignments are worth. The worth of an assignment should generally determine your overall effort in that assignment relative to other assignments. For example, I know some students who will study a massive amount of hours for a test worth 30%, yet will only spend 4 hours writing an essay worth 35%. This is very careless. Dedicate how much time to the assignment as you think the professor and other students are giving it. Obviously if you have an assignment worth 40% and one worth 15% around the same time, you should most likely dedicate more time to the 40% assignment. This may sound unbearably obvious, and it is, but many students do not really put the amount of effort the assignment deserves; either too much or too little. You should also determine whether the course is primarily lecture/textbook based. If the professor reads the slides verbatim and offers no real advantage to going to class, than do not go to their class. Spend your time on other things, such as reading the textbook or really anything else, rather than sitting in a class with no educational value. Moreover, understanding whether lectures are important in which classes can really boost or lower your GPA. If you listen to a boring 3 hour lecture that you could have covered in 20 minutes of reading the complete slides, than maybe you should devote your energy to other courses or avenues. So, quickly find out from the professor or previous students whether lectures are important to go to/yield higher marks. Whether the lectures are indeed important or not can be ambiguous, so a failsafe way to ensure you will have the information required for the tests is to record the lectures on a voice recording app (or get a recording from a friend) which will allow you to listen to them at any time if you feel the need to do so. 

Additionally, run though a hypothetical year for each course. A quick way to determine how well you have to do on assignments is splitting the course up by essay and test. So, say for example the class is 40% essay based and 60% test based. If I know I generally get between 80-85% on an essay and between 86-90% on tests, than I can quickly calculate what my hypothetical mark would be. I would take the middle of both numbers and determine the mark, which would be 86%. This is a quick way to look at what marks you should try to get and to quickly figure out a buffer zone if things go wrong. For instance, if you get a 75% on your first essay, you know you need to do better than an 88% on the next test. What I do personally to keep track of marking in courses, is to look at all marking in a very pessimistic way. So instead of working towards a total mark out of 100, I work back from 100 and lose marks every assignment I complete. For example, if an assignment is worth 50% and I got a 90 on it, than I lost 5 marks (45/50). I can only lose up to 15.5 marks (to get an 84.5) for the entire year to get a 4.0 in that course. A good goal to have is to only lose 5 marks per every 33 overall marks you receive or to only lose 7 marks after half of the course has been complete. 

Lastly, make sure to not let the stress of assignments bog you down. It is just university, everyone is stressed and at some point you will graduate, and the troubles of getting 4.0s will disappear. On that note, it is very important to take care of your mental health and happiness in university, for both enjoying this time of your life while also achieving your academic goals. If you are running low on motivation to complete assignments, than you should try to cognitively frame assignments in a way that will push you to put more effort into them. For example, I am very competitive, and I quite frankly hate losing to someone (getting a lower mark), so I make it my goal to get the highest mark with the least amount of effort. I cognitively appraise every course as a challenge from the professor and other students to test whether I can do so. Some other cognitive frames you can use to look at assignments are how proud your parents would be, how far you have come intellectually, how the assignment and consequent GPA is a stepping stone for further avenues etc. Just make sure to stay motivated and happy, while keeping a positive attitude towards school.             

Study:  

To study effectively, you have to ask yourself what the assignment expects of you. It is possible to know nearly everything in the course yet to do poorly on a test/assignment, and consequently it is also possible to not know much of the content and to do relatively well. You have to beat the test. What I am trying to say is that to beat the test, you do not need to be the most knowledgeable, unless the test is completely based on knowledge. Like I have said before, there are subjective parts of tests and essays, and maximizing the marks in these areas will go a long way towards your GPA. Now, to effectively beat the test you have to determine what type of test it is. Is it a test of recognition or recall? This boils down to whether it is multiple choice or short answer test. If it is multiple choice then it is a test of recognition; this simply means that you don’t need to understand all of the details or even the exact words for things, as long as you can recognize the right theory or word, you will get the marks. This type of test is generally better for people who have a more broad surface understanding of the course content and is worse for people who have a more specific deep understanding of the content (MC tests do not allow you to demonstrate your sophisticated understanding of content). A short answer test is a test of recall, meaning you will have to recall all of the details, including specific words and terms to get marks. This type of test favors students who have a more in depth understanding of course content over those who have a more broad understanding, generally speaking. Now, these two types of tests are nearly polar opposites, so the way you approach learning the information should be catered to what type of test you are taking. Know the details for recall, recognize them for recognition. 

The next thing to do is to determine what info is truly important to know for the test. This is typically done by looking at the important subjects talked about in lecture or brought up in the readings. Make sure to pay attention to key words, such as when the professor says “this is important”, or grand overarching themes to the course/dual paradigms. This leads us to focus our efforts on the most high yield topics for the test. High yield means topics that will yield the most marks, or in other words, topics that have a higher probability of being on the test. This step is essential, and becomes even more essential for higher education exams such as the MCAT, LSAT, GRE etc. Focus on what will most likely yield the most marks on the test. Do not spend hours memorizing a certain mechanism that you may get half marks on if the question will most likely be out of four (you would lose 2 marks). It would be much worse to get half marks on a topic worth 25 marks (you would lose 12.5 marks). So, with so much information in each course, and so many courses to study, focus on the high yield information the most. 

The process of studying efficiently is important. The first thing you should do is try to review all of the information that will be on the test. Once that is done, you should trim down the information to stuff that is high yield/stuff that you are struggling with. Trimming down information is absolutely essential when it comes to reviewing hours before the test. This can be done by condensing your notes, or highlighting previous notes and only reviewing the highlights. Do not become overconfident before the test. Many students think they know concepts due to completing practice questions, but they may just know how to solve that particular practice question. Expect a few curveballs during the test, and try to come up with hypothetical questions for yourself to answer beforehand. You should never go into a test thinking that it will be very easy or that you do not need to study for it. At the university level it is extremely rare that this attitude is truly warranted, especially at UofT. 

Another thing that students should be aware of is how our brains work. Our brains are not meant to ingest massive amounts of information at once like a computer. It has scientifically been proven that we learn better with spaced repetition. This means that we should study for an hour or two and then take a break, and then study for another hour or two. This also means that studying six hours every week for four weeks in a row for a course, is better than studying 12 hours over two days. The biggest thing that students don’t understand are the cognitive benefits that sleep provides for studying. Basically, it has been clinically proven that we strengthen synaptic connections in our sleep, which is called reconsolidation. I will give you an example of smart studying in an 8 hour period with reconsolidation. Student A studies six hours in a row then takes a one hour break, and then studies for one more hour (seven total hours). Student B studies for four hours in a row, takes a two hour nap, and then studies another two hours (six total hours). Student B’s plan is considerably better for recalling information, despite student A studying for an hour longer. This is due to reconsolidation, and frankly feeling rested, which will probably allow you to have more energy and a stronger ability to focus. So, naps are your friend! If anyone told you that the key to a 4.0 GPA is taking naps, you would probably think they are crazy, but it is true. A brief note on naps: make sure that they are in either 20 minute increments or hour and a half increments. 20 minute naps are to take a break, whereas hour and a half naps are to complete a sleep cycle. This is another topic, but it is basically better to take a nap of 3 hours than 3 hours and 45 minutes, despite sleeping more. Just know that one sleep cycle is roughly an hour and a half, and you do not want to wake up in the middle of a sleep cycle. 

Now, on to a few techniques that you should use to study efficiently. The first are flashcards, which you can make your own, or download a premade deck. I personally recommend the program Anki, which uses an algorithm to show you information you struggle with more in a spaced repetition schedule. Anki is a perennial favorite for medical students, and for good reason, it quite simply works. You can also make a story about certain words or concepts to remember them. Do not underestimate our ability to remember stories, as this technique is used by many Westerners to learn over 2000 Japanese Kanji in a short amount of time. The more creative and personal the story is, usually equates to how easy it is to remember. Don’t be afraid to speak out loud either, sometimes saying things over and over in your head will actually allow you to recall it better during the test. So, if you are struggling with memorizing certain things, talk to yourself about it. Try breaking up your study goals; splitting a large task into smaller tasks is usually how everyone completes something that requires a lot of effort, and studying is no different. Additionally, use other sources of notes, either through Facebook, Oneclass or any other website that has notes on your course. They may have stuff that you missed, or cover it from a different angle or point of view than you did. A psychological study determined that Times New Roman font is the easiest to read, and easiest to recall, so try to read in simple fonts. Also, know that it has been proven that people remember information a lot better when reading from a paper book compared to a digital PDF. Keep that in mind when you have the option of reading from a textbook or E-book.  

Finally, figure out what study environment works best for you. Do you plan to study in groups or on your own? Remember, if you like studying in groups, it generally benefits the least knowledgeable person the most, as they will typically set the pace that you will review information. Do you study better with music or complete silence? Do you study better in a library or your bedroom? Do you study better in the morning or night? Do you work better under pressure or worse? These are all personal preferences that you should know about yourself. Finally, a big issue for students are distractions. 

The biggest distraction to students is probably their phone. The easiest way to keep your phone from distracting you is to turn it over on your desk or keep it out of your sight and on silent. Another big distractor is the internet. A unique and novel way to keep the internet from distracting you is to use a self-control app (either a program or browser extension) that blocks certain websites for an allotted time.  

Also, figure out what nutrition your body best responds to. I will not get into the discussion of Adderall, but you should know how supplements affect you. For example, I may take caffeine before recall tests so I have the energy to write a lot of information, whereas during multiple choice tests I do not. Learn what supplements work best for you. The central theme of studying is to do what works best for you. We are all unique in what works best for us, so discover what works, and stick to it. 

Take Test:  

First, remember to breathe. Deep breaths increase the amount of oxygen to your brain, which allows you to work more efficiently. When the test starts, immediately write stuff you may forget/high yield information on the question sheet. This will take a lot of the cognitive load off from you by relieving your brain from actively rehearsing these topics, and allow you to focus your energy reserves elsewhere.   If it is a multiple choice test, try to think of the answer without looking at the answer choices. This will allow you to not be as easily tricked by the possible answer choices. Try to narrow the answers down, and be weary of unnecessarily long/detailed answers (these are often traps). Now, what I am going to tell you next is not a purely empirical method, but it is a method that can help you get a few extra marks. Try to get in the mind of the TA/professor/test creator. Some professors or TAs have the same pattern of tricking students on multiple choice tests, and if you fall for these traps consistently, then you are in trouble. So, if you recognize that the very detailed answer choices, or very specific answer choices, or choices that sound good to someone who does not know the material are sometimes wrong, then you may want to choose another answer. Finally, know that changing your answers is often better than not changing them. There was a popular myth that stated that intuition was favored over reason on multiple choice tests, this has been thoroughly disproven. Basically, students change answers right to wrong 20% of the time, and they change answers wrong to right 60% of the time. So, carefully reflect on each question, do not just rely on what seems correct.   

If it is a short answer/essay test than it is often better to take the road less travelled. This means that if given a choice of essays or short answer topics to write about, you should choose the question that will get the least amount of responses from other students. Obviously factor in how much knowledge you have pertaining to each topic, but if you have similar knowledge levels on all of the topics, choose the least popular ones. This idea basically comes down to the TAs attitude (boredom/exhaustion) towards marking the same topic over and over again. After marking 40 essays on a certain topic, you see a lot of good essays, and a lot of bad essays on the topic. Due to the large sample size, it is very easy to formulate a general normal distribution of what marks a student should receive in comparison to their classmates (Ex: only ¼ of students get above 80%). If you are writing about topics that are not as popular (6 people write about it) then the TA cannot accurately normalize the sample, so you cannot be artificially brought down by other students. Now, this is not mathematically proven, but after reading 30 of the same essay, I may be happier to see the other topic once in a while. Also, try to explain yourself in novel ways by using examples. Examples are key to maximizing your mark on short answer questions, so use them frequently. Finally, write as much as possible for short answer, especially if there is a small space for an answer. Most people in class will stop in the small space and may not explain themselves fully, so do not be afraid to exceed the space (write on the side of the sheet or below the lines) unless explicitly told not to do so.  

Correct:  

Now that you have received your mark, the least you can do is go over the corrections. I know a lot of students who gloss over why they lost marks, and this continually plagues them for the rest of the course. Make sure to attack your weak points. If you are continually getting lower marks than you want on essays, then attend a writing workshop. Fix your weaknesses and try to make them your strengths. Make sure to internalize why you are losing marks, and make sure to bring up any concerns, especially if you believe you lost more marks than you should have. I, along with many others, have been on the receiving end of marks mysteriously missing from some answers, and then talking to the TA and receiving them afterwards. Communicate with the professor or TA about past assignments and future assignments. Talking to the TA about essays is very important, as incorporating their feedback makes them feel important, which will most likely afford you a higher mark.  Finally, try to build a connection with your TA or professor during the course. It is no coincidence that you may be on the end of a positive arithmetic error by the TA if they like you. A good way to be known by a professor is to message them about something in their field. This shows them that you have initiative and are deeply interested and engaged with the course material. If you are one percent off a 4.0 (which happens more than you think) it is easier to get a TA/professor to boost your mark if they know you or like you. It is as simple as that. Make yourself known in a positive way to the person who is in control of your grades.  

Ultimately, do what works for you. The strategies in this article are not for everyone, so do what works for you. If you are getting 4.0s right now, then continue to follow your strategy. Remember, you are capable of having a 4.0 GPA. You just have to be smart about what courses you take and what strategies will get you that high grade. It will be a learning process. But once discovered, university becomes much easier and much more fulfilling.
