The GPA That Will Double Your Chances of Qualifying for Scholarships

Do you have it?

Let’s cut to the chase — earning that college diploma probably won’t come cheap. The good news is that you can seriously slash your college costs by qualifying for need-based and merit-based financial aid.

Whether or not you’ll qualify for need-based aid, like the Pell grant or Perkins loans, is pretty much outside of your control. Those types of aid are entirely determined by your family’s household size and income. But when it comes to private scholarships and merit-based aid offered by colleges themselves, the real power lies in how you perform on standardized tests and, of course, your GPA. But just how valuable is your GPA when it comes to winning financial aid? I reached out to a couple of my favorite higher education experts for insight.

3.5 is the magic number

It’s difficult to nail down the ideal GPA mostly because it is just one factor out of many that college admissions offices take into account when they look at your application. The average high school student carries a 3.0 GPA these days.

But if that student worked hard and boosted their GPA to a 3.5, their chances of qualifying for private scholarships would double, according to Mark Kantrowitz, a student aid expert and publisher of the scholarship search engine Cappex.com . The U.S. government has been tracking high school student performance in a nationwide study that began in 2009. As of 2013, their findings showed that students who have an unweighted GPA of at least 3.5 (on a 4.0 scale) are twice as likely to win private scholarships as students with a lower GPA, according to Kantrowitz’s analysis of the study.

It’s important to note that very few scholarship applications will actually ask for your GPA. What the study’s findings likely show is that students who have a higher GPA are more likely to be academically successful and, as a result, win scholarships.

Shoot for a 30+ ACT for the best scholarships

Remember, colleges look at a lot more than just your grades when they are reviewing your application. In fact, your ACT score may be an even better indicator of whether or not you’ll qualify for merit-based financial aid, says Joseph Orsolini , a college aid planning expert based in Glen Ellyn, Illinois.

“At many colleges, an ACT score of 30+ is sort of a magic door to maximizing scholarship money,” he tells Teen Vogue . The challenge in quantifying the amount of potential aid money based on test scores alone is that colleges are “all over the map” when it comes to determining merit-based aid, Orsolini says. Each school as their own chest of money to use when it’s time to dole out scholarships, and there is no one-size-fit-all list of requirements for merit-based aid.

The simplest way to find out how far your test scores will carry you is to visit college websites personally or contact their financial aid office. A fair number of colleges actually publish the academic requirements for certain merit-based grants and scholarships right on their websites.

At the University of Alabama, for example, in-state students are eligible for a handful of lucrative scholarships , valued at $3,500 to $10,470 per year. You’ll need at least a 27 ACT score or 1280-1300 (new SAT) score to have a shot at the lowest award available. But students who manage to snag a 30 ACT score are immediately eligible for the most coveted of all academic awards — a full-ride scholarship worth a total of $41,800 over four years.

If you’re lucky, your school will actually have a merit-based scholarship calculator posted online, like these calculators from Pace University in New York and Bradley University in Illinois. Other schools, like Boston University , publish tools that help you figure out the net cost of tuition -- that’s how much your family will have to pay out of pocket after qualifying for student aid and scholarships.

What if your grades aren’t looking too hot?

Not sure if your test scores are up to snuff? If you’re in your early years of high school, you’ve still got time to improve your GPA and test scores before college applications come due your senior year. Meet with your school counselor and come up with a game plan to boost your grades and seek out tutoring sessions that can help you nail the SAT and ACT.

If you’re a high school senior with hopes of enrolling in college with the Fall class of 2017, it’s a little late in the game to try to improve your academic scores. Most college applications are due the winter before graduation with acceptance letters mailed in the spring. But don’t be discouraged. Many students choose to enroll at a two-year college for their freshman and sophomore years and then apply to to transfer to their university of choice after. This is not only a smart way to ease your way into college life but it can save you a huge amount of money on college tuition and fees as well — two-year schools are generally much more affordable than four-year schools.
