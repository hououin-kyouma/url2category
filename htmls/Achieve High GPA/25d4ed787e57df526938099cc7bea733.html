You must recognize the central conceit of GPA vs other measures: it is a simple average, and thus unforgiving.

When people look at your work experience, projects, awards and honors, and whatever other metrics they use to profile you, usually, the impact of each item is weighted by its date; newer items are more relevant.

GPA is a cruel and unforgiving master. In order to make sure that you get that 4.0, there's just a few things to keep in mind.


UNCERTAINTY IS YOUR ENEMY
Hedge, hedge, hedge. You mess up once and that nice 4.0 is gone, likely never to return. Therefore, leave nothing to chance. Make sure you know when every assignment, project, and test is due, and what's covered on each. Make sure you know at least 3 people in your section that you can contact in the event of an emergency. Establish a rapport with your professor and TA early by demonstrating that you're a dedicated, hard working student and then actually talking to them (I rarely talk to professors about the class, usually it's something tangentially related. Nine times out of ten I'm the only one at office hours).

Figure out the best ways to calculate grades. Personally, instead of calculating my average so far, I prefer to calculate my negative. By that, I mean how many points from a 100 I've lost so far. I think this gives me a better picture of where I'm at and how much I can afford to risk.

Figure out the course. Find out its grade distribution. Find out how it assigns grades in the first place -  is it A/B+? Can you get the worst grade on the planet - an A-? How's the professor? What are the research focuses of the professor and TA?


PLAY THE PEOPLE GAME
"I'm a math/physics/computational third world literacy major! I don't need to ask for help!"

Wrong.  You may just need help one day. I got sick and missed class for a week and a half this semester, and took three midterm exams right after I got back. I have a 4.0. While I didn't need help on the material, you can imagine that I didn't pull that off unassisted. I emailed a classmate and asked how our lectures differed from my textbook. I asked a professor what he thought the most important insights he gave to the class were these two lectures. I asked my roommate to turn in my homework (I had run across campus to do the same for him a week before).

Keeping a 4.0 is like running on an inclined treadmill that recently suffered from a Vaseline spill. It helps to have people on the sidelines so that your stumbles don't turn into falls.


PLAY THE AHEAD OF THE GAME... GAME
You should be ahead of the course. Additionally, you should already have a high level overview of the entire course and how you think it fits in so far. This gives you a little breathing room if you need to miss some lectures, allows you to concentrate beyond the basic level of "knowing the material," and will let you stand out and ask better questions in class.


YOUR BODY IS YOUR BIGGEST ASSET
Sleep well. Eat breakfast. Take your medicine. Go to the gym. Make smart decisions, practice smart habits, and be safe.


DON'T BE A BLOWHARD, BLOWHARD
You want to make sure that people want you to succeed. Don't be that guy, because when you fall from grace, instead of support and a possibility of redemption, you're going to get a lot of well-deserved heckling.
