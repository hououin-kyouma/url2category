Should 2.0 GPA be required of student-athletes?

Monday
Jun 10, 2013 at 12:01 AM Jun 10, 2013 at 6:19 PM

Steve Huffman / Times-News


Should students involved in extra-curricular activities be held to a higher academic standard than their classmates?



Members of the Alamance-Burlington Board of Education on Monday discussed the matter, a continuation of several earlier instances where the grades of student-athletes have been scrutinized. The latest discussion came during a monthly work session.



Specifically, board members have discussed raising to 2.0 the minimum GPA for students wishing to participate in sports. There are requirements pertaining to the number of classes that must be passed, but the state doesn�t require a minimum GPA for students to play ball.



Some individual school systems require a 2.0 minimum. The ABSS isn�t one of them.



On Monday, board members were told if they wanted student-athletes to maintain a minimum 2.0 GPA, the same requirement must be made of a student wishing to participate in any extra-curricular activity. Band members and others must also meet the minimum, said Kent Byrd, executive director of secondary education.



�To single out athletes is not logical,� he said.



Assistant Superintendent Charles Monroe has said previously the majority of student-athletes perform well in the classroom, a comment he reiterated Monday. He presented board members information that detailed the GPAs of the system�s 970 student-athletes.



�For the most part, (these students are) taking courses that are very noteworthy and making very good grades,� Monroe said. �These athletes are doing pretty well.�



But Patsy Simpson, the board�s vice chair, quickly perused the list and counted, she said, more than 90 student-athletes with GPAs below 2.0. Simpson has been the board member to most vocally advocate a minimum 2.0 GPA for student-athletes.



She said the board has in recent years pushed for high academic standards for its students. How can the board purport, she asked, to back such a plan when it allows a percentage of its student-athletes to perform poorly in the classroom?



�If we as a board do nothing,� she asked, �how are we supporting our vision plan?�



Board members took no action Monday, but agreed to get more information from administrators at a future work session. Brought up were a number of factors that might play into deciding a minimum GPA for students involved in extra-curricular activities.



�What is the fear of the 2.0?� asked board member Pam Thompson. �What�s keeping us from doing this?�



Board member Jackie Cole said she�d like to hear from principals, athletic directors and coaches before a decision was made.



�I don�t have a problem with 2.0,� she said, �but let�s make sure it�s the right decision for our students.�



Byrd said before such a minimum GPA was enacted, several factors need to be weighed. Would it be enacted, he asked, to encourage students to work harder? Or enacted as a result of a crisis involving a high percentage of student-athletes performing poorly in the classroom?



�I don�t think we�ve seen a crisis,� Byrd said.



Discussion for several minutes pertained to student-athletes being under the misconception they were all but assured of college scholarships and future rewards as professional athletes. That�s the ultimate realization of a very select few, school officials noted.



But would requiring a minimum GPA encourage student-athletes to do better in the classroom, or would it result in their dropping out because they didn�t meet it and sports was their only reason for going to school? Byrd said it was a �sweeping generalization� that every student-athlete with a GPA below 2.0 was convinced a professional sports career awaited him or her.



�The opposite might be true,� Byrd said. �(Sports) may be why they�re in school.�



Board member Kris Moffitt agreed.



�Some of them are going to drop out (if the minimum GPA is put into effect),� she said. �I guarantee it. I�ve seen it.�



Moments later, Moffitt continued: �What�s wrong with a dream if it gets them through high school?�



Superintendent Lillie Cox also cautioned against jumping to a decision before getting all the facts, noting that doing so would be �making assumptions based on numbers on a piece of paper.�



Board member Brad Evans agreed he was worried what might happen to those students who struggle academically and are booted from sports teams because they don�t meet a minimum GPA.



�I�m concerned with the kids who have a 1.5 or 1.75 (GPA)� he said.
