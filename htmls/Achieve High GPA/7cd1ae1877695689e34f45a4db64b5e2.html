We Are Getting Good Grades!

Results fromthe 2012 â 2013 Annual Evaluation

 By Sarah Shah, Director of Evaluation

 

As the Director of Evaluation, my job is to wake up every morning and think about whether or not we are making an impact. Fortunately, findings from our external evaluator at Stanford University for 2012-2013 confirm both that there is a dire need for our services and that our advisers are successfully helping more and more low-income, first-generation students enroll in college.

Using college enrollment data from the National Student Clearinghouse (source for education verification and student educational outcomes research), the team at Stanford found that a schoolâs college enrollment rate increases on average by three percentage points three years after having a College Advising Corps adviser. Many schools saw college enrollment increase by more than 10 percentage points. This means that thousands of low-income, first-generation students have enrolled in college who likely wouldnât have without our advisers.

An even more exciting finding is that of the students we serve who enroll in college, 74% are persisting through to their second year. This average is on par with the 2012 national average of 72% (ACT data), which is extraordinary given that so many of our students are low-income and first-generation.

Data from the randomized controlled trial (RCT) in Texas also shows that our advisers are doing a good job of targeting students. Students who meet with an adviser are more likely to be first-generation students, eligible to receive a free or reduced price lunch, and more concerned about financing college.

According to our student tracker data, 83% of all seniors in our schools met with an adviser. And the work is clearly paying off. Advisers assisted 39% of all seniors with their FAFSA and 45% of all seniors with their college applications.

According to student survey data for the class of 2013, seniors who met with an adviser were:

  • 23% more likely to apply to a college/university, 16% more likely to apply to 3+ institutions, 6% more likely to apply to 6 or more institutions
  • 24% more likely to apply to a first choice college
  • 23% more likely to submit a FAFSA
  • 18% more likely to have heard of Pell Grant
  • 18% more likely to take the ACT, and
  • 14% more likely to take the SAT 1

All of these data points highlight just how valuable it is for underrepresented students to work with an adviser.

Of course, we still have work cut out for us. Even though we are effectively increasing college enrollment, the average college enrollment rate at our schools still hovers at around 48%, highlighting the need for our continued services. A case study analysis in Missouri also revealed that most schools could benefit from more parental involvement.

Nonetheless, it excites me to know that what we are doing truly matters. Our advisers are clearly making an impact and every year, theyâre helping more and more students attain the college education they deserve.
