Planning a study timetable

 

 

Follow these steps to plan your study timetable:

 

1          Fill in sleeping and eating times.  Be regular about this.  The lack of a routine schedule for eating and sleeping is sometimes used as an excuse for not studying.  For you to be in control you need to train your body to keep efficient eating and sleeping habits.  Decide what time you will get up, what time you will eat your meals, what time you will go to bed (at least on week days).  This helps to reinforce the idea that study is a “job”.

 

 

2          Build in your regular commitments.  Make a list of these first – classes, work, travel time, family, sport, church, helping at home.  Don’t under-estimate the amount of time and effort you spend on these.

 

 

3          Build in family and friends time.  This way you can ensure minimum disruption to your personal life with maximum time to study.  This also allows you to discuss your commitments with your family and friends.  Understanding your study needs will be a vital aspect in their support, and remember you will need a consistent system of encouragement and support.

 

 

4          Include relaxation and exercise time.  Make exercise a regular part of your timetable.  This will help to develop good positive attitudes.  It will also help to prevent stress.

 

 

5          What blocks of time are left for study?  You should plan to find 3 to 4 hours per day for homework and study.  Be realistic and honest with yourself.  If you have a favourite television program, include it in your timetable.  You are the one who has to keep to your timetable, so don’t plan study at impossible times.

 

 

6          Evaluate your priorities.  Check your study hours – if you do not have enough, you will have to find them somewhere.  You are the one in charge.  You have to arrange the situation so that you maintain your sense of purpose and your commitment to achieve your goals.

 

 

7          Arrange your ‘study’ to increase the effectiveness of your learning.  You can start doing the hard work of preparing for exams right from the first week of term if you take advantage of the idea that 4 or 5 practices or active repetitions of information will place it in your long term memory.  Each day after school, schedule a review session (half an hour should be enough).

Schedule a weekly review session for each subject.  Colour code your subjects to organize your study and make your timetable easy to read.

 

 

8          Are you a morning or evening person?  What time of day are you most alert?

Plan your study to fit in with your mental and physical peaks.  If you are tired and have to study, start with an easier task eg writing out a bibliography or putting notes in order.  You might find that the sense of achievement gained from completing these tasks can build up your motivation to go on to the harder tasks.

 

 

9          Work out the demands of assignments.  Plan your weekly timetable to accommodate your assignments, even if 2 or 3 are due in the same week.

 

 

10         Include flexibility.  You will need some spare study time to allow for any unforeseen interruptions to your usual routine.

 

 

11         Plan each study session and set realistic and achievable goals.  Plan each session so that specific tasks can be completed eg summarise 3 pages, read 1 chapter.  Start the planned task as soon as you sit down.  Try the ‘half hour goal’ which includes that important review time.  What did I just read?  What was the main idea? 

 

 

12         Keep in mind your learning style (visual, auditory, kinesthetic).   Your learning style will affect the way you spend your study time.  Plan around your strengths, weaknesses and the demands placed on you by family, friends, commitments etc.

 

 

13         Include a weekly planning session.  Spend some scheduled time on Saturday or Sunday planning your timetable for the coming week, taking into account the specific demands of that week, both social and academic.

 

 

14         If your timetable is not working, if you are not following it – change it!  If you find that you cannot get up, or go to sleep, or study at the times planned, change the timetable so that it will work.  In fact, revise and update it regularly (each term).

 

 

15         Discuss your timetable with your family and friends.  Let them know when your study and free times are.  When people know that you are studying, they might let you get on with it.

 

 

16         Only study for as long as you are effective.  If you study for 3 hours but can only remember one hour’s worth, you have wasted 2 hours.  Study only for as long as you are fully productive.  Take a short break after half an hour.  Two to five minutes should be enough – do some relaxation exercises, walk around the room, have a drink of water.  The ‘half hour goal’ helps with this too.

 

 

17         Build ‘rewards’ into your timetable if you have trouble concentrating.  Rewards are important incentives for increasing motivation, so use them to help you keep to task.  When you decide what tasks you want to complete in each session, decide on the reward as well.  For example, an hour’s relaxation might be the reward for making notes on a chapter; a movie might be the reward for completing an assignment.  This works the other way around as well.  If the task is not satisfactorily completed, then you should not reward yourself.
