Stay out of the uni bar and off the nintendo system in the library. Stay away from the opposite sex (unless their course notes are better than yours) (That's what I told my kids...)

Turn up to all of your lectures and Tutes. Do your reading etc before you go.

Do your Homework.

Don't leave assignments etc until the last minute.

Here is how I always get a 2 week extension on long term assignments. If the due date is March 30, I write it in my diary as March 15. Then if I haven't finished by then, I have an automatic 2 week extension.

Study as you go along. By the end of each week, have all the work and study for that topic done and reduced to one or two pages of notes. (A Godsend at exam prep time)

Give every assessment 110%, even if it is only worth 5%

Use your lecturers FREE consultation times. You will likely be the only one there, so it is free individual tuition from the dude who writes the exams. Even if you think you are on top of the topic, go anyway and have him peruse your condensed notes to comment if you have covered all the essentials.

Very early in my business degree, the head of the department told us – Every one of us are experts in the field of business. Not one of us is a trained teacher, so we don't know how to teach. However, we have every piece of information that you need for your course, your job is to make sure you get it out of us.

I think the best advice I can give here is to be aware that university is a system. In order to get good marks you must navigate the system successfully. Learning and understanding the material well does not automatically translate into good marks. At the same time, you can often get good marks without having a solid grasp of all the learning materials. The really important thing to getting good marks is to identify what is important to how you are being assessed.

For assignments a marking sheet will almost always be supplied with the assignment brief. It is a table showing precisely what aspects will attract marks, and how those marks will be awarded. Spend time getting a really good understanding of how the assignment brief and marking criteria go together. Do not spend heaps of time on something that isn't being marked. Do spend as much time as possible on perfecting the things that attract big marks. Have your assignment ready at least a day or two before it is due. Proof read it. Go through the criteria sheet and mark off everything you've covered. When you find something you haven't covered, or haven't covered well, go back and fix it.

Also, a really, really, really important point that most first year students miss – there can be ambiguities in assignment briefs – it is your responsibility, and in your best interests, to verify with the appropriate member of the teaching team what the actual requirement is.

For exams it can be trickier to do well but there is usually something available to help you prepare. If you are an on-campus student attend every lecture – lecturers will often give out exam hints as you go through the semester – compile notes on these hints somewhere. Definitely be at the last couple of lectures as many lecturers will give you a very comprehensive outline of which things to study and which things not to worry about. But, you must be there to get the information – fellow students who are putting in the hard yards when you're not are unlikely to share this info and lecturers rarely make it available through any other means.

If past exams are made available make use of them. But, don't just work through them blindly. Start by analysing them. If you have exams from say the last 3-5 years you can check the cover page to see if it has always been the same teacher teaching the subject and the same time limit as you will have on your exam. If the exams are for the same lecturer and the same time limit you have then start comparing the content.

Some lecturers will run the same questions every year just changing the variables. If this is the case then practice those questions over and over and you should be able to do well. If that is not the case look for other patterns. Are half of the questions recycled? If some of the questions appear every year then make sure you can do those well and quickly. You may find that although the questions are different they focus on the same content so you need to learn concepts really well rather than be able to respond to specific questions. It is rare that you need to know every single concept, formula, method of calculation etc to do well in an exam. Don't waste time learning things that won't be tested.

In summary: Make sure to put time aside at the beginning of your study to work out how best to spend your time. You don't have much time to study – endeavour to spend it as efficiently as possible.

- Do the prescribed readings! Preferably before that weeks class. Take notes while you read, then read through the lecture slides, then write summary notes of the weeks topic, matching your reading notes against your notes from lectures.

- Whenever your lecturer mentions something commonly comes up on exams, or often trips students up in exams, highlight it and make sure you know it.

- Start reviewing your summary notes around 4-5 weeks before exams, to note concepts that are developed across multiple topics, links between topics etc. Draw flowcharts, mind maps, something visual, anything to help you remember it.

- If you need to do calculations, practise them by doing the prescribed tute questions. Don't look at the answers until you have finished and need to check. If you struggle with a question, make sure you work through the answer either through the worked response, with a friend or with the tutor. Make sure you understand why you're making the calculations at each step, not just rattling them off.

- Make friends with the best students in the class, and try to work collaboratively. Also good for group assignments.

- If you have an open book exam, make life as easy as possible for yourself. It's not an excuse to not know what you're doing, it's a reason to provide yourself with the best tools you can in an exam. Put tabs on your books for important pages, if you can take notes in, put in the effort to make them clear, easy to navigate, and easy to check. Dot points! Flow charts! Colourful tabs! Hit Officeworks and pimp your exam notes out – and then practise questions using those notes.

- Practice exams – use them! If the lecturer goes through a past exam in the last week, do it beforehand so you know what they're talking about, and which questions to ask. If past exam questions show up in the tutorials, know them inside out. Then find as many past exams as you can and look for common trends, then hammer those questions until you can do them in your sleep.

- Plan your exam time carefully. Try to hit all questions at around 80% of your ability, then hopefully you'll have time at the end to go back and refine them. Better to lose a mark or two on each question than answer half perfectly and run out of time.

- Treat assignments seriously – the less marks you lose here, the more you can relax come exam time. It's easier to get high marks in assignments than rely on the exam. Plan your time, ask questions, check things you aren't sure about with your tutor. If you're still not sure, email the lecturer – its not uncommon for tutors to say one thing and lecturers another, rely on the person who will actually be marking your work.

And have fun! Uni is the best time of your life. It is possible to do all the above and have an amazing time. And if you can do both, you're setting yourself up nicely for future job applications too.
