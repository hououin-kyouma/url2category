A DIY guide to developing good study habits

September 2, 2014/0 Comments/in CB Blog, College life, College success tips, Learning resources, Study tips, Test prep /by Alana Joli Abbott

Welcome back to campus! You’ve probably already had your first week or two of classes by now, and whether you’re starting your freshman year or you’re a graduating senior, now is the time to start developing good study habits for your semester.

A group of Harvard students study together in a dorm dining hall. (Credit: Tbuckley89)

While plenty of campuses have tutoring and writing centers to help you organize your schedules and study plans, you can also follow some do it yourself tips. Think it’s better to start with a healthy GPA than to have to improve it at midterms? Then follow this DIY guide to getting the semester off on the right track.

Start to study now

Guest blogger Nicole contributed “A DIY Guide to Developing Great Study Habits” to Tech & Science on November 8, 2012. Her first piece of advice? Don’t wait to get started. “The earlier you begin, the more time you will have to develop these habits,” she explained. Her other tips included:

  • Take notes. This may seem like a no-brainer, but make sure you’re writing things down that you need to remember, whether it’s in class or while reading your textbook. Don’t worry about rewriting things that are already in your textbook, though; use a highlighter to mark the important bits you’ll need to find later.
  • Set a schedule. College students often have jobs and extracurricular activities that need to be scheduled around. While college is a great time for socializing, don’t forget to schedule time for study. If you don’t set aside blocks of time, Nicole wrote, “you will never get around to it.”
  • Find a quiet place. Your dorm room may not be the best place for studying. A quiet nook in the library may be better. And you may find that you do better by alternating your study locations so your brain doesn’t get stuck in a rut.
  • Take breaks. Stand up, stretch, get a drink of water, and let your brain take in what you’ve been studying. It’s easier to find time to take breaks if you haven’t waited until the last few days before the test to start studying. One pro-tip: avoid Facebook breaks. They seem like a good idea, but using Facebook (like Twitter or Pinterest or any other social network) can quickly go from a quick break to a much longer time-waster.
  • Join a group—maybe. Some students do well in study groups, while others find that they spend more time socializing than studying. If you can gather a group of likeminded peers, especially including some who have a good understanding of the material, you can get a lot of work done. If you find yourself doing too much gabbing, though, consider a different group or solo study time.

Take a class, or ask your prof

Some universities, including Kansas State University, actually offer courses on developing good study habits. According to one educator from the university, referenced by Keener A. Tippin II in “Developing strong study skills involves more than just hitting the books” for K-State Perspectives, “only about 20 percent of college freshmen have developed good study habits before entering college.” In the Kansas State class, geared toward college students in their freshman year, students learn how to efficiently take notes and develop time management strategies—such as using the hour between classes to study the subject the student just came from.

Have questions about a course? Do not be afraid to ask your prof. In a 2012 survey conducted by Questia, reported in “College study habits: Are students and instructors on the same page?” the Questia Staff found that both students and profs agreed about using the prof as the number one resource for college help.

Improving that GPA

What if you started the year dragging last year’s grades behind you? You may know the general guideline that you should study two hours for every hour you’re in class, but as Lorraine Savage wrote on Questia‘s Citations blog April 15, 2013, “How to study after midterm exams: Reassess your study habits,” “to improve a grade point average (GPA), a whole lotta studying needs to happen.” Savage reported that one college professor found a 40 hour per week increase in studying was necessary to increase your GPA by a full letter.

Can’t fit an extra 40 hours per week into your schedule? Find ways to make more efficient use of your time. Stay away from social networking sites, make the most of those hours between classes, and avoid procrastination. Instead, break up your projects and papers into small, manageable chunks to avoid feeling overwhelmed. Start papers the day they’re assigned to avoid last-minute stress.

If you’re a graduating senior, what advice do you have for forming good study habits during the freshman year?
