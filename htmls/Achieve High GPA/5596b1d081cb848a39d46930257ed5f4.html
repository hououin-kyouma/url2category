10 Tips to Getting Good Grades in College

Wow! Week 3 over already! It seems like just yesterday we got to school! Midterms are coming up in about 2 weeks, and everyone is beginning to feel the pressure! However, college isn't always as scary as it seems, especially with the right mindset and resources. Here are some college success tips for getting good grades:

1. Take charge. College isn't high school. There's no teacher or parent to remind you every day of what you need to do. What grades you get will depend on what you yourself do. Manage yourself wisely. 

2. Don't settle. To get good grades in college, it's very important that you pick the right courses. Pick classes that you think you can do. And be sure to pick the right level in required courses such as math, English comp, sciences, and languages (in some colleges, there are five courses all bearing the name "college math"). Pick your courses one by one, paying careful attention that some fulfill distribution requirements, some count to a possible major, some satisfy some interest of yours, and at least one is something that somehow "sounds interesting." You'll do better if you've made the right choices and are doing things you enjoy.

3. Don't overload. Some students think it's good to take as many hours as the college allows. It isn't. Take four or at the most five courses each semester. And, unless you came in with lots of credits or are really really ambitious, don't take more than two majors. You can easily kill your GPA by taking classes you aren't fully committed to. 

4. Make a plan. Part of getting good grades is balancing off the various things you have to do, week by week. So get a calendar—Google calendar has worked wonders for me—and enter in all your classes, exams, and papers, and professors' office hours (more on that later). 

5. Get to class. Most students have a cutting budget: the number of lectures they can miss in each course and still do well. But in a quarter system, you have limited in-class time. Miss 5 classes and that's about 25% of the quarter gone!

6. Be a notetaker. In many intro courses, the professor's lectures form the major part of the material tested on the midterm and final. Write down everything the professor says. Structure is nice, but content is even better. There are lots of note-taking systems, but (Cornell Note-Taking System, Mind Mapping, or the "five R's of good note taking"). just get it all down—you can always fix it up later. 

7. "Hook up" with the prof. The most underused resource at college—and the one most likely to benefit your grade—is the office hour, either in person or electronic. This is really the only time that you can get one-on-one help from a prof or TA. When you miss something in class or don't understand something, meeting one-on-one to figure it out can be crucial. 

8. Study like you mean it. At college, you're expected to prepare an hour or two (sometimes more) for each class meeting. This means lots of time management. Make sure to find the right studying environments (no devices, no social networking, no friends, no eating—just your mind up against the work. We know this can be painful but it's worth it at the end. 

9. Believe in No. 1. A large part of good grades is good attitude: believing—really believing—that you can do it (and then doing it). Do not let myths—"you're just not that good a student," "you have trouble in math and science," "your brother is the smart one"—undermine your confidence. Your college took you because they thought you could do well. Prove them right.

10. Don't be a Wiki-potamus. If your course has a research paper, make sure you use proper, scholarly materials. Look to the assignment sheet and/or instructions in lecture or section to see what the prof is expecting. Above all, forget about Wikipedia and blind Google searches: These typically do not yield the sort of content that is right for a college paper.

 

Good luck everyone!
