Filovirus
U of T should be fine. Grad programs look at your GPA from the last one or two years, mostly (the ones I applied to, also in the Faculty of Medicine, looked at my 3rd and 4th years and didn't really care about the rest). If you are intelligent and determined, you should have no problem getting that GPA in 3rd and 4th years, where class averages are usually B (3.0) to B+ (3.3). Just double check with the program you are interested in though.

I think the advantage of going to U of T is that you will get the most research opportunity. After all, U of T ranks the highest in Canada in terms of research output, the quality of research probably depends on the area (TBH I feel U of T is quantity over quality, in terms of research). This is especially important for grad school at U of T (U of T really cares about research for postgraduate programs), where a heavy emphasis is placed on your undergraduate research experience. So aim for an NSERC USRA or something during the summer after your 3rd or 2nd year (getting it before your 3rd year is almost impossible, so look for other opportunities after your 1st and 2nd year). To my experience, summer is the best time to do research, doing it during school will likely affect your GPA.

Other than GPA and research experience, grad schools also tend to look at your choice of courses. Try to take more courses relevant to your program, and take courses whose level is suitable for your year (e.g. take 400-level courses in 4th year). I was criticized for taking too many 200- and 300-level courses in 4th year when I talked to the graduate admission person for the program I applied to (they still gave me the offer in the end, so I guess it wasn't too terribly important).

And of course, as an U of T undergrad, you have great access to researchers who can supervise you for the U of T program you are interested in (although some programs will claim that securing a supervisor will not affect the admission decision, I think it does, since they make a note of it in your files when you tell them). I've heard a number of stories where U of T undergrads went on to grad programs, supervised by the same researcher who supervised them for undergrad research.

P.S. You should always have a backup plan/program.
