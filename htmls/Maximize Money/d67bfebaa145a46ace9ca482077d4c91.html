How To Get Out Of Debt

Home   /   Blog   /   Debt   /   Getting Out of Debt   /   How To Get Out Of Debt

Below Read More
Prev Post
Next Post
by Lindsey Burgess

Because you know you should, you just didn't know where to start—until today.

I’m pretty sure I don’t need to make a case for the value and/or importance of getting out of debt.

Debt is the worst. It holds you back. It messes with your mind. It stresses you out in ways that you are conscious of, and some that you are not. It hinders your ability to make good decisions and your future in general. Ugh. Debt.

Now, debt. What are you going to do about it? Pay it off! How are you going to do it? By budgeting! (I was chanting that like a cheer, just in case you want to go back and re-read it properly).

You can pay off your debt. This is kind of our thing. This is what we do. Here’s how:

Step 1: Commit To Budgeting

No, like, really commit. Forget every negative connotation you’ve ever associated with budgeting. Here is the honest truth: budgeting is your ticket to freedom.

Budgeting is a powerful tool to help you spend your money on the things that are most important to you. (Spending? See! Fun!) It also happens to be key to getting out of debt.

Don’t consider it, or sort of, maybe, explore the idea of it. Decide that this will be the year you give budgeting everything you’ve got.

Begin to think about giving every single dollar a job. Your money is finite, so a dollar spent here, means it cannot be spent elsewhere. What is most important to you? Sit down with the money you have right now, and decide what every dollar needs to do before you get paid again. And just like that, you are budgeting.

Step 2: Budget for Immediate Obligations

First up (whether or not you like it), are your immediate obligations. You’ve got to pay rent and keep the lights on and all that good stuff.

So, even with a renewed focus and energy toward paying down your debt, before you can do anything, you have to have money for your immediate, non-negotiable obligations.

Step 3: Budget for your True Expenses

Now, before you start throwing money at your debt, you have to make sure that you don’t go into more debt. The way you do that is to consider and budget for your true expenses.

True expenses are larger, less frequent expenses, that if you don’t put money aside for every month when they do come up (and you know they will!), you will a) feel stressed, b) feel frustrated that these things always sneak up on you, and c) probably reach for a credit card. This is the cycle we need to break.

There are expenses you can expect like clockwork—Christmas, insurance premiums, vacations—and ones that you can expect, even if you don’t know exactly when they will happen or how much they will cost you, like car repairs, medical expenses, gifts, essentially, life.

Instead of always being “surprised” by these things, people who are debt-free, anticipate expenses for the future, and treat these expenses like monthly bills. Every month you should be putting a little bit away for Christmas, your insurance premiums, car repairs, house repairs, a new laptop fund if it is on it’s last legs, or whatever it is for you.

By budgeting for the future, you will not go deeper into debt. That is no small victory…

Step 4:  Create A Debt Pay-Down Plan

Once your immediate obligations and true expenses are budgeted for, then you can take a look at what’s left and create a debt plan. This will be your starting point. Here are a couple best practices to speed things up:

Focus on your smallest debt first. Make a list of all your debts from smallest to largest. Start with the smallest debt and determine how much a month you can budget toward this debt. Set up a debt pay-down goal to keep you honest, motivated, and on track. Choose a payoff date—one that is maybe even a little bit of a stretch—and dwell on the end in sight! That first success (even if it is small), will feel great and will free up cash to put toward your next debt target.

For all other debts, budget for the minimum payment. You need this information front of mind. You’ve probably avoided thinking about your debt in the past, but this is the new debt-crushing you. You aren’t afraid to look at the balances, and you know the minimums by heart. You will not get behind. You will not avoid the reality of it. You are focused and making progress. (And I for one, am very proud of you!)

Attack the next smallest debt with fervor. Now you’ll be paying minimum payment plus all the money that was going to the last debt—and maybe even more if you’re really getting the hang of this budgeting thing! Repeat this process and you will pay off your debt. You will never regret it.

You Can Do This!

Commit to budgeting, get organized and get ready to change your life! Get pumped up to pay down your debt! We’ll be back in a few days with some ideas about how to get aggressive and really speed things up (but we’ll give you a second to get pumped up first!).
