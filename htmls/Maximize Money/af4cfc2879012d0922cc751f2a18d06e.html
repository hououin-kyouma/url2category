9 Tips for Getting Out of Debt

Author Lauren Greutman found her way out of $40,000 in debt. Here’s how to go from compulsive spender to compulsive saver.

By
Emily Bary
Updated June 21, 2017 3:26 p.m. ET
  • Text Size
  • Regular
  • Medium
  • Large
    •
Photo: Pixabay

If you’re buried in debt, you don’t need someone to tell you to get out. You know what you have to do, but often the process of getting started is difficult, especially when you’re in deep enough that it doesn’t seem like a few more dollars here or there can make any difference.

Eventually you’ll hit that point when you’re ready to turn things around, whether it’s because your debt is keeping you from your dreams or because you find that it’s starting to hurt the people you love. One recent book, The Recovering Spender, offers tips from frugal-living expert Lauren Greutman, who went from being $40,000 in debt to being a model saver.

We compiled some of her best lessons to help compulsive spenders become compulsive savers.

• Stop eating out. Greutman realized she and her husband were spending $1,000 on food every month. They quickly slashed that to $200, by cutting out restaurants and being more selective about grocery choices.

• Map out your meals in advance. One relatively simple way that Greutman saved big on groceries was by planning out a week of meals every time she went grocery shopping. Check out those supermarket flyers that highlight the best discounts. Then, come up with a bunch of meals from those foods. By planning ahead, you won’t have excess food and you can take full advantage of sales on staples, like meat and bread.

• Go in on a cellphone plan with friends or relatives. You can double your data limit without doubling your cost. So if you need 1 GB, go in on a 2GB plan with a friend or a 4GB plan with three friends. Each step up costs less on a per-gigabyte basis so the split works out favorably for everyone.

• Plan things down to the dollar. If you have trouble cutting your spending, having a very specific budget can keep you honest. A budget that’s too flexible may entice you to make reckless purchases. Greutman’s initial budget left her with just a $7 margin for error. That motivated her to be disciplined about things like electricity and gas, or else she’d have to cut from other essential categories, like food.

• Pace yourself. If you use up all the money in your “eating out” category on the first of the month, you’re going to be disappointed for the next 30 days. Try breaking down the budget into smaller pieces, like weekly chunks, so you don’t leave yourself too strapped at the end of the month.

• Consider prepaid cards. Put your online-shopping money on a prepaid card. You won’t be able to go over that budget because the funds will run out, even if your shopping interest doesn’t.

• Sell your stuff. The clutter in your apartment isn’t doing you any good, and you could be turning it into cash. Look for designer clothing items you don’t really need. They sell well on eBay , Greutman says. Plus you can probably make $200 by selling books you’ve already read.

• Treat budgeting as a challenge. Instead of making impulse buys, figure out how you can get all your basic necessities for a low price. Getting your monthly budget lower than the prior month’s can turn into a game, as can doing real research to find the best deals. Being frugal doesn’t mean you shouldn’t buy new shoes when your kid needs them. It’s just that you shouldn’t jump at the first pair you see.

• Stay accountable. All these rules are great, but you might be tempted to cheat. Find a friend to keep you accountable and in check.
