College not all about making more money: Student Voices

Published 6:56 a.m. ET May 24, 2017 | Updated 7:18 a.m. ET May 24, 2017

Micaila D’Angelico11th gradeLacey Township High SchoolTeacher: Anita Soto (Photo: Courtesy Micaila D'Angelico)

36 CONNECT TWEET LINKEDIN COMMENT EMAIL MORE

Editor’s note: This is the May first-place winner in the grade 9-12 category of the Asbury Park Press Student Voices essay contest. Micalia D’Angelico of Lacey Township High School views college as a journey to self-discovery rather than simply a way to make more money.

For a high school student who still has to ask if I may use the bathroom, the thought of college is a daunting one. As important as college may be in the eyes of my family and my future employers, sometimes I must take a contemplative step back and determine what going to college and getting a four-year degree precisely means to me.

Currently, my dismal perception of college is an assembly line that sucks in money and in turn produces a plethora of conformist automatons whose only purpose is to work and make money. In the glory days, college was meant to create bright, young individuals who go off to become honorable leaders in society after graduation; now it seems one of the main reasons students attend college is because they only care about getting a good job and making money, a vicious cycle of greed and self-preservation.

Although getting a four-year college degree is by no means a waste of time, it all depends on why you want to obtain a higher education. One must ask themselves if they are going to college for shallow purposes or if they are going to find a true calling and achieve greatness through the power of learning.

For my future, college is immensely important, not just because of my future profession but because of the experiences. At this age, I have never experienced the world beyond the walls of my jejune diversity-lacking town. Learning new things and meeting new people unlocks an immeasurable amount of opportunities. I long to go to college and obtain at least a four-year degree, not only to pursue my future career endeavors but to begin an invigorating journey of self-discovery and carving out my path to genuine happiness and fulfillment.

Micalia D'Angelico
