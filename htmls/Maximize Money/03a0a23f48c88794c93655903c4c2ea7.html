How to get out of debt by retirement

  • Share
  • Share via Email
  • Share on Google Plus
  • Post on facebook wall
  • Share on twitter
  • Post to Linkedin
  • Share on Reddit
by Sam Henderson

It can be worth holding the right properties (even with debt), says Sam Henderson who answers your questions on super.

Q: I am in my early fifties and have nearly paid off my principle place of residence, but I have some investment properties that have debt associated with them. Sam, you often mention you should retire with no debt. Does this mean no debt on the family home (personal loans etc), or no debt overall? Should my plan be to try and remove the debt on the investment properties as much as possible before retirement? I would like to keep them as long as possible to help fund our retirement income. Currently they are either close to positively geared or positively geared. Simon

A: Simon, don't get me wrong, I'm a fan of property but just not debt in retirement, and specifically on the family home. Often debt makes sense during your working life because the difference between the rent received from an investment property and the interest, and other costs payable, is tax-deductible.

In retirement, you generally don't have to pay tax as most people don't have any material taxable income so therefore the tax deductibility of an investment property is a moot point. That said, you mention that you are close to or in a cash flow positive scenario, so the properties will not cost you anything to hold. This is an important point.

So if the properties don't cost anything to hold and they continue to go up in value over time (as property as a general asset class has), I'd certainly be keen to hold the properties. Depending upon your overall cash flow situation, I may be far less fussed over your investment debt.

Residential property is a strange beast, though. Many investors live on low after-cost cash flows because many properties only return 1-2 per cent net even if they're debt-free. The capital gain more lately has offset that low cash flow. But if you can afford to keep your property for the long term, then I'd have no issue with it so long as you can derive the income you need in retirement and you're not constricting lifestyle to own a sub-standard property with debt, paying interest rates that may rise and extinguish potential profits.

Q: I was just made redundant and decided to retire. I turn 59 this month. I need to know whether I can put some of my payout into my accumulation fund rather than the banks. And is there any limit on how much I can put into the fund? Carl

A: Carl, redundancies can be somewhat problematic and personal advice is essential. But given the right circumstances such as close proximity to retirement and many years of service, redundancy can be a real boon for retirees. For bona fide redundancies, the first $10,155 plus $5,078 for reach year of service will be tax free in the 2017-18 financial year.

Up to $300,000 of this can then be contributed to super as an after-tax (or non-concessional) contribution by using the three-year bring-forward rule (previously up to $540,000 in past years). The remaining amounts payable to you above the tax-free component will be taxable and either the employment termination payment (ETP) cap (most likely as it sounds as though yours is a bona fide redundancy) or the whole of income cap will apply, depending on the nature of your termination.

Long service leave and annual leave are also payable and taxable at 32 per cent (unless you have pre August 1978 accumulations, of which 5 per cent of the total is calculated at your normal marginal tax rates). , These may not be salary-sacrificed but you can contribute your after-tax amounts to super as a non-concessional contribution.

Again, there's more to know than what I can fit into this limited space, so advice is essential and the earlier the better. Timing is important and knowing your options is paramount.

Q: Sam, I am 72 and have $2.8 million in in my SMSF, all of which was in pension mode before June 30, 2017. I have rolled back $1.2 million into accumulation phase. I will not be making any further contributions. 1. Will my pension portion retain its tax-free percentages of July 1, 2017 if I continue to withdraw appropriate pension percentage levels? 2. Will my accumulation portion have its tax-free percentages diluted over time by the net after-tax earnings on its balance? 3. Do the tax-free/taxable components only come into consideration on my death? 4. Are withdrawals of any sum from my accumulation portion tax-free given my age? Kay

Q: Kay, the answer to question 1 is yes. Amounts remaining in pension phase, assuming you continue to draw your minimum (5 per cent), won't be subject to tax on capital gains and earnings, and withdrawals for pension payments will be tax-free. Question 2: earnings will go towards your taxable component in your accumulation account. Question 3: yes, in your case that is correct. Interdependent beneficiaries will pay 15 per cent tax (plus surcharges) on the taxable portion of your super fund. Dependents will not pay tax, for example a spouse will not pay tax nor a dependent adult child who may live with you. Question 4: As you're over 60, withdrawals from super are all tax-free.

These answers must be taken as general advice only. Financial planner Sam Henderson is CEO of Henderson Maxwell.
