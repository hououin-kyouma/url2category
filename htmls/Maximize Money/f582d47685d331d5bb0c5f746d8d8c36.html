Consumer credit: the best way to borrow money and pay off debt

CHRISTINE LONG

Last updated 16:10, December 7 2016

Catherine Robson of Affinity Private shares the three fundamentals of credit card management in an increasingly cashless society.

Four years ago Vicki Lanini had a credit card debt of A$14,000 (NZ$14,600). Now she's a home owner and she has just spent five weeks in Bali without any help from a credit card.

Her secret? Setting a goal to get rid of her debt in six months and then smashing it in five.

"I ended up paying it off earlier because I kind of became obsessed with it," the 27-year-old personal trainer and nutritionist says.

Wayne Taylor

For personal trainer Vicki Lanini, beating credit card debt made her financially fit.

First, Lanini shifted her debt to a zero per cent balance transfer card. "The interest was what was really killing me."

Then she worked out how much she would need to pay each week to be debt-free in six months and "knuckled down". That meant cooking at home and ditching her habit of three coffees a day.

But Lanini didn't want to erase all the fun from her life. So she changed the way she socialised, inviting friends over for barbecues instead of going out for dinner or drinks.

"Or I would catch up with friends and go for a walk," she says. "So it actually built my connections with my friends."

Another tactic: when the new card arrived with a A$15,000 limit (NZ$15,650), Lanini gave it to her Mum for safe-keeping and, as soon as it was paid off, she cancelled it.

That's when Lanini set a new goal of becoming a home owner. "I had all this spare money so I had a choice: I could go back to living how I used to or I could save the money and put it towards something awesome," she says.

Australian Securities and Investments Commission figures show Australians owe about A$32 billion (NZ$33 billion) on credit cards, with the average cardholder paying about A$700 (NZ$730) in interest a year.

So if you're carrying a balance on your credit card from month to month, what can you do about it?

Ross Marais, financial planner, More Time Financial, says setting a goal of when you want to be debt-free and finding someone to hold you accountable is a good start.

Identifying how you got into debt in the first place is also key if you're going to avoid repeating the pattern.

"Sometimes it's not your fault…but for a lot of people it's the result of living above their means, trying to keep up appearances and lack of financial education," Marais says.

Having that insight can help when it comes to choosing a strategy for getting out of debt.

Balance transfer deals

As Lanini's story demonstrates, getting out of debt via a balance transfer takes focus.

Bessie Hassan, money expert, Finder.com.au, says: "To make this strategy work borrowers should focus on repaying as much debt as they can during the interest-free period."

The best deals come with a zero per cent interest period of 18 months. But if you use the card to make new purchases the interest rate can be as high as 20.74 per cent. Plus, your repayments will be allocated towards that debt rather than your transferred balance.

If you can't pay off the debt during the zero per cent interest period, the revert rate (the interest rate that applies at the end of the zero per cent period) will be important. Or you will need to find another balance transfer deal when the zero per cent period expires.

Hassan advises people to keep an eye on the card's annual fee. "This can be around A$100 (NZ$104) or more, depending on the provider." A transfer fee of 1 to 2.5 per cent of the debt transferred onto the new card can apply as well.

Personal loans

Personal loans can seem expensive when you compare them with a zero per cent balance transfer but there are some situations where they can be your best friend. Perhaps your debt is too big to pay off in the space of a zero per cent term; or you have multiple debts you want to consolidate; or you know you'll find it hard to resist using a new credit card.

If you want the certainty of paying off a regular amount without being tempted to spend on a credit card a personal loan can be the way to go.

Hassan says personal loan rates range from 7.9 to 17 per cent for fixed and variable rate personal loans. Apart from the interest rate, she advises borrowers to check the fees and repayment flexibility.

There can be an application fee, monthly fees and annual fees on these loans and some loans may not allow you to make extra repayments.

Peer-to-peer lending

Peer-to-peer (P2P) lending is another way to consolidate debt into a personal loan. It involves borrowing from investors matched via platforms like Harmoney and Lendme in New Zealand.

Personal loan rates are generally lower than those offered by traditional lenders (ranging from 7.9 to 9.42 per cent) and the application process faster.

But the best rates are not necessarily available to all borrowers. "Borrowers can benefit from lower personalised rates based on their credit score," Hassan says.

Loan amounts are generally lower than the personal loans offered by traditional lenders, too. "Generally, borrowers can only borrow up to $35,000."

Often the risks of P2P lending emphasise how the investors can be affected by borrowers who default. There is also a risk for borrowers if the platform closes down – they may not lose money in the equation, but they would likely face inconvenience. Quakle in the UK closed down in 2011, with an almost 100 per cent default rate.

Hassan's tips for people considering a P2P loan: "Check the credibility of the platform and make sure they have a relevant credit licence."

The provider should also issue you with a credit guide. "That outlines what the borrower should do if things don't go to plan," she says.
