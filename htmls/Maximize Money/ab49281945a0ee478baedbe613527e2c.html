Increased number of students move back home to pay off debt

  • Bailey Schulz | Art by Haley Heesacker
  • Dec. 10, 2015
  • (…)

According to a recent study, more than one-third of college students who enrolled in fall 2015 are planning to move back home after graduation.

The study, a Harrison Poll conducted on behalf of the American Institute of CPAs, was administered in August and given to 751 college freshmen. The study indicated that the students’ main reason for moving back home is to help pay off student loan debt.

Megan Scherling, program coordinator for the Student Money Management Center, said moving back home can be a smart financial move, depending on the student.

“It can be (a smart decision) as long as they’re using the money that they’re saving to prepare to be on their own,” she said. “Sometimes we see students that aren’t really sure what kind of job they want or can’t find a job and move home, but then they’re spending all of their extra money and then they never really get off the ground again.”

Aidan Malmstrom, a freshman finance major, said he’s not surprised that so many students plan to move back home because of the cost of college. While he doesn’t know where he’ll live after graduation, he said he doesn’t want to move back home.

“But if I have to, I will,” he said.

Elizabeth Rice, a junior secondary education, social science and history major, said it’s more common for her to see someone moving back home than away after college. She personally knows about 15 people moving back home after graduation.

Scherling said it’s no surprise so many students move back home.

“We’re still kind of recovering from the recession, but I think a lot of that was students were not being able to find jobs that pay as much as they thought or only part-time jobs, so it was harder to afford rent,” she said.

Heather Reed, director of First-Year Experience & Transition Programs, said she sees a lot of students moving back home after graduation, as well. She agreed that a recession plays a big part in so many students moving back home today.

“Parents saved up for their students’ college when the recession hit. And now those students are in college, right? And so that’s a big piece. And so they’re not coming in with as much savings,” she said. “It’s still easier to take loans out than anything else sometimes.”

Moving back home has both positive and negative effects on students, according to Scherling. On one hand, students are given the chance to avoid being locked down with year-long contracts that often come with renting, and they save money. On the other hand, students could lose some of the freedom they gained in college.

“That transition to go back into the home can be very challenging,” Reed said. “Establishing good ground rules with your parents, and is it okay that I bring somebody over and when is it not, how late is too late… you’ve kind of got to adapt.”

And living at home doesn’t always mean finding a place for free. Some parents may expect rent payments, help with grocery bills or other forms of payment.

Scherling advised graduates who are living with parents and wanting to move out to know their budgets and how much they owe in loans.

“I think that a lot of people could move out of their parents’ house in a short period of time as long as they’re actively looking for jobs and making a budget and making sure they are not spending all of their money on stuff they don’t need,” she said.

Along with the number of students ready to move back home, the study also showed 37 percent of students are open to taking a job outside their field of study.

Reed said most students who switch their field of study do so to help pay back their student loans.

“Thirty-seven percent say, ‘Hey, I would go outside of my field of study because maybe I could make more money in another field,’” Reed said.

Another statistic the study shared was the lack of knowledge students have about their loans. According to the study, 79 percent of the students didn’t know how much they will have taken out in loans by their graduation, and 36 percent didn’t know or only had a vague idea of the total amount of loans upon graduation.

The general rule when taking out loans, Scherling said, is students should not owe more in loans than what they’d make in their first year’s salary.

“There’s a lot of students that not only think they owe less than they really do, but there are students that think they owe more,” Scherling said. “And payment comes usually six months after graduation, so you want to make sure that you’re ready for that, and you know what your monthly payments will be.”

Most graduates are put on a standard repayment plan that lasts 10 years, but there are also payment periods of 20 or 25 years that students can choose. While the longer payment periods lower the monthly payments, they also increase the interest rates.
