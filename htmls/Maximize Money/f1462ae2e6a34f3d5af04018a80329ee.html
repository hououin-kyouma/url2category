13 Money Saving Tips for College and University Students


You’re off to university, eager to experience living away from home and enjoying the freedom it promises. It’s an exciting time in life: you’ll make new friends, learn about topics and issues that will stretch your mind and try all kinds of new experiences. It’s your first step on the path toward career and independence, and there is much to discover.

With freedom, however, comes responsibility, and that includes fending for yourself financially. Obtaining a post-secondary education, whether at a college or a university, can be expensive. Beyond tuition, there are housing and food to consider, as well as textbooks, clothing and activities. If you aren’t careful, expenses can spiral out of control.

Don’t panic! A part of the education you get at university or college is learning life skills. You’ll learn to manage your money. You’ll also learn how to take advantage of opportunities to save money, another useful lesson. Here are 13 tips for assessing expenses and coping with your newfound financial freedom:


1. Tap Into Financial Aid

Tuition is pricey, so investigate the options for assistance, whether student loans, scholarships or awards. Your institution’s financial aid office is a great place to visit regularly, since the staff are tuned into the myriad of offerings available.

Often, alumni have created awards geared toward outstanding students, students with specific interests or students from particular backgrounds. Why not see if you qualify?


2. Invest The Extra

If you don’t need all of your loan or scholarship funds immediately, consider placing it into an interest-bearing vehicle, such as a mutual fund. Why not let your money earn interest until you need it?


3. Compare Housing Options


Is living on campus in a residence cheaper than living off campus? Do some homework to see whether you’ll realize savings by choosing one option over the other.

When you weigh the possibilities, consider that an apartment may also mean paying for utilities and transportation. Do, however, remember that for your first year, living in residence may smooth the path toward making friends.


4. Insure Your Valuables

Think about protecting the items you own that would be expensive to replace: your laptop, your cellphone, your bicycle and your speakers are good examples. Residences are home to many people, not all of them honest.

For a small monthly fee, you can get renter’s insurance to save you the cost of replacing stolen property.


5. Evaluate Meal Plans

You need to eat, but how many meals will you actually eat in the dining hall? If you skip breakfast and munch a bagel instead, you may not want 21 meals weekly.

Consider the trade-offs between the various options and choose the one that suits your lifestyle best.


6. Think About Textbooks


Textbooks are expensive. Before you rush off to the bookstore and spend hundreds of dollars on books you may never use again, consider alternatives. You may be able to get a copy at the library, share one with a classmate, download one or purchase a used version.


Check with upper-year students in your program to see if they have books they’d like to dump. Perhaps someone will be kind enough to offer a loan.


7. Create A Budget

Learning to live within your means is a terrific skill that will serve you well forever. Look at your monthly income/funds and make a list of all of the expenses you know you’ll have. The remainder is yours to spend or save.

Track your spending for a month or two to look for patterns, see where those dollars go and whether there are places you can cut back or find cheaper alternatives.


8. Get A Job


Most campuses have work-study positions available, giving you a bit of extra income for assisting in a departmental office or at the dining hall. If you have computer skills or writing talent, you can pick up freelance jobs for a bit of extra cash.


9. Cash In On Student Discounts

Your student ID card is a valuable asset. Many stores, restaurants, entertainment venues and travel companies offer discounts of 10 to 15% for students. Even outlets outside the surrounding area may be part of a discount plan; it’s always worth asking.


10. Switch Banks … If Necessary

Many banks offer free ATM usage and waive other fees for students as a way of building loyalty for the days when you are earning good money.


11. Enjoy Free Food


Scout out the opportunities for free food on campus. If you belong to a student club, there will often be pizza nights. Volunteers for various activities may get fed, since they’re not getting paid.

Lectures and conferences often serve refreshments and snacks, so attend a few – why not broaden your mind and feed yourself at the same time?


12. Cut The Coffee

Buying coffee and snacks on campus adds up. Why not bring an insulated cup from home and pack your own goodies and pocket that change?


13. Appreciate Activities

Why look for pricey off-campus entertainment when you can likely find free activities on campus? A gym membership is generally part of your student fee, so take advantage of it.

Many faculties or institutes host free lectures, films and concerts with high-quality speakers/performers; broaden your mind while tightening your wallet.


It’s a challenge to fend for yourself, but you’ll soon learn your own tips and tricks for staying solvent. Make your education away from the classroom count.
