Money saving tips for adult students

Money saving tips for adult students

  • July 23rd, 2015
  • Kate Lyons
  • Career, Community, Education, Events
  • 0 Comments
Returning to school as an adult can be a costly, albeit rewarding, experience. Let’s face it, it costs money to earn a quality degree. And while many students take out student loans to help make ends meet, your pocketbook can get a little tight.

There are some things you should know when getting your finances in order for a trip back to the classroom (whether virtual or in real life). This article discusses those things and provides you with tips you can use as an adult student to save money.

Know the facts

First, you should know the facts behind your tuition. Find out what fees are associated with the education program in which you are interested. Knowing how much everything will cost and whether you have to pay for things up front or when the cost is accrued can help you avoid financial surprises.

In addition to knowing the facts behind your tuition, understand how you will be paying. If you are taking out loans, you may choose to opt to pay off loan interest while you are still completing your coursework. This will save you a great amount of money.

Also, take advantage of tax incentives for being a student. There are education credits you may qualify for on your taxes, and you can check with your tax preparer to see if you are able to write off any interest you pay on your student loan debt. Plus, starting a tax free college savings account can help you pay for your expenses while saving money.

Money saving tips

While loans defer your payment date, you will still need to pay for the classes you are taking. A good way to prepare for those student loan payments is to start cutting back now. Every little bit counts, and cutting corners before and during your program can make life a little more comfortable after you earn your degree.
Make a budget

The first tip is to make a budget and stick to it. You are a student and you should live like one. While many young adults on traditional campuses extol the financial benefits of a diet of Ramen Noodles, this may not be the best choice for adult students. After all, there are often families to consider and nutritional needs. Likewise, adult students have bills and other financial obligations that students of a traditional college age do not have to worry about.

One way you can stick to your budget is to separate your needs from your wants. In the moment it is easy to think you need something, but backing away from the situation and adding some perspective might provide the clarity needed to know that the item isn’t needed.

For example, while in grad school I really felt I needed a new smart phone. This new phone would make my life so much easier with its new app features and bells and whistles. In truth, the phone I already had worked just fine. There was nothing wrong with it, and the new model that I wanted would not drastically change my life as a student. So, I decided I did not need the phone and refocused my efforts on what was really important, my studies.

Cut unnecessary expenses

Cutting unnecessary expenses can help save money. Cable may seem like a necessary expense, but in truth you will be able to get by without it. After all, there will be less time to watch tv when you are dividing your time between your family and your studies.

The average American family spent $123 a month on pay-TV in 2013. That works out to be $1,476 a year. That’s a lot of money you could be saving, especially since many Americans receive some broadcast channels without signing on with a cable company or dish provider. If you don’t think you can go cold turkey without the channels, think about cutting back.

Another way to save money on entertainment is to get rid of Netflix and Hulu and instead watch your DVDs and borrow movies from friends. While the video streaming services are great, they are an added expense. Netflix’s most inexpensive streaming plan runs $7.99 per month, or $95.88 a year. And that’s just the one screen viewing price. Hulu costs the same monthly for their plan that does not include Showtime (with popular shows like Homeland and House of Lies). If a person were to subscribe to both, they would be paying more than $190 a year.

You can also save money by not purchasing a new printer for your coursework. The one you already have will likely do the trick, and if you don’t have one you may not need to get one. Sure, you’ll need to print things out every now and then (or more often depending on your learning style), but you don’t necessarily need to incur the cost of a printer, ink, and paper.

Check with friends to see if you would be able to borrow a printer. Print your pages from a public library. The pennies you spend per page will likely be cheaper than the cost of owning a printer. Check with work to see if you would be able to use the printer there. Many companies have strict policies on this sort of activity, so you’ll want to make sure you check first.

Save money

Stop making trips to expensive coffee shops for your morning pick-me-up. Instead, invest in a coffee machine. Prices vary, and you don’t have to spend a ton of money to see the savings start to pour in. A simple cup of coffee could end up costing you hundreds of dollars a year. Do the math on your beverage of choice. While making your own coffee at home may not be as appealing, it is definitely more appealing than being in massive amounts of debt.

You can also cut back by going shopping at places that offer student discounts. The inevitable shopping trip will come up, and there are a number of retailers that offer percentages off for when it does. Generally, all you need to do is show your school ID. While these retailers may not advertise these discounts in their stores, there is information about their discounts online.

While you are compiling your shopping list, use price comparison websites and apps to shop the sales. This comes in especially handy with grocery shopping and buying everyday goods. These websites are pretty easy to find online and there are even websites and reviews that compile the best of them.

You can also save a considerable amount of money by cutting down on lunch runs. Going out to lunch every day of the work week could end up costing you thousands of dollars (based on an an $8 lunch figure). Just because you are taking your own lunch to work doesn’t mean it has to be boring. There are plenty of websites that offer tips and advice for how to spice up workday meals.

Sell unwanted items

By selling unwanted and seldom used items you can find cash where you didn’t realize you had it. Take a look around your house for old iPads, cell phones, clothes, electronics, furniture, etc. Websites like Gazelle and Glyde will either buy your items from you or help you sell them. You can post other items on websites like EBAY and Craigslist, and they’ll connect you with potential buyers. Always be careful when arranging a meeting between yourself and a buyer. When conducting business over the Internet you never really know what you are getting yourself into.
It is wise for students to start thinking about saving money upon beginning a degree program rather than waiting until the bill is due. You’ll feel less of a financial strain later if you start saving now.

One of the best things you can do for yourself while you’re in school is to go to class, complete assignments, and get good grades so you don’t end up needing to retake a class or withdrawing before you have finished your degree. Just because you didn’t successfully complete a course doesn’t mean you won’t have to pay for it.
