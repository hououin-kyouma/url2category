GET OUT OF THE RED

Martin Lewis reveals how to escape the hell of living in your overdraft and how you can get out debt

The founder of MoneySavingExpert.com warned that dipping into an unarranged overdraft can be more expensive than taking out a payday loan

By Tara Evans, Digital Consumer Editor
5th June 2017, 12:47 pm
Updated: 5th June 2017, 2:18 pm

MARTIN LEWIS has warned that going into an unarranged overdraft can be more expensive than taking out a payday loan.

The founder of MoneySavingExpert.com issued a warning on ITV’s This Morning programme.

He said: “If you do the maths on it the effective APR is higher than a payday loan. It’s one of the most expensive things you can do.”

Mr Lewis also set out if his advice to help people get out of the red in their bank account and pay off their debts.

“If you’re overdrawn your overdraft is a debt, like any other and it needs to be dealt with as such. You need to manage it. You need to get it lower”, he added.

“Even if you only dip in occasionally it can be expensive. Some people are paying hundreds of pounds every year”.

Mr Lewis said that those who are in debt can switch to a different bank but warned that they would first need to pass a credit score to do it.

He said: “Many people who are overdrawn will be allowed to shift. It can be a very good thing to do.”

Mr Lewis then picked out two bank accounts which could be good for people looking to pay off their overdraft.

The first one was from First Direct. A bank that is consistently top of customer satisfaction polls.

It pays customers who switch £125 and also has a 0 per cent overdraft with a £250 limit. To get the deal you’d need to paying at least £2,000 a month.

Mr Lewis then mentioned a second account which is better suited to be people with “much bigger” overdrafts.

The Nationwide’s Flex-Direct current account has a 0 per cent overdraft for 12 months, which makes it ideal for people who have a large chunk of money to pay off.

He then went on to explain that some customers might be better off moving their overdraft to a 0 per cent credit card.

These specialist “money transfer” credit cards give those in debt up to 36 months to pay off the money.

Although, customers will have to pay a one-off 2.9 per cent fee. These are only available to people with top credit scores though, Mr Lewis warned.

For more information on how to get out of debt, see MoneySavingExpert.com’s guide to cutting overdraft costs.
