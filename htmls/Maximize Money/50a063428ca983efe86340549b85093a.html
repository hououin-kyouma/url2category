Think It’s Impossible To Start Saving Money As A College Student? Here’s Why You’re Wrong (And How To Do It)

By Christopher Murray  • September 12, 2017

Saving money as a college student may seem impossible, but if you cut back on a few things, you’d be surprised how much you can save.

College costs a lot. Duh. But it’s more than just tuition and room and board.

The cost of books alone will make you cringe, but there’s also school supplies, meals out when you just can’t stomach the mystery meat in the dining hall, activity fees (which most colleges conveniently don’t mention until you want to do extracurriculars), and of course beer (for those students over the age of 21, of course).

That measly amount of money you make as a work-study student, or a side-job that exhausts you, can be hard to stretch. And the thought of actually saving some of your money seems impossible. But there are a few places where you can find an extra few-dollars here and there.

Use the library

Unless you’re a science student whose professor is cruel and makes you buy the $250 newest edition textbook, don’t buy your books.

Your school library should have access to all the books you need, and if not talk to your librarian. In my experience, they like having something to do and can easily find the books you need.

You’ll be saving a good $100-$300 a semester.

Work set-up events

On college campuses there are always events going on. Luckily, they need people (usually students who will work for next to nothing) to help set up and take down chairs, tables, or whatever else is needed.

Granted, you won’t make a whole lot, but the few hours you put in during the morning or late night will give you a little extra money. Plus, the job is one of the easiest you’ll have.

I’ve heard that catering jobs in particular pay well, and they let you take home some free food at the end of big events!

Sing on the streets

This may sound like an odd piece of advice, but I knew a few people in college who were musically inclined and would set up shop on street corners and play for a couple of hours.

Again, this won’t earn you a whole lot of money (unless someone is feeling particularly giving), but you’ll be doing something you enjoy and you can earn a few extra bucks.

Work at a coffee shop

College towns are filled with coffee shops that need employees on weekends or in the mornings. If you can fit it into your schedule, a part-time job can go a long way. And at coffee shops you’ll likely be making tips.

If you can’t fit a side hustle into your busy schedule, consider becoming a Resident Advisor (RA) in one of the dorms on campus. Typically, an RA gets free room and board in exchange for weekly meetings, and making sure parties don’t get too loud.

Avoid meal plans

When I found out how much I was paying per meal for terrible dining hall food, I wanted to scream (and mourn the fact that I hardly ever got up for breakfast).

It was something like seven dollars for breakfast and lunch and almost $14 for dinner. So skipping breakfast cost me $35 a week (breakfast was only served five days a week).

If your college has multiple meal plans, choose the one that’s minimal and buy other food in bulk, like pasta or ramen.

Use public transportation

If you go to a college anywhere with a public transportation system, take full advantage. Typically, there are discounted or free passes for students.

If there are occasions when you absolutely need a car, Zipcars are becoming more and more popular in college towns. Or there’s always students who have a car and may be willing to give you a ride in exchange for gas money or snacks.

Go to free events (especially ones with food)

This one may seem obvious, and maybe you’re thinking it’s not cool to go to events sponsored by your university, but trust me, more people go to them than you probably realize.

Colleges tend to have guest speakers, movies, game nights, and even parties that are free to their students. Usually they come with free food. Hitting up these events rather than going out to a bar or restaurant can save you quite a bit of money.

Don’t study abroad

Everyone always says travel when you’re young and to take advantage of study abroad programs. I wholeheartedly agree…if you can afford it.

As someone who opted out of studying abroad at the last minute due to cost, I learned that it’s extremely difficult to study abroad when you’re on a budget or trying to save money for expenses after graduation.

Even if your tuition is transferable and you don’t need to pay any extra to actually attend a school overseas, you’ll still need to factor in the cost of a plane ticket, all of your necessities you’ll need to buy when you get over there, and room and board (most European schools, for example, do not provide room and board to upperclassmen).

If you just have to go abroad because you don’t think you’ll get the chance to again, try choosing a place that’s on our list of cheapest study abroad destinations.

Summary

Saving money as a college student is difficult, especially where there are so many extra expenses you may not have considered. But there are a few areas where cutting back can help you save hundreds.
