Credit card debt getting out of control again

Wednesday
Jan 4, 2017 at 3:47 PM Jan 4, 2017 at 4:56 PM

Rene Ray De La Cruz Staff Writer @DP_ReneDeLaCruz

VICTORVILLE — After millions of Americans struggled to climb out of debt after the Great Recession, many of them are right back where they started.

WalletHub recently announced Americans may soon have as much credit card debt as they did during the Great Recession of the late 2000s, with indebted households having credit card balances averaging $16,061 — just shy of 2008's high mark.

According to WalletHub, Americans racked up a record-setting $21.9 billion in credit card debt during the third quarter of last year. The record was marked as the largest third-quarter debt increase since 2007 and the seventh-largest in the last 30 years.

The Daily Press spoke to dozens of High Desert residents who either claimed to have their credit card debt under control, or were struggling to survive financially and having to go the way of plastic on many occasions to make ends meet.

College professor Ruth Nolan, who grew up in Apple Valley, said her card debt is “within payable reaches” as she makes just enough money to pay them off in a reasonable time.

“We have too much credit card debt, but not more than we can handle,” said Nita Paddack of Apple Valley. “We never use them anymore and are working on paying them off.”

Paddack said a large chunk of her family’s debt came from paying airfare, hotel accommodations and food when they accompanied their late daughter, Jessica Paddack, to New York for medical treatment.

Numbers are still being tabulated, but WalletHub projects final numbers for 2016 will show a net increase of roughly $80 billion in credit card debt, which would bring outstanding balances within striking distance of 2008’s all-time record.

Several High Desert residents told the Daily Press they owe anywhere from $500 to $30,000 in credit card debt, with many of them remarking they’ve had to use their cards to pay for medical bills, car payments, students loan payments and an occasional monthly mortgage.

“Our family has never really recovered from the recession,” said Mark Larson, 42, of Phelan, who lost his full-time job in 2009. “We tread water every month and occasionally use our credit card when we go under.”

With the previous three quarters showing equally high debt results, WalletHub believes an ominous trend is beginning, with 2016 representing “serious cause for concern.” The financial site predicts the trend to continue, resulting in debt levels hitting pre-recession levels sometime in 2019.

Total debt, which included mortgages, auto loans and student loans, was expected to surpass the amounts owed at the beginning of the Great Recession by the end of 2016, WalletHub reported.

Household debt has been fueling mortgage debt, which jumped from $159,020 per household in 2010 to $172,806 in 2016. Debt from auto loans also grew from $20,032 in 2010 to $28,535 in 2016.

Larson was one of several residents who said they took a financial hit during the recession, with many residents losing jobs, experiencing pay cuts or taking lower paying jobs.

Some residents told the Daily Press they avoid using credit cards, use cash only or pay off their credit card debt on a monthly basis.

"There are people out there using their credit cards to buy TVs and vacations, but I think most of the debt is caused by people just trying to survive," Larson said. "I think most of us are struggling."
