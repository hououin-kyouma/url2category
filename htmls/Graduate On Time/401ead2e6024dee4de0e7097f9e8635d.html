How to Get Your College Kid to Graduate in 4 Years

August 29, 2016 | Posted in: College, Kids and Money

Want to help your child graduate from college on time? Here are tips on how to push them through school in four, rather than six, years.

Prepare for College

College-bound kids? Let’s plan.

By Lisa Kaplan Gordon

A four-year college education has gone the way of VH8 cassettes, with more than half of students taking five or six years to complete their higher education. 

What’s a parent planning for college to do? Save more? Select a less expensive school? Lay down the law and tell junior he’s out in four or pays for the extra years? 

Here’s what you don’t do: Invade retirement funds to pay for your child’s higher education, or overfund college savings vehicles like 529 plans.

Dipping into retirement investments to pay for college is risky. There are ways to make college more affordable, but you don’t have that same flexibility when funding your retirement. Pensions and Social Security may provide a base of income, but personal savings will have to deliver the rest of your retirement income. 

529 plans carry restrictions about how the money can be used, and by whom. To give yourself some financial flexibility, we suggest saving no more than 80% of the cost of four years of school in your 529, then putting the remaining 20% of college costs in an unrestricted investment account. 

If you can afford to stash away another two years’ college expenses without hampering your ability to save for your own retirement, it may make sense to invest those funds in an unrestricted account, too. 

Do you know how much you’ll need to pay for college? Goal Investor’s Education Fund Guide helps you design a clear plan. 

College Reality

Still confident your child will be marching to “Pomp and Circumstance” in four years? Check out these sobering facts from the Chronicle of Higher Education, the U.S. Department of Education and the advocacy group Complete College America.

  • Four-year graduation rate at public colleges: 33.3%
  • Four-year graduation rate at private colleges: 52.8%
  • Six-year graduation rate at public colleges: 57.6%
  • Six-year graduation rate at private colleges: 65.4%
  • Six-year graduation rate at private for-profit institutions: 26.5%
  • Students who still haven’t earned a degree after eight years: Nearly 40%.

Paying for Extra Years

If those stats don’t make your heart race, keep in mind that scholarships and merit money typically last no more than four years, so tuition rises to the rack rate for years five and six. 

Student loan debt can rise significantly after four years if you use private or federally unsubsidized loans that begin charging interest as soon as you get them. That interest compounds during extra years of school. 

Hint: If you can do it without straining your budget or reducing your retirement savings, begin paying the monthly interest on private student loans as soon as they’re issued to avoid compounding.

To make matters worse, every extra year a student stays in school amounts to another year of lost wages, a median $43,000 in 2015 for those with a bachelor’s degree, according to New York Federal Reserve data.

Six-year graduates, therefore, not only pay more in tuition and potentially accrue more debt, they also sacrifice nearly $90,000 in lost income. 

Whose Fault Is It? 

Why can’t today’s students muddle through college in four years? 

Complete College’s experts say kids: 

  • Arrive unprepared for college-level work, forcing them to take remedial classes.
  • Lose credits when they transfer to a new school.
  • Change majors and need different classes to graduate. 

The group says schools are to blame, too, because they: 

  • Close kids out of classes they need to graduate.
  • Require more than the 120 credits typically needed to graduate.
  • Don’t offer required classes frequently enough.
  • Set complicated graduation requirements that are hard to meet in four years. 

Here’s What You Can Do 

Don’t panic. Advance planning, smart school selection and a little tough love will help and encourage your child to complete college on schedule. 

Try these seven tips for shepherding your teen to an on-time graduation: 

1. Wait until they know what they want to do. 

Attending college on autopilot is a chief reason kids don’t graduate on time, says Jack Schacht, founder of My College Planning Team, an Illinois consortium of college planners. 

“Changing majors is one of the significant factors on why they’re not getting out in four years,” Schacht says, suggesting students delay college until they discover their passion or at least know what they want to study. 

Career assessment testing and counseling might provide direction, as can a gap year focused on volunteering, travel or work. 

2. Push your child to take advanced classes in high school, if he can handle the rigor. 

Encourage your high schooler to take Advanced Placement or International Baccalaureate® classes and to study for the end-of-course exams to earn college credits. 

When college shopping, check the advanced placement credit policy — not every school lets you use AP/IB credits to cut time off your degree. 

Smart, motivated kids can also test out of intro-level courses via the College-Level Examination Program (CLEP), earning three credits for each $80 exam passed. Almost 3,000 schools accept CLEP credits. 

3. Send your teen to community college. 

Have your high school junior or senior knock out some general prerequisites at community college during the summers. It’s a good way to see what interests them. 

Caveat: Avoid taking classes in the intended major, because schools have different requirements and don’t always accept major courses from other colleges. 

4. Don’t fall into the transfer trap. 

A third of all two- and four-year college students transfer schools — 47% of those who moved in 2014 changed from one four-year school to another, potentially losing hard-earned credits along the way, according to The Journal of College Admission. 

Some tactics to reduce the risk of transfer:

    • Don’t encourage your child to follow the herd to the same top schools that show up annually in Best Colleges articles.
    • Go beyond the college tour and sit in on classes to assess teaching styles.
    • Have your high school senior spend a weekend on campus to get a real feel for the schools that accepted him.
    • Pay attention to social fit. If you’ve raised an independent, don’t send him to a school where 90% of kids go Greek.
    • If your freshman is struggling socially or academically, encourage him to seek help from an advisor before he becomes overwhelmed. 

5. Evaluate the time commitment athletic scholarships require. 

Weigh the costs and benefits of sports scholarships, especially if the program encourages athletes to take a reduced course load during the playing season. Unless your child can use AP/IB or CLEP credits to fill in the gaps, he could come up short senior year and have to attend at least one more semester to graduate. 

Verify any statements the coach makes about time commitments by checking with players who’ve already graduated (look for them on LinkedIn). 

6. Check the stats.

College guidebooks publish schools’ four-year graduation rates. You also can get schools’ graduation data from the Chronicle’s College Completion website. 

7. Read the fine print on fifth-year promises. 

Some colleges “guarantee” that your child will graduate on time, or the fifth year is “free.” Check the stipulations on those guarantees. Your child may have to forgo study abroad, or he may have to declare and stick to one major to earn the “free” year. 

A clear, practical plan and steady contributions win the college savings race. Find out what you need to set aside each month to save for the education you want to give your child.
