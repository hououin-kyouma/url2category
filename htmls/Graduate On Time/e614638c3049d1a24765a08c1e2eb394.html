Want to graduate in 4 years? Go to a private school

Daniel WheatonContact Reporter

California Gov. Jerry Brown wants more college students to graduate within four years, something that is becoming more difficult for students nationwide.

In proposing his latest budget summary, he listed the four-year graduation rates of the Cal State schools, and nearly all of them fell behind the national average of 34 percent for public universities.

The message from the governor came as the Cal State Board of Trustees met in Long Beach this week.

Brown didn’t detail exactly how he wanted the board to improve the low graduation rates, as it will be up to the board to put together a plan.

Four-year graduation rates lag nationally for several reasons. As the Complete College Group explains in their “Four Year Myth” report, incoming freshman aren’t always certain what they want to study and might not be ready for certain college-level courses.

Students who start college without declaring a major, or have to take remedial courses, can add several semesters to their time in college. Eric Forbes, Cal State’s assistant vice chancellor for student academic service, told the Los Angeles Times that the system is still reeling from cuts in 2013. Those cuts resulted in a loss of faculty and classes, making it even harder to graduate on time.

The group advocates for having more general education courses count across multiple majors, to help undecided students find their passion without delaying graduation drastically. The delayed graduation has tangible costs: the report estimates that taking six years to graduate increases college debt by 70 percent.

The report notes that research intensive schools tend to have a higher 4-year graduation rate than smaller schools, 36 percent versus 19 percent, according to their own internal metrics. More than half of students in the University of California System graduate within four years. The UC system is more selective than the Cal State system, favoring students likely to excel in a more competitive environment.

Socioeconomics play a role as well: not needing to work or care for family expedites graduation, the report notes, so colleges that serve lower-income and part-time students will have lower 4-year graduation rates.

San Diego’s colleges and universities follow the national trends: Private colleges tend to have the highest graduation rates, followed by large selective research institutions. Private colleges that derive their funding from enrollment numbers often have plans in place that keep students on a 4-year graduation track, while state schools tend to give students more freedom in determining their course load. Conversely, the larger the campus the harder it might be to register for certain classes, another possible delay.

In most cases, with a four-year framework, it's difficult to measure how a school is performing. Last year, SDSU was lauded in a report for nearly doubling 6-year graduation rates for minority students, with white students showing significant gains in the last decade. Cal State San Marcos will graduate about half of its students in 5 years, which differs from its 4-year rate of 14 percent.

The Complete College Group encourages schools to streamline requirements so less time is spent on prerequisites and remedial courses. Improved advising is also critical, as it helps students to make the most out of the array of class options.

But, if students have to take an extra semester or two, it isn’t the end of the world.
