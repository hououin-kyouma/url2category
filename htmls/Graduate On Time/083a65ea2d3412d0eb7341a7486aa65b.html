How do people graduate from colleges in less than four years? Do they take exams for courses they already knew and skip the beginner level courses? Is graduating from college early is worth it?

Others here have answered the question already of whether it is possible, so I will try to enrich your other question, of whether it is worth it? In short, it is not worth graduating early, unless you have severe financial constraints or a work bond (like some from Singapore) that make the incentive extremely high to finish as quickly as possible. Here is why:
  1. College > Classes: The view that college is equal to the classes you take (i.e. graduating early by finishing classes) is a very limited view of college. If this were true, I could guarantee in 10 years, that we'd all just do classes online and college as we know it would disappear, yet MIT and Stanford put their material online for the world to use, so clearly there classes are a small part of what comprises undergrad.
  2. Don't Graduate Young: Graduating early in many cases also means you'll leave when you are younger than most others going into the work force. This can seriously suck! I loved the fact that I was 21 in my Junior year of college, it enabled me to go to an awesome Mexican bar and restaurant near campus (Mad4Mex) with friends (they didn't allow you to enter if under 21). I had some amazing times with friends there, and is still a top fond memory of college! Also, when you start working, if your colleagues are going to a bar, you don't want to be disallowed from entering. (ps. I am not encouragin drinking, simply ENTERING the bar).
  3. Summers for Exploring: If you graduate early it means you have less summers during college. This means less internship/other experience opportunities! This can be a major disadvantage, to not have explored all of your career opportunities extensively.
  4. Extra-Curricular Activities: While in college, you should be doing a lot of outside class activities. These activities can be seriously enriching in ways that have a direct positive impact on your professional life. A simple example is being on the leadership board of a club and helping organize activities. These organisation, communication and follow-through skills are important no matter what you do next. Especially if you can successfully get ultra-busy undergrads to show up in high numbers (without just awesome free food), it shows amazing marketing skills as well!
  5. Friendships/Relationships: You want to spend 4 years cultivating amazing friends and relationships. To not do so in a safe and concentrated environment like undergrad is a major missed social opportunity. A large fraction of my best friends are from undergrad, and I expect this to remain true over time. This is in no small part because of a large amount of shared experience (very little of which is tied to classes). These people are also my strongest professional network now and have certainly helped me professionally as well!
  6. Extra Classes: Finally, you can take a number of 'other' classes, both at a higher level (masters classes for example) or in different fields. This can be academically very enriching. However, as I said earlier, classes are only a small part of the experience, and value!
So don't rush it, and certainly don't assume classes are everything.

(Adapted from Lawrence Chiou's answer to How do people graduate from colleges in less than four years? Do they take exams for courses they already knew and skip the beginner level courses? Is graduating from college early is worth it?)

Generally  speaking, it's possible to complete college in as little as two or  three years depending on your pace of work, college credits accrued  during high school, and qualification for credit by exam.*

Many  colleges and universities award a significant number of credits for AP  exam scores (as many as 8 credit hours for a single AP exam) or college  courses taken in high school, which can add up to a lot of credits. From  a combination of college classes taken and credit for AP exams, I had  upwards of 70 credit hours on my Iowa State University transcript by the  time I had graduated from high school. A couple of my friends in a  similar boat who went to Iowa State graduated comfortably in two or  three years. Some had fewer AP credits but chose to do upwards of 20  credit hours a semester to graduate early.

Even  schools that don't offer credit for AP exam scores often have  provisions for early completion of undergraduate degrees. Harvard, for  instance, offers Advanced Standing or "sophomore standing" as a means to complete a bachelor's degree in three years.

That  said, many students who do qualify to graduate early often don't. The  vast majority of Harvard undergraduates qualify for Advanced Standing,  but it's rare for anyone other than combined bachelor's/master's  students to activate sophomore standing. It's a testament to the fact  that the intangibles of the undergraduate experience are valuable enough  that almost all of the students want to stay a full four years (which  is actually relatively short).

No matter where you end up going to college, you know you've found a great community and good fit if you find that you want to savor every additional moment of research experience, advanced coursework (including graduate coursework) with world-class instructors and peers, leadership experience from participating in student groups, and life experience gained from interacting with passionate and multi-talented peers.
*While  it'd be technically possible to complete college in one year, many of  those who do probably have special circumstances: for instance,  concurrent enrollment in college while in high school. Even if they  don't, at some point going to college becomes more of a formality than  an actual learning experience.
