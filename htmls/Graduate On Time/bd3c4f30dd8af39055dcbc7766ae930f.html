Community Question: Recommendations for Starting a Homeschool Coop

What does it take to graduate from your home school?

by Heather Sanders on March 28, 2012

  •
  • share
  • tweet
  • pin

Friday evening our family was graced to entertain a delightful young man and homeschool graduate. Knowing he was 18 I asked him if he was looking forward to graduating. Smiling, he explained that he graduated at age 16. Since then, he has steadily worked two jobs and is taking online courses to learn specific programming languages. His dream is to develop his own team of programmers and developers and work within the gaming industry. He and his older brother are also in the brainstorm and development phases of building an app.

Admittedly, before we attended last summer’s THSC Southwest Convention and Family Conference, the thought of a 16-year-old graduating from a home school would have been a bit of an eyebrow raiser for me. Until that point, I felt the only “right” way to homeschool a high school student was to progress through four years of high school curriculum that would ideally parallel that of any student enrolled in a Texas public high school. That mindset originated from concerns that if our children’s transcripts differed too much from that of public school students, they may be overlooked if/when they decided to apply for colleges.

However, one of the workshops Jeff and I attended at the conference opened our eyes to the advantage of letting our children graduate when they fulfilled their high school requirements, as opposed to pushing through four years just for the sake of it. Once completed, they could then begin advanced learning experiences in an area of their personal interest at the age of 16 or 17, right about the time they “normally” would enter their Junior or Senior High years.

This was a huge game-changer for us and felt much like we discovered the missing link. With Emelie entering high school I was really beginning to struggle with “expanding” her curriculum with additional, unnecessary, “busy” coursework just to ensure she didn’t finish a year’s work in a single semester. For those of you shaking your head at me, yes, I realize it was a ridiculous approach and a huge waste of time, but for whatever reason I truly felt that, since we were headed into high school, I had to stretch it out for four years.

Jeff and I both believe standards are important, so we are still in the process of deciding exactly what our home school graduation requirements will be, but we DO know that it will not include a compulsory 4-year attendance.

As we set about determining those standards, I searched online and discovered several links that clearly outlined state requirements to earn a high school diploma. And of course, the Texas Education Agency has clear requirements, as well as recommendations, for what public schooled high school students need (in credits) in order to graduate. Thankfully, home schools are treated like private schools in the state of Texas, and are not regulated. Therefore, we have a good deal of freedom in setting our graduation standards. I also discovered there is no minimum age requirement for graduation.

Naturally, Jeff and I do not want to limit our children’s college opportunities and see no reason to disregard the recommendations of our state entirely; we just may approach it with a different methodology. And thanks to the work of so many of the homeschool pioneers, as well as the state and federal work of the HSLDA, the “U.S. Department of Education allows homeschool graduates to self-certify completion of their secondary education in a homeschool setting.” Our serious responsibility is to strongly consider what requirements we will place on our own children before awarding them a self-certified diploma.

Now that we are coming to the close of Emelie’s “Freshman” year of homeschool we can look back on the past year to gain perspective as we consider future expectations for all our children’s ultimate “graduation”. This year has given us insight as we watched her balance education, work and play. Throughout the year she has narrowed her focus, has become more disciplined in her approach, and has taken on more responsibilities.

That said, we also recognize she is one of three children and that each one of them will approach the standards we set a bit differently. The point, we think, is simply to set the standard for them to reach, provide the help, resources and tools they need, and then, let them traverse the path with whatever approach best suits them.

I am intrigued by all the many ways families homeschool their children. At what point will you “graduate” your children, or if you have already done so, at what point did you “graduate” your children?

    We are “graduating” our first of 4 kids that we’ve homeschooled from the beginning of their school experience this spring.

    She was home full-time for her freshman and sophmore years. She began taking community college classes when she was 15 (In our cc system you can take the entrance test as earlier as 14).

    This past year, which would have been her senior year, she became a full-time student at the community college. But on paper this is her senior year of high school, therefore it is considered dual-enrollment for the college applications and the FAFSA. (We were instructed to document in this manner by the 4-year college she will be attending this fall.)

    Not keeping her at home for the full 4 years of high school was definitely the way to go with her (and I foresee with her 3 siblings).

    The biggest advantage was having her take ownership of her education and be responsible for her classes, time management and conversing with the instructors.

    She has done a remarkable job – we have been very pleased with the process and see many advantages to letting her go this route:

    1. she was in college while still at home, we helped with the minor adjustments of a classroom experience since she was young
    2. the tuition cost is lower
    3. the lower level classes (Eng 101, Speech, etc.) will transfer (make sure to check with prospective colleges)
    4. she is very confident is her abilities (both educational and social)
    5. she was able to take chemistry and biology labs that we could not replicate at home (she is going into nursing)

    We are very confident she will excel when she leaves home and the academic part will not be such a big transition – but being 1300+ miles from home might (but I doubt it!).

Artificially holding kids to a 4 year plan can result in this kind of thing.   • Sarah Fowler

  • http://rubberducky26.blogspot.com m @ rambling musings

    I went to public school and skipped or tested out of enough math and english classes that I wouldn’t have enough credits in these subjects to meet college entrance requirements (which are actually a combination of number of credits and competencies depending on the school). Yikes! My school district had a program which allowed me to take courses at the community college for free (because I was still in HS) and I leveraged that opportunity into getting my first two years of college requirements out of the way. Now, not every college will accept transfer credits. When I got to my state university I was able to enter my major program directly which meant skipping the large lecture format classes completely. Additionally, when competing with my entering class the college transcripts were a distinct advantage when applying for scholarships. Both socially and academically the years I spent as a hs student in the community college were the best years of hs.

    Point being that maybe not graduating your kids early might not be in the best interest for all involved. If your child is already active in a local coop, community college courses may feel like a natural extension of their existing coursework.

  • Katie D in LBK

    While I don’t homeschool [yet], I fully support the customization of a child’s education to give them the fullest advantages and academic encouragement.

    That being said, one thing to keep in mind, as someone who works with prospective students in higher education: Whatever you do with her high school curriculum, if foreign language [including ASL] is involved, make sure the curriculum is followed for two consecutive school/calendar years once your student has officially started high school. Most colleges and universities in the state of Texas [the state my institution is in] are now waiving foreign language requirements but only with proof of two consecutive years [passed] of the same language. Bear in mind that work prior to high school only counts as half a year each year, regardless of the level of work, and some colleges don’t count middle school work at all. [Spanish spread out over four semesters in 7th and 8th grade is only one curriculum year.]

    Aside from that, at about 16 many community colleges will let students start taking “dual credit” coursework, and core classes [English, math, science, etc] are a great option for that if your student is considering college. If a course is classified as “core” at any accredited institution in the state of Texas, any other institution in the state of Texas must transfer it in as the same. Classes within a discipline of interest/potential major do not always enjoy the same privilege due to vastly varying expectations between institutions. Be aware, though, that lower level college maths [college algebra and college mathematics, for example] are usually a “count one but not the other” sort of thing. Additionally, most institutions have a section of their registrar’s website where you can see how courses will transfer in.

  • WB

    I’ve got two early home school graduates currently in college. We found two very important factors in our decisions about both high school requirements and graduation dates – individual college requirements and state scholarship requirements.

    1. We looked at the requirements of the specific colleges that the kids wanted to attend. If the college doesn’t have a section on their website about requirements for home educated students, call up the admissions department and ask. We talked with admissions counselors who specialized in home education applicants and got the nitty gritty on what would have to be in the transcript to be considered.

    Every school is different – don’t assume one Florida state university will have the same requirements as another state university in Florida, for example. (UF required SAT subtests for homeschoolers but FSU did not.)

    2. We looked at scholarship requirements. This was extremely important at the time here in Florida. Both kids got “100%” tuition scholarships from the state because we were careful to meet the requirements.

    As an example, the state required home education students to be home educated in their junior and senior year of high school which sounds like a problem for early graduates but it wasn’t. We just put together a transcript using their 8th, 9th, 10th and 11th grades as the four years of high school, so that 10th and 11th grades looked like the junior and senior year. What this means is you may need to be thinking ahead about this in 7th and 8th grade.

    On another note — your home education diploma is just as good as any private school diploma. If a job application or college application asks if you graduated from high school or hold a high school diploma, the answer is simply yes…not yes, but…..
