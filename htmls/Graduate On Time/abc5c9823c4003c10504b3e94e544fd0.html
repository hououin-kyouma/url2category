    Getting into College and Paying for it

    University of North Carolina Greensboro to Offer 3-Year Degree

    By Jacques Steinberg
    February 22, 2010 1:30 pm February 22, 2010 1:30 pm

    The University of North Carolina at Greensboro is announcing today that it will allow “highly motivated students” to graduate in three years, beginning with the freshman class that will be seated this fall.

    Specifically, the program will be offered to incoming freshmen who already have at least 12 college credit hours, either through Advanced Placement or community college classes, or other accelerated work. By paring a year off their undergraduate experiences, those students would save an estimated $8,000, 0r 22 percent, in tuition, room, board and other fees, compared to their peers in four-year programs.

    In a statement, Linda P. Brady, the university chancellor, described the program as “perfect for students who are eager to earn a degree and get on with other life goals.” Among the majors for which the accelerated program will be offered are those in accounting, elementary education, information systems and romance languages.

    We’ve occasionally engaged readers of The Choice on the subject of three-year degrees. Last October, Senator Lamar Alexander, Republican of Tennessee and the former secretary of education, floated the idea in a Newsweek cover article. While Senator Alexander highlighted three-year degree programs at Lipscomb University and Hartwick College, both are private colleges, while Greensboro is public.

    What do you think of the UNC-Greensboro idea? Do the potential savings outweigh the potential benefits of a longer college experience? Please use the comment box below to let us know.

    AnonymousStudentT February 22, 2010 · 3:19 pm

    This strikes me as a public announcement of an already existing trend. The catch phrases are “freshmen with 12 credit hours” and “majors such as accounting, elementary education, information systems and romance languages” (i.e., small majors). A freshman entering college with 12 or more credit hours probably already has enough creds to graduate in three years if his/her major is small and if he/she is willing to take more classes per semester than average. That is, unless the school’s core curriculum is structured such that those freshmens’ credits cannot be applied to the core (which is how a lot of universities structure their core). Now, if the university had decided to restructure its core to allow students to use those credits toward graduation, then that would be something. If so, why only offer it in certain majors? And the phrase “highly motivated students” also catches the eye. That kind of makes me the think that the curricula will be structured such that students will have to take more courses per semester than average. And then, just like early graduating students in the past, you have to know that you want to major in these programs from the beginning. So, this announcement seems rather silly to me. I don’t see that they’re doing anything groundbreaking here. Even so, I’ve said it before and I’ll say it again, I think that pushing students to graduate in three years is a bad idea. It’s very rare for a freshman to arrive at a university knowing what he/she wants to do. Most college students don’t declare until the end of their sophomore years. When I matriculated, I knew a number of freshman who entered with enough credit hours to place them squarely in their sophomore years, but they didn’t have a clue what they wanted to study. They didn’t graduate any faster than anyone else because they spent their first two years shopping for a major. I also knew people who entered college supposedly knowing what they wanted to major in, but would later change to something else (some as late as in their senior year of college). Only rarely does a freshman enter college knowing what he/she wants to major and actually _sticks_ to that major. I was one such freshman, and I entered with 15 credit hours, but I still graduated in four years for another reason that makes this idea stupid: I was a physical science major. My major was so big that my colleagues who entered without credits had to take several 18-hour semesters and go to summer school just to finish in four years. And they had never changed majors. Now, while admittedly, there were parts of the overall curriculum that we all could have done without such as empty hours (to fill three hours, one of them took a cooking class) or first year language classes that don’t count towards the core, there are just some majors that are big enough that even a minimal core and starting from year one in that major results in graduating in four years. And yet, if you ask those majors what classes they could cut from their majors, they would be hard pressed to come up with one. And while there are some stupid and superfluous things that universities might make a student take, eliminating the core is not a good idea. The core is what makes a college education enriching and rewarding. Take that away and going to college would be nothing more than job training. Really, the idea of pushing students to graduate in three years is nothing more a reflection of our society’s over-emphasis of the economic benefits of a college education (that among other things). We’re thinking way to much of sending students to college so that they can get jobs when they graduate, rather than providing them with an enriching experience while they’re there. One of the good things I can say about academia is that it has continued to resist this trend (though, I note to my dismay, that its resistance is crumbling). And while I think that there’s substantial room to reform college education to more so meet the demands of today, I don’t think treating a college education like job training (or pushing students to graduate in three years) is the way to do it. We have some of the best universities in the world. Students from all over the world, including countries that have systems that this idea is trying to emulate (like England, France, and Germany), are coming _here_ to go to college. So, we must be doing something right.

    And by the way, implementing a three-year college system probably won’t reduce amount of time people spend in college, in any case. Students will still change majors, lengthening their time to graduation. And further specializing the majors will simply increase the number of people who go back to college later in life because they’ve decided to change careers. Except next time, tuition will be much more expensive.

    JS17 February 22, 2010 · 5:16 pm

    I am opposed to this idea, because I did it. I graduated in three years from an Ivy League school with a double major in 1978. The main reason was my parents pushing me to save money. Yes, I had AP credits, but I also went to summer school & took 18 credits a semester.

    The reason I think it’s wrong is because college is the magic time between youth and adulthood. You’re probably going to spend 50+ years working, so what’s the rush? Looking back, I missed many experiences with my friends and the time to mature, explore other possibilities, and grow. Saving a few bucks is not worth that loss, IMO.

    When I go to reunions, I cannot relate to half the stories people tell, and have far fewer contacts than others.

    Thirty years after college, I remember the experiences and relationships I had, not the course work. As I say, most of my learning took place outside the classroom, not inside.

    Another way I explain this is college is NOT like saving money for a down payment on a house: the sooner you save the $, the sooner you can move in.

    Instead, it’s like a trip to Paris: you don’t want to spend 5 minutes at Notre Dame, 5 minutes at the Eiffel Tower, 5 minutes along the Seine, etc., just to say you’ve been there. You need to savor it, take in the experiences, live it. You can’t experience Paris in an afternoon.

    You miss a great deal of life with only 3 years of college.
