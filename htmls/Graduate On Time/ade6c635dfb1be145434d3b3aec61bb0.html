UCF aims for higher graduation rates

Graduates show off their style with messages on their caps during… (Jacob Langston, Orlando…)
July 6, 2014 | By Denise-Marie Ordway, Orlando Sentinel

UCF finance major Tyler Wright is eager to graduate.

He started college with about 30 credit hours earned in high school through Advanced Placement courses and dual enrollment, but he still won't earn his degree until December 2015 — four-and-a-half years after he began.

"If I were able to graduate early, I would be able to get into my career faster," said Wright, 21, who hopes to work in sports marketing or wealth management.

The University of Central Florida recognizes that it takes most of its students more than four years to earn a bachelor's degree. And while the school has made progress in raising graduation rates in recent years, it is ramping up its efforts even more.

Among the new programs UCF will launch this fall is a campaign to get more students to take at least 30 credit hours a year.

Most bachelor's-degree programs require 120 credit hours. Many students take lighter course loads to make time for extracurricular activities, clubs and jobs, said Maribeth Ehasz, UCF's vice president for student development and enrollment services.

If students can commit to 30 credit hours a year, they will have a much better chance of graduating on time, Ehasz said.

"If that campaign works even a little, we're going to see the four-year [graduation] rate improve," she said.

In 2012-13, 41 percent of students who started and finished their studies at UCF graduated within four years — up from 35 percent in 2010-11, according to university reports.

The percentage of students who graduate in six years or less jumped 5 percentage points to 67 percent over the same time period. University of Florida and Florida State University are the only public universities doing better.

Last month, UCF President John Hitt announced what he called "ambitious" goals for helping more students stay in school. The university will raise the six-year graduation rate to 70 percent within three years, Hitt said.

UCF and other public universities are under a lot of pressure to help students get through school faster. State and national leaders want more Americans to have college degrees. But, also, the longer students are in school, the more they and taxpayers end up paying for their educations.

All 11 state universities recently submitted plans for boosting their graduation rates to the Board of Governors, which oversees the State University System of Florida. The board has begun awarding performance funding based partly on six-year graduation rates.

Florida Atlantic University, which had the worst six-year rate in 2012-13, aims to improve by 8 percentage points by the end of 2016-17. If successful, FAU will boost its rate to 48 percent.

Officials at UF and FSU said they expect gains of 1 percentage point and 3 percentage points, respectively, between 2012-13 and 2016-17. UF's six-year rate in 2012-13 was 86 percent while FSU's was 77 percent.

Another way UCF hopes to boost its graduation rates is by starting to use "predictive analytics" — a statistical technique that uses new technology to analyze large batches of student data that have been collected over time to predict student behavior.

Officials said the information will help the them identify students likely to need extra help. Academic advisers will reach out to those individuals to make sure they stay on track.

"We want them to succeed, and now we're focusing energy on developing specific interventions," Ehasz said.

UCF also has formed a partnership with the John H. Gardner Institute for Excellence in Undergraduate Education in North Carolina. Ehasz said that initiative is designed specifically to help UCF better understand the needs of students who transfer from other colleges and universities.

UCF takes in thousands of students from local community colleges through the DirectConnect to UCF program. But UCF has not made the same progress in improving the graduation rates of students who come to UCF after earning an associate degree somewhere else.

About 27 percent of those students finish a bachelor's degree within two years at UCF, according to the university. That represents a slight dip since 2010-11. About 66 percent finish in four years at UCF.

Valencia College student Jeyleen Silfa Salas, 18, said she has been discouraged by the graduation rates for transfer students reported by many universities. She hopes UCF's rate goes up because she wants to transfer in a year or so.

"It makes me feel kind of insecure about whether I'm going to be part of that percentage that isn't going to graduate," she said.
