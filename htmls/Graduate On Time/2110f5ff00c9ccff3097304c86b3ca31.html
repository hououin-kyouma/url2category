Teen to graduate high school with bachelor's degree, no college debt

Posted on May 22, 2016 at 8:33 AM
High school student graduates with a bachelor's from SVSU, following in sister's footsteps
4

Gallery: High school student graduates with a bachelor's from SVSU, following in sister's footsteps

By Bob Johnson

bob_johnson@mlive.com

KOCHVILLE TOWNSHIP, MI -- When Rachel Klammer finishes the last course she is currently enrolled in at Saginaw Valley State University, she will graduate with a bachelor's degree in literature.

Just a few weeks earlier, the 19-year-old Frankenmuth teen received her high school diploma.

She follows in the footsteps of her sister, Sarah, who a year ago accomplished the same feat of graduating high school while earning a 4-year college degree.

"It feels great that I'm done with undergrad," Rachel Klammer said. "It's kind of like I've just added four years to my life because all of my other friends are just starting college and I'm already starting my masters."

How did she and her sister do it?

Rachel Klammer, who describes herself as "sort of a nerd," began taking college classes at age 14 after enrolling in a program called the Paris Academy of Co-Secondary Education, or PACE.

PACE is a dual enrollment program that allows students of any age to attend college or career-technical/CTE classes. Students can attend full or part-time, in some cases tuition-free, completing classes that fulfill requirements of both the high school diploma and college degree, according to founder Nancy Paris.

PACE is offered at Academic and Career Education Academy, a charter school in Midland. 

"They would enroll in the academy," Paris said. "They take college classes and earn credits. If a student is ambitious like Rachel, it's possible to complete college while attending high school."

Klammer attended the program tuition-free. Paris said the academy makes it possible by using state per pupil funding to cover college and high school costs.

"There is no cost to the student to attend the college," Paris said. "The school pays the tuition to the college."

Klammer had to take one online class through the PACE program and every other class she took was at Delta College or SVSU, getting rides from family members to the campuses, her mother said.

Klammer said her drive to attend college early began in middle school.

"I didn't think I was intellectually challenged in middle school, so I thought I was wasting my time," she said. "My mom said 'Hey, how about this?' and I said I would give it a try.

"I was worried about the classes, but they turned out to be about the same."

According to J.J. Boehm, spokesman for SVSU, Klammer is enrolled in the final course she needs to complete her degree. She participated in SVSU's commencement on May 7.

"It is a unique accomplishment," Boehm said about Klammer's graduation.

Related: Frankenmuth teen graduates SVSU with no college debt

Now, all four Klammer siblings have college degrees, though not all debt free through PACE, according to their mother Lynn Klammer. She said she is happy for her two daughters to not have a huge "anchor of debt hanging around their neck" while trying to start their careers.

"It's just incredible, the burden on families, first of all, and then the burden on students who have to worry about paying off all of that money before they could earn enough money to pay it back," Lynn Klammer said.

"I went to college and was able to pay for it working summer jobs. I didn't have any money when I finished school but I didn't have any debt."

Lynn Klammer said she found out about PACE when she did some research after seeing a commercial.

"I found out there were over 200 early college program across the nation," she said.

Some kind of genius

Rachel Klammer spent the majority of her high school years on a college campus, but it didn't stop her from hanging out with her friends or going to the prom.

"We still hung out and went to games," she said. "Although football is kind of boring."

On SVSU's campus, Klammer said she was treated like an adult since few knew she was still in high school.

Klammer would bring cupcakes to school for her birthday and sometimes would be asked how old she turning. When they found out, they would usually jokingly tease her, she said.

"People would be like, 'Oh my God, you're like 2. Are you some kind of genius?' Klammer said.

Klammer said she doesn't think she is a genius at all.

"I don't think that what we have done was so amazing," she said. "Other people are going to do it, it's just that other people did not have the same opportunity."

According to Paris and Klammer's mom, Rachel Klammer was accepted into a master's program at Central Michigan University, which will be fully funded by the university, as well as a teaching assistant position.

"It'll help me become a teacher or get into another profession," she said.

Klammer said she is thankful for being provided the opportunity from PACE and SVSU.

"They are wonderful people for taking this time to make this work for me," she said. "I can't thank them enough for what they did."
