On business: Job market best in years for South Carolina college grads

  • By John McDermott jmcdermott@postandcourier.com

    John McDermott

  • May 6, 2017
  • (…)

Thousands of career-bound college students in South Carolina will walk in caps and gowns to collect their hard-earned degrees this month. They'll also be stepping into the most inviting job market in years.

“It’s broad as well as deep,” said Tom Halasz, director of the Career Center at the University of South Carolina in Columbia, where about 5,000 undergraduate and graduate students were expected to receive their academic sheepskins this weekend.

"As the state has recovered, the employment opportunities have rebounded ...  in just about every area," Halasz added.  

A recent CareerBuilder survey backs him up. About 74 percent of U.S. employers plan to hire recent college graduates in 2017, up from 64 percent a year ago. The outlook is the most bullish since 2007, just before the last recession kicked in.

It was a tough slog for newly minted college grads during and after the downturn. The young turks found themselves scrambling to compete against more-experienced applicants for fewer job openings. More than a few elected to stay in school in the hopes of waiting out the storm.

"But after a long and sluggish recovery, young graduates’ economic prospects have finally begun to look up," according to a new study from left-leaning Economic Policy Institute. "Sustained improvements in economic conditions in recent years have brightened young graduates’ job prospects for employment and wage growth, particularly for those graduating from college." 

It's much the same in South Carolina, where the labor market has tightened considerably. The jobless rate is now hovering at its lowest point in more than 15 years, coming at 4.4 percent in March. Recent figures suggest that the strong demand for workers is pushing wages higher, though the income needle hasn't moved much when viewed on a per-capita basis.

At USC, Halasz said about 44 percent of the class of 2017 who responded to a survey said they had landed full-time jobs as of last week. Another 22 percent aren't going to work, opting for graduate school instead.  

Not surprisingly, the information technology field is among the hottest markets. This year, according to Halasz, job seekers appear to be getting an extra boost from President Donald Trump. 

"Some of the employers in that industry are looking to hire U.S. residents and citizens because of anticipated changes in immigration policy," he said. "The employers that have openings are recruiting very hard to make sure they can fill those positions." 

Engineering graduates and health majors, such as budding nurses and occupational therapists, also are in high demand. 

"Those folks are doing fine," he said.

And the surging manufacturing sector in South Carolina is fueling demand for workers schooled in specialized support services, such as supply chain management. 

Brandon Clark, who has worked with job-seeking students at Clemson University, Bob Jones University and Presbyterian College, among others, has witnessed the pent-up demand for college-educated candidates firsthand.

"It seems a lot of them now have jobs lined up as they get out of school," said Clark, vice president of of the Greenville office of the staffing firm Robert Half International.

Neil Burton, executive director of Clemson's Center for Career and Professional Development, said he's not seeing "a tremendous amount of churn" among the Upstate university's most popular undergraduate programs, such as engineering and various business disciplines.

Computer science, on the other hand, "is going like gangbusters," he said.

The job market also is improving for would-be teachers.

"We had an event for education majors and we had 60 different schools represented. I can remember four or five years ago we had 19, so that's big," Burton said.

The latest wrinkle for college career counselors is what he called "iGen" students. Mostly technology-savvy, they're increasingly seeking advice about how to venture out on their own after graduation rather than cut their teeth old-school-style at an established nine-to-five employer. 

"We're getting a fair number of students saying that. ...  They go out and try stuff. They're brave and they're bold," Burton said. 

It's the same story down on the coast, said Jim Allison, executive director of the Career Center at the College of Charleston.

He's hearing from more seniors who are interested in working at tiny startups or launching their own businesses. Flexibility is often a high priority for this demographic.  

"Without question, entrepreneurship is coming up more and more .... at a higher rate than in the past," Allison said.

It all makes the job of a college career counselor more interesting. And more challenging.

"It's harder ... to merge employers' needs and students' needs because they're so divergent right now," Allison said.
