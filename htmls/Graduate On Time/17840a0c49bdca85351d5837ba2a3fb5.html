If you’re thinking about going back to school or enrolling for the first time, but wondering whether or not to get an online degree, take heart that determining what’s right for you is actually quite simple!

We at College Choice know the ins and outs of online education, and we are eager to point you toward the best possible academic fit.

Are online degrees respected?

The short answer is, yes and no.

Some degree programs—namely those that are regionally accredited and allow credits to be easily transferred in (and out!)—are highly reputable. Likewise, some online degrees have been in place for over a decade and have gone to great lengths to earn their renowned reputation.

For a list of such schools, see below. Then there are those offered through for-profit schools, are nationally accredited, and have not been in place for very long. Keep your eyes out for those shaky programs.

Should I get an online degree?

Online degrees are an affordable, flexible option for some people, especially working adults who juggle many responsibilities. There are dozens, if not hundreds, of fantastic online degree programs that are, at once, highly regarded and affordable.

For example, you can earn a degree from renowned Penn State while living and working from anywhere in the country. However, this means Penn State football games, student social opportunities, campus lectures, etc., will not be part of your college experience.

Are online degrees easier? More affordable? Faster?

Because schools must offer the same curriculum to their online students, it’s not easier to get an online degree. Added challenges include a difference of technical and academic support as an online student.

However, many programs offer deep cuts on tuition rates for their online students (these vary by school), as well as the option to expedite your degree, earning it a full year (or more!) before “traditional” students. Again, this varies by school.

What do employers really think about online degrees? Can I get a job with an online degree?

Here’s the truth. Employers care much more about advanced education than whether you took classes online, and most won’t even know because your diploma lists only the school name and degree granted.

Job placement is never a guarantee after graduation, but online education is definitely not a hindrance. And advanced education is a guaranteed strength on your resume!

How are online degrees different than “traditional” degrees?

Actually, the differences are minimal, and that which is different points to the strengths of online education. That is, online learning lends itself toward flexibility, affordability, and adaptability.

The difference between the two kinds of learning is mostly in “the college experience.” That is, you won’t have access to the kind of social life you would if you took classes on campus.

However, “traditional” degrees don’t emphasize the self-driven nature of online degrees, a huge asset in all industries.

How do online colleges work?

This really depends on the school itself, whether students are required to login for lectures, to test at exam centers, to supplement their online education with practicums in their local community, etc.

However, most schools subscribe to a student-centered approach to education, stressing flexibility and independence. Online students complete coursework at their own pace, logging into the school’s online infrastructure to access services, lectures, assignments, virtual discussion, and much more.

How do I spot a good online college or university?

This one is super important. Here’s what to look for in online education, regardless of what degree you are pursuing:

  • A highly reputable, regional accreditation. Though some national accredited schools still provide great programs, regional accreditation points to high academic standards.
  • A school that accepts prior educational credits and, perhaps even more importantly, allows students to transfer credits if they choose to switch programs later.
  • Solid stats when it comes to graduation, retention, and student loan default rates. You can find all over at U.S. News & World Report.
  • Can boast a breadth of services to their online students, from academic advising to technical help, financial guidance, and faculty accessibility.

What Are the Best Online Colleges?

In our 2017 Rankings of the Best Online Colleges, we account for factors like the number of online degrees offered, academic reputation, affordability, scholarly quality of the faculty teaching online courses, engaging methods of teaching, and technological resources.

In this list, we don’t consider associate’s degrees or certificates, only undergraduate, graduate, and post-graduate degrees. Occasionally, our list includes hybrid programs as well.

Our data came from the National Center for Educational Statistics, from the websites of the universities themselves, and publications like U.S. News and World Report.
