61% of state’s full-time college students graduate

Share this:

  • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
  • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
  • Click to share on Reddit (Opens in new window)
  • Click to email this to a friend (Opens in new window)
  • Click to print (Opens in new window)
By Orange County Register
September 28, 2011 at 8:09 am

California trails national graduation rates by a slim margin, with both rates falling below 61 percent even when students take eight years to complete a four-year degree, according to a new report on college completion.

The report, released Wednesday by Complete College America, a nonprofit focused on increasing college graduation rates, tracked higher education students in 33 states with data provided by state governors. It is the first report to take track both full- and part-time students working to complete certificate, associates and bachelor in order to understand and evaluate the challenges they face.

The federal government only tracks full-time student success, yet 40 percent of college students attend part time and are far less likely to graduate, even if they have twice the time. Only a quarter of students are full time, live on campus, with parents who pay for most of the tuition. The study showed that 75 percent of students juggle work, school and family while commuting to classes.

“We set out to tell the whole story, the story that college students today are living, not the story of a system that doesn’t count nearly half the students it serves,” Complete College America President Stan Jones said in a press release.

If the nation hopes to improve graduation rates, “we must see every student,” the report said.

Nationwide, less than half of African American, Hispanic, low-income students and those over 25 years old complete their degrees in 150 percent of the time it should take to complete a four-year degree. Taking more time doesn’t increase the likelihood of graduation – rates slow because life often gets in the way and degrees get put on the back burner, the report said.

Nationally, 60.6 percent of full-time students graduate in eight years. In California, the figure is 60.2 percent.

In California, only 14 of every 100 full-time students graduate from a four-year public university on time; after eight years, when part-time students are included, only 59 of 100 had graduated, with two of those attending part time.

The average time it takes a full-time student to complete a four-year degree is 5.2 years; part-time students need an additional five months. This is slightly higher than the national average of 4.7 and 5.6 years, respectively.

California will have to fill a 31 percent skills gap by 2020, when an estimated 67 percent of the state’s jobs will require a certificate or degree, according to the report.

Because states opted into the survey, the report depicts only a slice of California’s college students – only the Cal State system provided information to Complete College America. Additional information on total enrollment and graduation rates by campus were gathered from the U.S. Department of Education’s NCES Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System. The California Community College system is working on gathering data for the commission, which aims to update the report, according to Chief Financial Officer Mike Baumgartner.

In 2002, California had the second-highest number of students working toward their bachelor’s degrees, but only about half completed degrees in six years. California has the highest on-time graduation rate for transfer students at 62.7 percent.

The state enrolls the second highest number of Pell grant students in the nation, and more than two-thirds of those students need remediation, what the report calls the “Bermuda Triangle of higher education” because students in need of remediation are far less likely to complete their degrees.

To solve the graduation problem, the report recommends additional online classes and predictable block schedules that run yearround so students can expedite their degrees. Embedding remediation into courses and creating more peer learning networks are also recommended.

UC Irvine has the fourth-highest, six-year state graduation rate for California four-year public universities at 82 percent; Cal State Fullerton has a graduation rate of 52 percent. UC Berkeley is first at 90 percent, with Cal State LA’s graduation rate of 34 percent in last, according to 2009-2010 statistics from the U.S. Department of Education.

The Cal State system admits a vast range of students, some who need remedial work, while others are qualified to attend Ivy Leagues, said Ed Trotter, Cal State Fullerton’s acting associate vice president for undergraduate affairs. These students also come with a wide range of family experiences – variables that affect their performance at the university.

For example, some may be transfers or first-generation college students who are unsure of what to expect in college, while others are English language learners or students supporting families, said Ed Sullivan, CSUF assistant vice president of institutional research and analytical studies.

Creating a campus community is also important, especially at a commuter university, and to provide services that fill what might be a lack of family experience in higher education, Sullivan said. The university is also working to build a collegiate culture through partnerships with high schools and community colleges, he said.

Fullerton’s retention rate has increased 10 percentage points in the last two years, bringing it into the 80s, which will be reflected in the university’s graduation rates in another two years, Trotter said.

UC Irvine calculates the number of seats each student will need based on their major requirements to ensure that students can get the classes they need to graduate, said Sharon Salinger, dean of undergraduate education. Class sizes have also increased to accommodate a larger freshman class.

“We know who the students are when they come in, we know what majors they’ve selected, so units literally predict the number of seats they’re going to require,” she said. “It’s really about every program ensuring that they’re offering the courses required.”

The four-year graduation rate at UCI has increased in the past few years, which may be because students want to avoid paying for an extra year of tuition, she said. The proportion of students enrolled in summer school has slightly increased as well, Salinger said.

The university monitors different student populations to ensure they’re on track, and is particularly concerned about veterans graduating on time because they have a controlled amount financial aid from the federal government as well as transfer students.
