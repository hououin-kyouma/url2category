Less than half of R.I. public college students graduate in 4 years

Feb 7, 2014 at 10:03 PM

Lynn Arditi Journal staff writer lynnarditi

If your son or daughter is enrolled in a four-year public college in Rhode Island, and you�re counting on graduation within four years, you may be disappointed.

At the University of Rhode Island, the four-year graduation rate in 2013 was 44 percent � the second-lowest of any state university in New England. The University of Maine had the lowest rate, at 33 percent.

�The good news is that Rhode Island is no worse than the national average,� said Richard K. Vedder, director of The Center for College Affordability and Productivity, a Washington-based nonprofit research center. �The bad news is the national average is horrible.�

Nationwide, about half of all full-time students enrolled at private colleges and only 32 percent of those at state colleges or universities graduate in four years, according to the National Center for Education Statistics.

At predominantly commuter schools, the average is even lower. At Rhode Island College, where 85 percent of the students commute, the four-year graduation rate last spring fell to 13 percent, the lowest in five years. The national average for peer schools is 14 percent, according to data compiled by RIC.

Educators cite myriad reasons for graduation delays, from lack of college preparedness to the pressure on students to earn money while in college that takes time away from their studies.

Now, with national attention focused on improving the nation�s �college completion� rates, Rhode Island educators are devising new strategies to keep students on track. And they say they�re seeing some encouraging signs.

Donald H. DeHayes, URI�s provost and vice president for academic affairs, said the university is �aggressively working� to improve its four-year graduation rate, while maintaining its commitment to its mission.

One major reason why URI�s four-year graduation rate is lower than those of its New England peers, DeHayes said, is because it serves a larger proportion of students from disadvantaged backgrounds.

Among them are the 300 to 330 students each year who enroll in URI�s �talent development� program, designed to increase access of minority and disadvantaged applicants to higher education. The four-year graduation rate for students in that program last year, he said, was 23 percent.

�When you look at Vermont and New Hampshire,� DeHayes said, �they are very heavily recruiting very wealthy out-of-state students, with a much lower proportion of in-state students.�

URI graduates also include a high proportion of transfer students � about 22 percent � who are not counted in the four-year graduation rate, DeHayes said.

In the last four years, URI has begun using a software program called an �early alert system� to identify students in trouble before they drop out.

In addition to its regular academic advisers, the university now deploys �intrusive advising� techniques such as texting students to check in with them and remind them to stop in as part of the university�s new �College for Academic Success.�

URI also has shortened its period for adding and dropping classes � from eight weeks to three weeks � to discourage students from dropping classes and winding up with fewer than the necessary 15 credits per semester needed to graduate.

Rhode Island College is working with more than 200 students to explore barriers to completion, said Holly Shadoian, assistant vice president of academic affairs at RIC.

RIC has also redesigned its general education program to include first-year seminars aimed at engaging students. The classes are limited to 20 students and taught by full-time faculty.

The seminars are part of the college�s effort, now in its fourth year, Shadoian said, to improve the retention rate of first-year students.

�When you improve retention,� she said, �you improve the graduation rate.�

The efforts are showing early signs of success.

The retention rate for RIC students who entered in the fall of 2012 rose 2 percent from the previous year � the biggest uptick in more than a decade, Shadoian said.

And at URI, 65 percent of all freshmen last year completed 30 credits, setting them on track to graduate in four years. That�s up from 45 percent in 2005.

�We should see that in a couple of years that turns into higher graduation rates,� DeHayes said.
