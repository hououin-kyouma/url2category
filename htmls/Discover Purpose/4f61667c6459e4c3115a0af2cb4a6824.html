Passion, Purpose and Meaningful Careers: Think Big, Start Small

Most of us want to find purpose and meaning in our work lives. Type “How to find my purpose in life” into Google, and you’ll get over 260 million results.

There are scores of lifehacks and techniques and tests claiming to help us figure out who we are, and what we’re meant to do- but they won’t do the trick for you.

As a former monk, Jay Shetty knows a little something about the search for meaning – and as a motivational speaker and corporate coach, he’s seen firsthand how that search is made easier with a little guidance and support.

According to Jay, finding and living with purpose is key to both organizations and to the people working within them… but we make finding and living our purpose far more difficult than it really is.

In prep for his talk during #impaqt fest this fall, we sat down with Jay to find out what purpose really is, how he suggest you go about finding it, and how we can incorporate our individual purpose into our working lives.

 

 

How do you define purpose, and why is it so important?

 

Purpose is why we do what we do. It’s important because without knowing our why, we can’t find motivation and meaning and innovation.

Dr. Martin Luther King said “If you have nothing to die for, then you have nothing to live for.”

I think that sums up what purpose really is. It gives you meaning, it gives you motivation, it gives you pride, it gives you passion – it’s actually the root of all other positive emotions, having a purpose.

I think that’s why today, so many people feel lost, so many people feel confused, so many people don’t like what they do, so many people feel stuck. They don’t have a purpose, or didn’t know that they could have one; without purpose, their life loses meaning, loses a message, loses passion, loses motivation and drive.

We’re all searching for a lot of things – happiness, money, success – but I think the most important thing we can have is purpose and meaning, because that’s where it all begins.

 

How do we discover what our purpose is?

 

I don’t think finding your purpose is like doing a test online, or finding out your personality type. I don’t think it’s something you do for 3 years, like a degree, and then at the end of it there it is, there’s your purpose. I don’t think there’s a formula or a strategy. But I do believe there’s a process.

When I’m working with people, I ask them what advice they would give their younger self; if you could speak to your 10-yr-old self, what would you tell to them to do? What have you stopped doing, that you loved when you were 10?

The second question I ask people is: if we fast-forward to when you are 80 or 90 years old on your deathbed, what are you going to regret that you didn’t do? Not what you regret that you did do, what are you going to regret that you didn’t do?

What we find is that the answers to these specific questions start telling us what we have to do now.

So if I were to put this into a process, Step 1 would be to spend some time reflecting on what you loved doing as a child: What came naturally to you? What you were naturally good at? Forget about money, forget about a job, forget about how you’re going to pay the bills – just think about what you were naturally inclined towards as a young person, that you were naturally talented at – music, language, communication, math, science, whatever it was. Think about it.

Once you figure that out, Step 2 is to try doing some of those activities again. Take an online course, take an in-person course, go to a workshop – there are so many ways of testing whether you like something. Go out and do those activities. Go do what you think it is that you like.

A lot of people say follow your passion – forget that. Don’t follow your passion. Invest in it.

And then finally, once you’ve done that, you’ll start to realize what level you are at now with your passion. Sometimes you realize you’re really good at it, sometimes you realize you’re not so great at it, which is fine. And that’s when you move to Step 3: you decide whether it’s going to remain a passion, or become a purpose.

I often say your passion is for you, and your purpose is for others. Your purpose is when you use your passion to serve other people.

You have to become an expert at your passion so that you can offer it to other people, serve other people through whatever it may be.

 

Money, especially at the beginning of our careers, can be an obstacle to investing in our purpose – we have to pay the bills. With that in mind, how do we find the best job or role or company to work for where we can bring or explore our purpose?

 

I think the problem is that we start with boxes in mind, we start with containers. We say: “Am I going to be a doctor? Am I going to be a lawyer? Am I going to be in business?” That’s actually the problem – we’re trying to fit purpose, which is an infinite, amazing abundant thing, into a career that’s been invented so that we and other people can make money.

So when you start expecting purpose in your job, that’s a big mistake because very few companies, very few roles were created with the goal of making someone happy, or giving their life meaning.

It’s not anyone else’s job to tell you what your purpose is, and really they can’t. So it’s important to find it out for yourself by doing the work, and then applying it to work – whatever you realize is your purpose outside of work, bring that to work by trying to fill that role.

Purpose is less about doing your purpose as a job – it’s more about what environment you flourish in. If you’re a leader outside of work, try and lead a society or club at work; if you’re great at analytics outside of work, bring analytics inside your work; if you’re someone who’s great at organizing social functions, bring that to the workspace.

Bring your personality and your purpose into the workplace in a social sense rather than expecting there to be a business angle straight away. Find out who you are and bring that into the workplace – if you do that people will actually notice you more.

 

When you feel you’re the only one wanting to change things at your company, where do you start?

 

I think what’s stopping us from bringing our purpose to work is that we’re not confident in being ourselves at the workplace. Sometimes the work environment can be tricky to navigate – you’ve got lots of relationships there that are dependant on other things. You’re being watched and evaluated by certain performance metrics.

So, you start by creating something outside of work that you’re passionate about – whether it’s a community project, philanthropy, whatever it may be – you start by exercising and building something externally, and then find other people who also do the same thing. You’ll be shocked when you realize that there are people just like you at different companies.

I think that sometimes, we feel that our purpose is only ours and we’re alone in it, but there’s a lot more people out there who feel the same as we do. Find them. When you do that, you’ll get more confidence about bringing that passion and purpose into work.

 

Corporations often get a bad rap, but some people are opening up to the idea that big business can solve big problems and be a force for good. Is that genuine, or just PR?

 

I think that a lot of companies, with the leaders that they have right now, genuinely believe they can make a meaningful change, and there is a genuine desire to help. The majority of leaders I’ve worked with are very humble, they want to do good, they have the right values.

What we as millennials have to remember that these leaders didn’t have that sort of ethical guidance and leadership themselves – they had what was passed down through the hierarchy, they were just part of the conveyor belt system. Now people are conscious – they’re waking up.

So in my experience, it is real. There are people in organizations who want to care for people, take care of people.

 

If you feel like you’re missing your ‘tribe’ at work – like-minded people – is it because you’re at the wrong organization or are you looking in the wrong places?

 

If you’re not finding similar-minded people at your organization, it’s probably because you haven’t been open about who you are and what you want. When you genuinely and authentically share who you are, the people who are the same or similar will gravitate towards you.

Some companies who have done amazing things to help this happen. I was reading a study the other day that found when you sit next to people who are in different roles than you, productivity goes up by 10-20%. Google has a rooftop garden for employees, and they always put your allotment next to someone who works in a completely different department, because they want you to talk to each other so you can come up with more innovative ideas.

These types of things show that there’s a need for us to accelerate our learning by going out there, meeting people, and making more of an effort, rather than always sitting with the same 5 people everyday and very likely becoming the average of those 5 people.

A big part of my message is: don’t wait for anyone to find your purpose, to find your talent, to spot you and think you’re going to be the next great whatever it is. Don’t expect anyone to find your purpose for you – take responsibility to find it and to find those like-minded people in your organization.

 

There is so much going on in the world ecologically, politically – are these positive drivers for people wanting to make a change, or is it better not to focus too much on the old or the problems and just create the new?

 

Just starting to do something is the most important thing. The people who are doing great things are so busy doing that they don’t have time to criticize anyone else.

If you’re not doing anything, you have a lot of time on your hands to point fingers, find fault, complain, vent – it doesn’t change anything, you’re not solving the old or creating the new.

I recommend people be a part of growth. If you know what you want to grow or change, do that; if you don’t, join someone you’re inspired by. Sitting around complaining about what’s happening in the world isn’t solved by us complaining about it.

And it can be something small, it doesn’t have to be global. So many of us think that change has to be big, and that stops us from making small changes – but change is local and global.

One person making a change impacts the world because that person is in the world. All of our actions have an effect.

We tend to forget that everything big started out small. When we all started using Facebook it was already big, but it was very small when it started. Because we’ve never been trained to scale things, and because we’ve always been told to get things right the first time, we’re fearful.

But again, think back to being children. When a child falls over and gets up 50 times, they don’t say ‘oh god, I don’t think walking is for me’ and give up.

When we were children, we took small steps and we fell down and then we started walking. It’s no different.
