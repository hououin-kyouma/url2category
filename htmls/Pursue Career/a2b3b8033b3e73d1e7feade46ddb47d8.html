Ariel Hyatt
Founder at Cyber PR & Cyber PR Music

@CyberPR

Introduce yourself to our audience. Tell us who you are and what you are currently focused on.

I’m Ariel Hyatt and I have been an entrepreneur for 20 years. I run Cyber PR, a social media and content strategy firm based in New York. We work with female entrepreneurs garnering digital PR, and guiding them through establishing and strengthening their online brands. I have just released my fourth book, Crowdstart: The Ultimate Guide to a Powerful and Profitable Crowdfunding Campaign, which provides a clear, actionable roadmap that helps entrepreneurs figure out who’s in their crowd and how to marshal that support into an effective crowdfunding campaign.

What has been the biggest challenge in your career to date?

Understanding the success treadmill and how to get on and get off of it with ease, elegance and grace. If you spend too much time on it you will very quickly forget why you do what you do in the first place. If you stay off of it you will lose your gravitas and place in your industry – it’s a fine line.

What was your biggest career breakthrough moment?

Realizing that you don’t really have a breakthrough “moment.” It’s all made up on tiny (and some big) moments. In our society we only seem to celebrate millionaires and billionaires and people who have made quantum leaps. However, I have found after 20 years of running a successful business that it’s not about massive moments. It's about jumping from precipice to precipice where you learn, relearn, and keep discovering who you are, who you are here to help, and re-framing those passions powerfully and constructively. That is what has enabled me to have a successful sustained career.

Finish this sentence: "I knew I had 'made it' when…"

I was on my first all expenses paid international flight on my way to speak at a conference (Reykjavik) a few weeks after I self-published my first book.

If you could go back and talk to your younger self (before your career really began), what professional advice would you give her?

I would tell myself this: Not everybody has to like me. I spent a lot of unnecessary stress and upset worrying about clients who were unsatisfied even after I had given it 110% (this is the nature of PR – you will always have unsatisfied clients.

I think for woman this lesson is hard. We are supposed to be likable, otherwise we are called bitchy or bossy and that doesn’t feel good. But think about this: How many of your male colleagues aren’t loved by all, and how much more success are they experiencing?

There will be people who are jealous or threatened or just not into you and your ideas - and social media can compound this because it’s a landscape filled with people who just can’t wait to give their feedback – not all of which could possibly be positive. I would tell myself: don’t focus on them. Instead focus on how you help and serve.

Share your two cents about money. What lessons have you learned about money along the way?

Two things — First: “Do what you love and the money will follow,” isn’t true. If you can align how you make money with doing what you love and at the same time focus on your business and your sales, then the money will “follow.”

Second: Your passion is not really what people pay you for.

Turns out, we have been sold a bit of a false bill of goods about following our passions. Think about it for a second: what are you really passionate about? When I started out, I thought I was following my passion for music but, it turned out I am a very good communicator, I’m great at social media strategy, publicity and marketing, and all of this things actually had nothing to do with my “passion.” I just happened to be working in the music business, so I mistakenly thought that I was aligning my passion with my career. The best book I’ve ever read on this subject is called “So Good They Can’t Ignore You.” it changed my perspective about work and why and how we strive for satisfaction around our careers.

What is your secret to success?

It’s a combination of staying in alignment with my purpose, grit and true desire to make a difference. I believe that it is vital to share our gifts and talents no matter how big or small, but you need to check in with yourself and make sure your reasons stay fresh. If they go stale and you feel yourself slipping out of gratitude it’s time to look at that and shift. I also think that if you are not giving back to your industry or your community in some way, success will also feel like it is eluding you. I also, with age, have realized that every few years your purpose shifts. The secret to success is realigning with your purpose every so often so that you can carry on with the same level of enthusiasm.

Why are you a member of Ellevate?

I love women – we just get sh*t done. We network really well, we support each other in incredible ways and we contribute much differently than our male counterparts. This fills me with joy. I also find it fascinating to meet women who are outside of my industry and network with both older and younger women.
