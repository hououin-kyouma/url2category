Published on Dec 13, 2016

At College Recruiter, we believe that every student and recent grad deserves a great career.
It’s no surprise that foreign language skills are coveted by employers. But do you know why? There are some interesting reasons that may be a surprise, and it may make you want to reconsider that Spanish class.
B. Max Dubroff works at Einfluss, an HR advisory firm. He says he has “hired people who were not as well-qualified as other candidates because they knew another language, even if that language was not specifically needed in my organization.”
Why? For starters, if you’ve had to study that language (as opposed to learning at home with your family), you’re demonstrating initiative and passion for self-improvement.
Max says, “Throughout my career, language has helped me make and strengthen relationships. I have never had a job where language was required, but I have always had everyday events that benefited from this understanding. I was twice selected for awesome opportunities in the Air Force and my understanding of German was a factor in me being chosen over other very well-qualified people.”
Whether your language skills are due to studying hard, or your family being bilingual, your skills say something about your regard for others.
Max says, “In many ways, language is the study of interactions; if you value language, you are also more likely to value other people,”. This is not lost on employers who need you to work well with others. Studying a foreign language creates more positive attitudes and less prejudice toward people who are different.
Simply put, if you have language skills, you stand out among your peers. While it may not be listed in a job ad, the value of foreign language skills can be a “signal” for recruiters to pay attention to you.
Bettyjo Bouchey is an Associate Professor and Program Director of Undergraduate Business Programs at National Louis University. She says that signal tells “a future employer that this candidate has an interest and sensitivity to multicultural issues and is interested in developing themselves outside of their primary vocational aspiration. It is a signal of a well-rounded prospective employee; someone that is interesting.”
As organizations become more global, language ability is crucial to recent college grads seeking to advance in their career. For example, company X is considering a potential partnership with a firm in France, so they consider employees who know French.
But if you have knowledge of a language, be prepared to prove it. Your interviewer may bring someone in who can listen to your skills and evaluate your abilities.
How should you list language skills on your resume and cover letter? Don’t just list a language. Explain how your language skills have helped you evolve as a person, or solve a specific problem. Word to the wise: It is best to describe the level of capability without overstating it. A person who says they are “fluent” but has never used it outside the classroom loses credibility.
For high proficiency, use words like “native” and “bilingual”. In the middle, use words like “professional working proficiency” or “conversant”. For beginner skills, say “elementary” or “novice”.
Employers value foreign language skills. As a college student or a recent grad, you should be sure to use that to your advantage when applying for internships and jobs.
