Getting a Government Job After College Experience

by Audra Bianca

Think of your dream -- finding a government job after college -- as different than landing a job in the business world. There are some commonalities between the hiring practices of public and private employers, but public employers must achieve a high degree of transparency in hiring. Expect bureaucratic procedures to make the hiring process longer than, for example, the entire process for a local company. If you don't ensure that your application meets all requirements of a public job, you won't make it through to the interview.

Knowledge, Skills and Abilities

If you've ever researched federal jobs, you know the U.S. Office of Personnel Management offers many guidelines for federal hiring. However, there are practices specific to each federal agency, and you should learn them before completing a federal job application. Ensure that every area of knowledge, skill or ability acquired in your college program is highlighted effectively on a resume and that you describe relevant experience in detail on an application's supplemental questions. The application is usually completed online.

Internships

Once you're determined to find a government position, confront the reality that it's a difficult job market and some agencies are reducing their workforce. While working at jobs such as being a barista at the local coffee bar, some graduates are getting ready for government jobs through unpaid internships with their target agencies. The internship may offer valuable experience and the chance to interact with people in powerful roles who can help you get a job. Be careful about what duties you accept because federal rules limit what you can do without compensation.

Pathways to Jobs

Government agencies may post pathways to getting a full-time job for students currently in high school or post-secondary school. It's important to use the websites of the agencies you want to work for to identify opportunities that you can pursue while in school, including paid internships, and within a few years following college graduation. The U.S. government has a Recent Graduates program to which you can apply within two years of completing an educational program. Veterans can get up to six years after they complete their degree program. The program provides mentorship and training and may lead to a permanent position.

Personal Considerations

It's necessary to prepare personally for public service. Government agencies look for candidates who can fit into their culture and maintain long-term employment. They want candidates with good recommendations, no criminal background and who make good choices in their community and online. Government jobs may require security clearances, financial background checks, physical exams, aptitude tests or other screenings that will bring aspects of your personal life under scrutiny.
