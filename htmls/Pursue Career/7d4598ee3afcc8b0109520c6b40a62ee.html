Finding a job after college is still a struggle

  • By SEKIA MANGUM Staff Writer

    Dan Grote

  • Jan 12, 2015
  • (…)

Two years after graduating from college in 2005, Melissa Ford entered the world of show business and had a set career in her chosen field — theater management and production.

But shortly after the recession, Ford, 31, of Brigantine, lost her job and started waiting tables at Angeloni’s II in Atlantic City. Even off the stage, the 5-foot-6 brunette maintains her “animated and intense personality,” she said.

“As much as it plays to the stereotype of theater people, serving tables is the closest thing I get to ‘putting on a show’ each day,” said Ford. “And hey, the better I entertain and please the customer, the better my tip.”

Even though job prospects are improving, many college graduates are still struggling to land a job in their field. The overall underemployment rate is 6.7 percent, according to the Economic Policy Institute. But because job opportunities remain scarce, underemployment is at 16.8 percent for workers younger than 25 as of May.

Dan Crawford, EPI media relations specialist, defines underemployment as people being jobless and hunting for work; working part time because they can’t find a full-time job; or who want a job, have looked within the past year but have given up searching.

Ocean City native Ryan Clark also is experiencing the underemployment blues. Clark, 24, studied education at The College of New Jersey and has wanted to become a teacher since graduating in spring 2013.

“I’ve always been pretty good at public speaking and teaching people multiple things, so majoring in education seemed promising,” Clark said. “I was offered a part-time job in mid-November as a substitute teacher in Ocean City and Upper Township schools, but I didn’t have a set schedule.”

It might be hard for some college grads to believe that overall, the job market is improving.

In September, the U.S. Department of Commerce reported that job growth averaged more than 200,000 positions a month in the first nine months of 2014 and the gross domestic product grew at an annual rate of 4.6 percent during the same period, replacing the agency’s previous reading of 2.4 percent in 2013.

Meanwhile, Friday’s report from the Labor Department showed employers added 252,000 jobs in December and 50,000 more in October and November combined than the government had previously estimated. The unemployment rate dropped to 5.6 percent from 5.8 percent in November. The rate is now at its lowest point since 2008.

“Young workers always face higher unemployment rates than the overall workforce. If we want to make sure young people have strong job opportunities, we should pursue policies that lower the unemployment rate and raise wages across the board,” Crawford said.

Prior to graduation, Ford landed a job on Broadway as assistant stage manager for “Tony n’ Tina’s Wedding” and toured nationally. In 2009, she became the company manager for “Finnegan’s Wake” at the Showboat Casino Hotel in Atlantic City until the company canceled her contract. Ford was not financially stable enough to leave the area and jump back into theater, so she was left with no other option than to wait tables in 2011.

“I was hoping to do entertainment with Revel Casino in Atlantic City before it shut down, so I had to keep waiting tables,” Ford said. “I barely make enough money to pay bills and eat.”

Clark had a tough time finding a job after receiving his bachelor’s degree. He went back to being a lifeguard on the Ocean City beach, which he has done for nine years. Since teachers have summers off, he liked the idea of being able to continue swimming and saving lives.

He isn’t the only one having a tough time finding a permanent spot in the classroom.

“A career in teaching can be a hit or miss,” said Clark. “Many of my colleagues are going back to school to get their master’s because they haven’t found any work in over two years.”

Like Clark and Ford, other graduates have been forced to take whatever is available to make a living. Out of 1,000 workers who graduated from college in 2012 and 2013, 46 percent said they were in a job that did not require their degree, according to a survey published by Bloomberg Businessweek in October.

The difficult situation young workers face today is not unexpected, given labor market weakness, but the market is headed in the right direction, very slowly.

Employers expect to hire 8.3 percent more new college graduates from the Class of 2015 than they did from the Class of 2014, according to a report from the National Association of Colleges and Employers.

“We have a decent market, but the struggle of a college graduate is always present,” said Ed Knox, director of research for the association. “Certain majors are doing better than others. English majors have a tough time getting a job with any level of education, opposed to a person that has a bachelor in engineering.”

A Job Outlook 2015 report by Knox’s group found that at the bachelor’s degree level, graduates in the business, engineering and computer/information science fields are most sought by employers.

Jennifer Chao, 23, of Galloway Township, graduated from The Richard Stockton College of New Jersey in December and received a degree in business with a concentration in marketing.

Chao brought her resume to the Fall Career and Internship Fair at her school in October in hopes of landing a job before graduation.

She did.

Chao was hired by Target in Mays Landing as an assistant manager and begins training Monday to take on her duties as the store’s executive team leader.

“It helps if students utilize the programs the school has to offer,” said Chao. “Employers take you seriously when you attend career-related workshops or recruiting events.”

Walter Tarver III, director of Stockton’s Career Center, said that in addition to career fairs, graduating students can sometimes draw on a network of successful alumni for help in a job search.

“The number of alumni who have signed up to be mentors has increased by almost 51 percent since 2010,” said Tarver. “The majority of the increase includes graduates from the past 10 years, which could be an indicator of our graduates’ workplace success.”

But while new graduates have help finding jobs, those who have struggled through the recession might find it difficult to get their career plans back on track.

In Ford’s case, four years have gone by and she is finding it hard to make use of her education again. She worries her likelihood of pursing theater is decreasing because of the experience gap.

“My long-term plans have shifted because I would have to start from square one,” Ford said. “My overall goal is to find a stable nine-to-five job so I can live comfortably. Wherever that job is, I would still get involved with local theater events, because that’s what I love to do.”

Clark took another part-time teaching aide position at Upper Township Middle School in December. The recent grad is going through multiple interviewing processes to become a full-time teacher.

“After a year of trying to gain more experiences, the future is looking bright,” he said.
