Cultivating Your Passions (A Response to the Minimalists)

While I wouldn’t call myself a minimalist, there is much of its philosophy that I hold dear. I value simplicity, freedom, giving back, doing meaningful work, consuming less, and eliminating the excess to focus on what matters most. The baseline of that for me is aligning my outside with my inside, including the work I do in this world.

I’ve followed the work of Ryan Nicodemus and Joshua Fields Millburn, the Minimalists, since they sprang on the scene a few years ago. I deeply enjoy their writing and the way they show up in the world. It’s inspiring and their writing is thought-provoking. They motivate me to keep making healthy changes and that is exactly why I keep coming back for more.

The Path to Freedom

I met Joshua, Ryan, and their friend, Colin Wright, briefly at the World Domination Summit in Portland a couple years ago. I admit to intentionally seeking them out for a conversation. You see, before launching the Mojo Lab, I wrote a blog called Smaller Living for several years that chronicled my own downsizing adventures in Phoenix and my evolving philosophy for my life and work. Though it, I met some wonderful people and made some good friends. So I naturally gravitated to the minimalist tribe to soak up some of their positive energy and camaraderie. Chatting with Joshua, Ryan, and Colin who all walk their talk with authentic kindness was one of the highlights of the WDS experience for me.

Life has changed quite a bit since my Smaller Living blog-writing days. I’m now blissfully married, living in a town called Independence in Oregon (you have to love that), and am a full-time, self-employed soulpreneur. It is a dream come true for me. I am living my passionate vision of writing and building a business founded on my closest-held values and principles. It all started with my Smaller Living blog and my interest in minimalism. That led me to clarify my desires for building my work and life around creative self-expression and helping others find their paths. Following the work of The Minimalists helps keep me tuned in to that piece of my own path that led me here, ensuring it stays a part of my own life and work.

So I read this interview with Cal Newport by Joshua on the Minimalists’ blog this morning with great interest. They were discussing Cal’s book, So Good They Can’t Ignore You. Cal’s premise is that following our passion does not necessarily lead to a happy or fulfilling life. Yep, you read that right.

Stay with me for a moment. Cal argues that we should cultivate our passions instead.

Bingo.

Is Following Your Passion a Mistake?

I’ve long appreciated the sentiment behind “follow your bliss” and “follow your passion.” It helped inspire my initial quests for more satisfying work and way of living when I was younger. From personal experience, however, I can attest that there’s so much more to it than simply following our passion.

We need our creative fire engaged to do our best work and have a sense of satisfaction about what we do. Also, as Cal points out, we need to hone our skills in our chosen field. When we find the sweet spot where those learned skills help us cultivate our passion and are aligned with our values, it allows us to contribute in meaningful, satisfying ways. Therein lies the happy.

It often takes traveling a long and winding road to learn what sparks our soul and makes us want to jump out of bed in the morning to get back to doing it each day. And that can change at points throughout our life. For me, the touchstone is freedom. Freedom for me isn’t about not working. Honestly, I’d be bored senseless. It’s about doing good work with integrity, enthusiastic engagement, and joy. Freedom in my work and life are fueled by self-determination, creative expression, and growth. Without them, the spark of passion dies.

Cal’s advice to not follow your passion, but to cultivate it instead takes a bit to get your head around, but it’s solid wisdom. I followed my passion into some insanely unhappy, unhealthy, and high-stress jobs over the years. When I stopped passively following my passion and began actively cultivating it, that’s when things began to shift. Instead of finding a job, I created my work.

Now that tastes like freedom.
