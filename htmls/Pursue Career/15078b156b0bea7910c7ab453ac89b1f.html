Purposeful Work: Aligning Passion

June 3, 2015 0 Comment

Sitting in my cubical, staring at several hundred pages of technical documents, a deep sense of futility engulfed me. My muscles weakened as my body slid low into my office chair. Forearms now level with my armpits hung limp on the armrests. Slowly my head lolled over the back of my swivel chair. I needed to end the mind-numbing torture of my daily routine—writer’s suicide—a pencil through the temple was the quickest way out.

Sadly this wasn’t the first time I’d contemplated ending my life to end my job. My resume was a series of stops and starts—positions traded every 2.3 years—replaced with the next great solution to job satisfaction. In fact, each and every job was destined to be my last—an end to my search for the perfect career. Each and every job was filled with promise and purpose. Each and every job was exactly the right one until it wasn’t.

It’s not the Jobs Fault

When I pondered my resume, remembering each position and the organizations I supported, it was obvious I participated in meaningful work: raising funds for children’s organizations; marketing for faith-based ministries; and creating positive publicity for shelters and nonprofits. Each and every one of these positions had the potential to be the job or career that changed daily work into purposeful living; but it wasn’t the jobs fault I felt despair. My discontent was more deeply rooted—a product of my inability to understand the foundations of purposeful work and how it was achieved.

Foundations for Purpose and Satisfaction at Work

Bill Hybels, senior pastor of Willow Creek Church and author of Simplify: Ten Practices to Unclutter your Soul, says that “one of the greatest blessings in life is to love your work, to be satisfied in your daily labors.” It’s an obvious statement and a principle we intuitively understand; but why then is it so difficult to accomplish? Why do so many of us struggle to achieve satisfaction in work?

There are many aspects of work that contribute to the I-love-my-job factor and Hybels suggests that each position has four foundational aspects that must be aligned: Passion, culture, challenge, and compensation. While Hybels does not explore how a person discovers his or her passion in this book, there are many qualified authors who do.

A best-selling resource that helped me through a major career transition is Richard Bolles’ What Color is your Parachute. A classic in the figuring-out-your-vocation category, Bolles helped me trace the evidence of joyful work back to my high school days, which inspired me to pursue a writing career. While I later discovered that technical writing was not the culture in which I belonged, Bolles’ workbook exercises put me on a path to pursue a track that later led to great satisfaction.

Culture is another significant contributor to job satisfaction and can often upset a career if not suited to your values.

Culture is another significant contributor to job satisfaction and can often upset a career if not suited to your values. As an example, I once worked for an organization that cared for its employees through excellent benefits and salaries, contributing to retirement and offering attractive perks. On the surface it had a nurturing culture that encouraged education and advancement—all pluses in my culture category. But when the slightest pressure created workday stress, everyone from the CEO to my direct supervisor, resorted to child-like temper tantrums and screaming matches that rolled down the chain of command like a runaway train. It wasn’t long before every employee felt the pain of unhealthy and misdirected anger. This toxic culture was ill-suited to my tendency to avoid confrontation and made more than one night’s sleep fitful and allusive.

Challenge, suggests Hybels, has a “sweet spot” that is “just above ‘appropriately challenged.’ When you can find that level of challenge in your job, your work satisfaction really begins to climb. You know you’re bringing you’re A game nearly every day, and it feels fantastic.”

Personally, if I’m not challenged I’m in a dangerous position. I often fall into this trap after completing a large, difficult project. I find myself frantic to keep engaged in exciting work hungry for the adrenaline-pumping rhythm that accompanies high-risk/high-reward projects. It requires intentionality on my part to ensure I stay ahead of the boring, mind-numbing work that starts me thinking about resignation by suicide.

The final factor of Hybels’ foundations for work alignment is compensation. Often a balance between rewarding, well-suited work and our desire for wealth and achievement, compensation can often be overplayed as the leading contributor to job satisfaction.

In my career, compensation often appeared to be at odds with passion.

In my career, compensation often appeared to be at odds with passion. There were times I left positions because the balance between compensation and passion was skewed too far one way or the other. For example, I closed a business I was passionate about because it didn’t pay my bills. I also turned down a six-figure salary because passion was totally misaligned.

The bottom line about compensation is that we can build a combination of paid and non-paid service opportunities to create a collective of wages and benefits that contribute to our whole work persona. One job that addresses all our needs and desires is not the likely opportunity for most of us, so we should not expect a one-job solution.

Making Balanced Changes

While most of us are not stuck in an absolutely miserable job we’re also not engaged in our dream job where passion, culture, challenge, and compensation are in perfect balance. If you’re struggling with how best to align the foundations of a purposeful career, spend some time deciphering and prioritizing which of these foundational elements need the most attention.

Adding acts of service to boost your passion or picking up additional income to supplement a job you already love may help you avoid a wholesale job change. Fulfilling your greatest needs first can also provide enough balance to help you further assess God’s intended blessing on your desire for purposeful work: “When God gives someone wealth and possessions, and the ability to enjoy them, to accept their lot and be happy in their toil—this is a gift of God” (Ecclesiastes 5:19).
