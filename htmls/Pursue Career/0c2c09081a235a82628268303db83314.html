How to Find the Right Job Based on My Skills

by Christian Fisher

  • 3 [Job Hunting Skills] | Techniques to Improve Job Hunting Skills

Recognizing and sharpening your talents and skills is as essential to success as having passion for your interests and commitment to hard work, according to “Psychology Today." Be persistent in your job search as you continually assess your skills and talents compared to the current job market. Knowing what you’re good at and passionate about can help you maximize your job search efforts and articulate to potential employers the value you create with your work.

1. Commit a certain amount of time each day to your job search. If you’re already employed, devote an hour or two after work to job hunt. If you’re unemployed, treat job-hunting as a job, with set hours each day. Expand your search beyond your immediate geographic area, if you’re willing and capable of relocating. Additionally, consider developing your skills through ongoing classes and training either online or on location. Use sites such as LinkedIn and Facebook to find and connect with others working in your desired type of job and contribute thoughtfully to discussions related to your skills.

2. Write a skills-based resume. Creating a skills-based resume can help you, especially if you’ve had several positions that are similar or if you are attempting to change careers where your former positions don't readily support your new direction. A skills-based resume can also help if you’re new to the job market, haven’t worked in a long while, or have a lot of volunteer or non-paid internship experience, suggests a January 2012 “Forbes” article. “Forbes” suggests you start by listing several transferable skill sets at the beginning of your resume; skill sets that are relevant to the job you’re applying for. Under each skill set, bullet point one or more instances where you’ve applied these skills and achieved concrete results. After the skills section, write your work history in reverse chronological order followed by your education and current professional affiliations.

3. Become a volunteer or intern. Organizations may have opportunities for you to fine-tune your skills and establish professional relationships that could lead to a paying job. Volunteerism or internships may be ideal for you especially if you’re a college student, want to work in the nonprofit sector, are changing careers, or find that your desired field requires more experience than you already have.

4. Create your own job, even temporarily. Applying your skills as a self-employed freelancer or home-based business owner, even part-time, is a way to craft your own job and apply your skills right away. Start small. For example, if you have graphic design skills, ask a local business to hire you to design their next brochure, or a local band to let you design a graphic for their new album release. Self-employment can be quite fulfilling as a source of income, networking and skill development. Keep an eye open for opportunities to apply your skills as an independent contractor on a per project basis while you persist at finding steadier employment.
