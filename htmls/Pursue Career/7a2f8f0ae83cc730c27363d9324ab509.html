How to Figure Out Exactly What Your Dream Job Should Be

Finding a job you love is no easy task — but it's definitely not impossible.

By Sara Austin
Jun 13, 2016

"Follow your bliss." The first time I heard this advice was from a classical-music professor, midway through a voice lesson in my junior year of college. The idea was so romantic and enchanting, I repeated it like a mantra for months afterward … but I still couldn't figure out what my bliss was. I loved singing but was skeptical someone would pay me to do it. And I loved a lot of other things — politics, media, Shakespeare, sports. Meanwhile, my grandmother, who was chipping in for my education, had a dream that I attend law school, preferably at Yale but no pressure.

After graduation, I was back living with my parents. I tanked the LSAT accidentally on purpose. I was lost, while all my friends seemed to be carpe diem-ing the shit out of post-college life.

Only now do I see that "follow your bliss" (and similar tropes like "do what you love" and "find your passion") is not the most useful advice. It oversimplifies a massively difficult challenge. "Many recent grads are very conflicted about how to find work that inspires and satisfies them," confirms Kathryn Minshew, co-founder and CEO of the career site The Muse. Her own early-career confusion — she tried acting and international relations before landing in the startup world — is part of what inspired her business.

Yet career ambivalence is out of fashion. Girl-boss narratives and the burden of student debt have fed a new urgency to earn ASAP. Minshew sees many recent graduates become despondent if they end up not liking the work they've chosen — and worse, they may stick with something they hate because they don't want to feel like a failure. We beat ourselves up for missteps when they are really just … steps. "Understanding that it's challenging and messy is one of the most powerful things you can do for yourself," she says.

If you're reading this and you haven't found your dream job yet, cut yourself some slack. But do keep looking! Some people may find that their true passion is something they do outside the office. Still, you'll likely spend one-third of your life at work, and everyone deserves a career that makes them happy, is financially viable, and maximizes their unique skills, says Chris Guillebeau, author of the new book Born for This. He interviewed hundreds of people who felt they had found the work they were meant to do and learned that "most successful people have not taken a linear path. One thing they have in common is curiosity. They constantly ask questions."

Start by questioning yourself. What do you like? What are you good at? "Write down all the things you do well — it could be house-cleaning, bookkeeping, throwing parties," suggests Farai Chideya, author of The Episodic Career. "Circle the things that could earn you money, then star the circled things you are willing to do for money. Sometimes you will find superpowers you didn't know you had."

Every career path comes with a certain amount of difficulty, but you can choose what that difficulty is.

Once you've drawn up a list of jobs that could tap your skills, suggests Minshew, take 30 minutes to research each position online or tap your alumni network to interview someone who does that work. Ask them what the worst part of their job is. "Every career path comes with a certain amount of difficulty, but you can choose what that difficulty is," Minshew says. "I started out thinking I might work at the CIA or the State Department because I loved the idea of solving weighty problems. But I realized bureaucracy is a type of pain I did not want to deal with. Whereas in the startup space, the pain is the pressure and the unpredictable hours, which don't bother me as much." Spend half an hour a day for two weeks and you'll have investigated 14 potential fields.

Focus on vocations — nurse, teacher, engineer — but also working conditions. Does the prospect of collaborating in teams, working in a small lab, dealing with the public, or schmoozing at a cocktail party make you feel excited or kinda homicidal? How important is it that you have a short commute, opportunities to travel, diverse coworkers, prestige, or a cause you care about? "You can be doing something that you like and believe in, but if you have a schedule or a boss that isn't working for you, then you will be stressed," Guillebeau says.

If a paycheck is what drives you, there's no shame in that. Chideya's research found that the money-motivated tend to be happier in their work than those who are mission-driven: They have clear benchmarks for success (plus, they probably have a beach house). That's not to say you shouldn't change the world, but being practical about your needs is OK. Chideya initially dreamed of being a novelist but, partly on her mother's advice, decided journalism was more financially secure. Success at Newsweek, CNN, and public radio led to opportunities in academia, and she is now a distinguished writer-in-residence at New York University. "It's been an incredible career — I got to sit in Nelson Mandela's living room — but I can't say that it was, strictly speaking, my first choice," she says.

Over the years, Chideya adds, what you prioritize in a job will ebb and flow. There's a time to make bank, a time to win a Nobel Prize, a time when you just want to be home to read your kids Good Night, Gorilla.

NO JOB IS EVER A DEAD END

As president of the Society of Women Engineers, Colleen Layman often mentors young women who are questioning if they belong in STEM. Her advice? "You don't have to know what you are going to do for the rest of your life. I'm fortysomething, and I'm still not sure," says Layman, who is vice president of HDR, an architectural, engineering, and consulting firm. She grew up in northeast Pennsylvania, where her dad worked in a coal mine and few people in her town had gone to college. When a nun at her Catholic school suggested engineering, she hardly knew what that meant. "I told her I wanted to use what I know to help people," Layman recalls. "She said, 'That's what engineers do.'"

Advertisement - Continue Reading Below

Layman set out to study electrical engineering but struggled. She was working restaurant jobs to cover tuition, away from home for the first time, and earning the worst grades of her life. "I ended up dropping out of school," she says. "I was demoralized and completely depressed. The town I grew up in had just opened a coal-fired power plant, and my dad helped me get a job there. I thought, This is it, I'll work here for the rest of my life." But slowly, as she leaned into the work, her confidence returned. The men at the plant trained and encouraged her, and soon, she was back in night school. "What felt like a failure turned out to be one of the best things I could have ever done because I got hands-on experience," she says. "Look toward the future, but do the best job you can do in the position you have right now."

If a paycheck is what drives you, there's no shame in that.

In any job, you can improve on what Guillebeau calls soft skills — writing, public speaking, negotiating, getting comfortable with technology — which are important but not always emphasized in school. Improve your follow-through skills by writing things down in a meeting, not walking away until you know the next steps, and setting deadlines. "Be that person who makes things happen," Guillebeau says. "Being a reliable person will help you stand out in a very competitive environment."

At the end of each day, review what you did. "Ask yourself, What gave me energy?" suggests Guillebeau. "Think about those things that bring you joy and energy, and try to do more of those things, with whatever autonomy you have."

If your chosen field turns out to be an energy-suck, you can always change lanes. Guillebeau abandoned an accounting major when his marks fell short. Minshew adored performing when it was a hobby but found that the more shows felt like a job, the less fun she had. "Young women feel so much pressure to be perfect. I think they feel it more than men do," Layman says. "Have a plan for where you want to go, but the plan is not everything. If you get into something and it's not what you thought it would be, that's okay."

For me, the year after college was a misery. My mom, a librarian, helped me get a job filing magazines in the stacks. It felt soul-deadening at the time but taught me a lot about magazines. Next, I took an assistant role at a nonprofit with an eye to arts management but found that where I shined was research and writing. I doubled back to study journalism, worked at a series of editing jobs, and a few years ago, landed a position at Cosmo that is more fun than should be legal (we even won a National Magazine Award). I followed my bliss in a dozen directions, and I have no clue what comes next. I'm in no hurry to find out.

SEVEN WAYS TO FIGURE THINGS OUT

1. Check your messages. Look at several months of your social-media feeds or email inbox. What favors do friends ask? What do they consider you an expert on? "A lot of people don't know what they are good at," Guillebeau says. "If you listen to other people, they will help identify your skills for you."

Advertisement - Continue Reading Below

2. Befriend an Old. "Cultivate age-diverse friend-ships," Chideya suggests. Talking to older people, like your friends' parents or your former teachers, about their careers can give you valuable perspective.

3. Be a joiner. "Get involved with organizations in your field of study," says Colleen Layman. "You need to get out of your college bubble and meet professionals." The U.S. Department of Labor lists groups at CareerOneStop.org.

4. Start a side hustle. If you're having trouble breaking in or not sure if a field is for you, try it on the side. "It's so easy to set up a website and a PayPal account," Guillebeau says. "You don't have to quit your day job, and it doesn't have to take over your life unless you want it to."

NBC

5. Go on a walkabout. Explore the world, if you have that luxury. "Travel probably won't tell you what you think of a potential career, but it might tell you a lot about yourself," Minshew says.

Advertisement - Continue Reading Below

6. "Quit" yearly. If you aren't sure that your current job or school program is the right one, says Guillebeau, set an annual date where you decide to resign. Once you've imagined making the break, evaluate whether it felt like the right choice. "Either way, you've made a conscious decision and can proceed with confidence," he says.

7. Find a circle you trust. It's tough to find your true calling around haters who want you to be someone else. "Surround yourself with supportive people who aren't yes-men and who are fundamentally on your side," says Chideya. Connect with them at LeanInCircles.org.

What are your ideal working conditions? Chris Guillebeau compiled a quiz to help you find out — head to BornForThisBook.com.
