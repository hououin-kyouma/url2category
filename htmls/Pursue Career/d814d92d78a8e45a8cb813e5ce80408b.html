How to Get a Job at 'Teen Vogue'

Lessons from Zoey on 'Black-ish' — and from editors themselves.

We at Teen Vogue are HUGE fans of Black-ish , and adore our December cover star Yara Shahidi , who plays the sassy and stylish Zoey on the show. And the feeling is totally mutual, as the team at Black-ish were generous enough to include us (and Teen Vogue Editor Elaine Welteroth as herself!) in last night’s show. In an episode that talked about nepotism — what happens when people in positions of power use their influence in order to give their family members or friends a leg-up — Zoey’s father Dre uses his connections to secure a fellowship at one of the coolest places to work (if we do say so ourselves).

When Zoey earns a promotion in just one week, her dad gets worried that she’s only coasting her way up on his good name… only to get a good schooling from Elaine on what a rock star her daughter is. In the episode’s most touching moment, Zoey gets to prove to Dre that her hard work and style intuition are ultimately why she’ll succeed, as he gets to see her talent and passion first-hand.

Trust us: Whether it’s the fashion or journalism industry, it’s impossible to truly thrive without tons of hard work — even if you get help from a lucky connection. Still, we get asked all the time: How do you get a job at Teen Vogue , and how can you work your way to the top? Below, a few of our team members share key advice on what it takes to make it, and it’s handy guidance on how to get ANY dream job.

Kaleigh Fasanella, Associate Digital Editor: “One of the most important things you can do to help get your foot in the door at a place like Teen Vogue is to make connections and stick with them. Even if you didn’t land your dream internship, you can reach out to some of your favorite writers and/or editors and tell them what you’re passionate about. Show them your voice, and learn how to write a killer pitch. Aside from that, know what sets you apart and always go into interviews with an upbeat, can-do-it-all attitude. And ALWAYS send the thank you letter!”

Sue Williamson, Beauty/Style Writer: “'Work hard, be nice!' is the best advice anyone in any industry can ever receive. Also, don’t be afraid to reach out to someone you admire and ask them for an informational interview or quick 10 minute meeting if you can. The worst they can say is no! Don’t worry so much about what you want to do when you get older and develop natural interests and skills instead. That way you’ll be prepared for the job of your dreams!”

Vanessa Willoughby, Digital Features Editor: “Perseverance and the ability to handle constructive criticism are important tools to use in any career. You could be the smartest person in the room, but if you can’t accept when you make a mistake, you’ll have a hard time advancing and in turn, networking with people who can open doors for you.”

Nicole Pizaro, Digital Photo Editor: “Realize that internships are really about gaining experience — something that simply can’t be measured. We are all where we are today because we learned exactly what we wanted to do because of some pretty amazing opportunities. And with that, take as many different opportunities as you can while in school (whether that’s an internship, a paid job, or some other experience) so you can find what really speaks to you.”

Michaela O’Shaughnessy, Social Content Producer: “Start getting as much experience as you can while you’re in school. Whether it’s a part-time job or an internship, all great things are done by a series of small things brought together that will help you get to where you want to be. There’s something to be learned from each and every professional experience, even if it’s discovering what you’re great at and what you’re not so great at.”

Ella Cerón, Digital Entertainment Editor: “Treat any and every job like it’s important. It doesn’t matter if you’re serving coffee or making copies all day long or selling sweaters at the mall; each job will give you certain skill sets you can and will take with you to the next job. You don’t learn everything from your college degree; a lot of important skill sets are accrued through experience. And no job — not even asking if you’d like fries with that — is beneath you.”

Amanda Chan, Digital Deputy Editor: “Seek out opportunities! My very first internship was with my local community newspaper when I was a freshman in college. The only reason it ever happened is because I reached out to the editor and asked if there was ANYTHING I could do that summer to get some experience. By putting myself out there and explaining how I could add value, I was able to create an opportunity for myself. Through that internship, I wrote some stories that I was able to use as clips and parlay into several internships with the major newspaper in my city; that experience helped me go on to intern, and eventually work full time, at my dream publications, including Teen Vogue .”

Jessica Andrews, Digital Fashion Editor: "My biggest internship lesson? Handle grunt work with grace. I never thought any task was beneath me, from fetching lunch to picking up dry cleaning. And it paid off tremendously. As it turns out, my bosses were never measuring how fast I brought them lunch — they were assessing my work ethic and my passion for the job. When it was time for me to get promoted, and that time totally came, they were confident that I'd work just as hard as a fashion assistant, a freelance writer and now a fashion editor. I'm grateful I had the opportunity to prove myself to them — by running errands with a smile on my face."
