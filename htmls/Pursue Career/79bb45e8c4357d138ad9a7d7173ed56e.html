Using The High Impact Indicators: 4 Skill Sets To Focus On In Your Classroom

April 11, 2016 Professional Development Eliza Wong

Are you making the most out of the High Impact Indicators (HII) in your classroom? At the Commission on Adult Basic Education’s (COABE) national conference, GED® test experts Bonnie Goonen and Susan Pittman took a closer look at four HII skill sets. Try these teaching strategies to ensure your students develop these important skills.


What are the High Impact Indicators?

While all of the indicators listed in the GED® Assessment Targets and Indicators describe skills that are essential for test-taker success, the High Impact Indicators (also available in Spanish) highlight specific skills that may be especially useful for educators to emphasize in their instruction.

That’s because these skills lend themselves well to classroom instruction and are covered on multiple GED® test subjects. Plus, research shows that many educators may not be spending much time on developing these skill sets in the classroom.

Skill Set #1: Searching for Evidence
Teaching Strategy: Sometimes it’s difficult for students to distinguish between evidence from source texts versus their own personal opinions. Here’s a tip to try in the classroom:

  1. Start by teaching students about the different types of evidence.
  2. Then, provide students with examples from source texts.
  3. Walk your students through the process of identifying the different types of evidence.
  4. Finally, it’s their turn to read a source text and find the different types of evidence.

Skill Set #2: Connecting Evidence
Teaching Strategy: Students need to understand how to evaluate evidence and connect it to a claim or hypothesis. One strategy is to teach students how to “State-Cite-Explain”.

  1. State: First, have students state their claim or argument.
  2. Cite: Then, have students provide supporting evidence from the text by paraphrasing or directly quoting from the text.
  3. Explain: Finally, have students explain how the quote(s) or paraphrase(s) support the claim or argument. For example, have them use terms like “this shows,” “this is because,” or “this highlights the difference between.”


Skill Set #3: Making Inferences
Teaching Strategy: A test question may require students to look at what a source text says explicitly and then make an inference as to why something occurs. Questioning strategies are a good way for students to see and consider things that are not literally on the printed page. Try using these questioning strategies in your classroom:

  1. Character analysis. As students are reading fiction or literary text, have them answer questions about the character, such as:  
    1. What does the character do?
    2. What does the character say or think?
    3. How do others feel about the character?
    4. How does the character change?
    5. What is the conflict that is expressed?
    6. What is the author’s theme or point of view?
  2. Questioning the author. This reading strategy has readers ask questions of the author during and after reading. Learn more here.
  3. “It Says – I Say – And So”. This reading strategy requires the reader to make inferences using this helpful graphic organizer.

Remember, it’s important to teach students which types of questions to ask when reading fiction versus nonfiction text.

Skill Set #4: Working with Graphics and Data
Teaching Strategy: Regardless of the test subject, interpreting graphics and scale factors, determining statistical data, and applying formulas all require the same skill sets. Spend time in your classroom explaining how data can be represented in different formats. Make sure students have experience reading and working with data in many different formats including:

  1. Tally tables
  2. Line plots
  3. Frequency tables
  4. Histograms
  5. Box plots

These are just a few examples to get you started – there are many formats that students should be familiar with and able to interpret.
