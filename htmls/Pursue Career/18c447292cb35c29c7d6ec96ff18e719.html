How to carve out your perfect career path

So you're fresh out of college and ready to jump into the job market. Or maybe you're employed in a position that's a bad fit, and you're looking to move on.

Taking the first step can be intimidating and confusing, but with a bit of preparation — and expert advice — you can craft a solid plan and find a career that's a custom fit. After all, the right job is often an essential factor for health and peace of mind, not to mention financial security, said Jody Michael, CEO and founder of Jody Michael Associates, a career counseling company based in Chicago and Atlanta.

"Finding the right career fit is so important," said Michael, a certified career coach. "Making the wrong career choice can be a catalyst for mental health challenges including stress, anxiety and even depression. When you feel stuck in a job that doesn't align with your talents, abilities, interests and values, it contributes to a sense of hopelessness and inertia."

But when you get it right, you'll actually look forward to Mondays. "You experience less stress and more fulfillment, flow, energy, focus and satisfaction — not to mention success," Michael said.

Go exploring

For young people starting out, "the early years are all about exploration, figuring out who you are, what you're good at and where you fit in," said Arlene Hirsch, author and owner of Arlene Hirsch Associates, a Chicago-based career counseling firm. She recommended an approach called "planned happenstance" from Stanford professor John Krumboltz's book "Luck Is No Accident: Making the Most of Happenstance in Life and Your Career."

According to Krumboltz, it's a good idea to start by considering a range of careers, seeking out people already working in those fields and then learning as much as possible from them. "And then see what happens," Hirsch said. "It's not unusual for people to fall into jobs that they hadn't previously considered."

Call for help

Michael recommends taking a number of proactive steps, including hiring a career coach and spending a day shadowing a professional working in your field of interest. Check in with your alma mater to see if it offers such a program, or scour your personal and professional networks.

"Visit the websites of professional associations to gain valuable industry insight," Michael said. "Consider registering for their newsletters or following them on social media channels. Even better, join or attend a local event that is open to non-members to learn more about current industry developments and to network."

Arranging for informational interviews with people working in your chosen field can be helpful as well. It allows you to clarify your understanding of the job's responsibilities, sharpen your focus and grow your professional network, Michael added.

Personality matters

For recent grads getting started, take stock of which classes you enjoyed in college, even if it wasn't within your major, said Judi Lansky, a Chicago-based career coach and consultant with Lansky Career Consultants.

"In addition to skills and interests, it would be very helpful to identify your personality traits and consider how they can help determine your ideal career," Lansky said. "If you loved your literature and psychology classes, you probably wouldn't be advised to choose a job where you sit behind a computer inputting data all day."

She suggested the Flower Exercise in "What Color Is Your Parachute?" by Richard Bolles. It helps you visualize what your ideal job might be, in environments where you can use the skills you most enjoy, flourishing like a flower.

Jumping ship

Let's say you drop into a position that seems great at the time but quickly goes sour.

"Try not to get too discouraged," Lansky said. "It happens to most of us at least once in the process of building a career."

Changing jobs is more common than ever, so don't think that you failed if your first (or second or third) career choice doesn't pan out, Hirsch said. "Millennials are faced with an overwhelming number of choices that make it difficult to know what to choose," Hirsch said. "As a result, they are often paralyzed by indecision or a fear of making the wrong choice."

The trick, she said, is to let go of the notion that there's one perfect choice out there and embrace the possibilities with a trial-and-error approach. "You try something. You figure out what did and did not work and then develop another prototype based on new experiences."

Be realistic

Everyone would love to find their ideal career. Who hasn't fantasized about being a star athlete or Oscar-winning actor? But the best career choice is usually something quite different.

Some people simply have more passion than talent — think of "American Idol" — so it's essential to look closely at your own individual needs, values and talents, as well as the job market, to make an informed decision, Hirsch said. Passion is often best served by an avocation or hobby.

"You don't necessarily have to choose the career that you love the most," Hirsch added. "My favorite skill is probably writing, but my counseling practice provides the data and my forensic practice pays the bills."

—Bob Young for Just Energy
