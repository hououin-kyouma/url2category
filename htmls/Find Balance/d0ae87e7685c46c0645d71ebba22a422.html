    Success over Stress

    Reviewed by Varnada Karriem-Norwood on June 15, 2012

    Sources

    Dr. Charles Raison, MD; Asst. Prof. and Dir. Behavior Immunology Program at the Dept. of Psychiatry and Behavioral Science. Emory Univ. School of Medicine. A-Z Health Guide from WebMD (Healthwise) Effects of Stress Chronic Stress Problems. WebMD Special Report: Tips for Reducing Stress

    WebMD Archive

    Video Transcript

    Male Narrator (Continued): We all have plenty to be stressed out about… some more than others There’s some compelling evidence activities like yoga, deep breathing, massage, meditation or Tai Chi can reduce pain, improve blood sugar in diabetics and in some cases lower blood pressure—not to mention reducing stress. In fact, there are a number of creative strategies you can use to limit stress: A meaningful hobby like art or music can strike the right chord. Find a social outlet…a study tracking breast cancer patients found that those who attended weekly support groups lived twice as long as those who didn't. Keep a journal. Research shows writing down your feelings can reduce tension. Try cutting back on caffeine, alcohol and if you smoke take steps to quit. And… Exercise on a regular basis—it triggers chemicals in the brain that block stress. Therapists are increasingly advising patients to unplug themselves from encounters that are likely to cause stress.

    Edward Rosensweig, PhD.: Turn off the media. You know, there's a hiatus there’s a moratorium. We don't answer the phone, the TV's not going.

    Male Narrator: Those who can find ways to turn their backs on stressful situations, experts say, will ultimately lead healthier lives.

    Tommy Futch, Comic: You can change these things…might take a little planning. It might hurt a little…don't stay stuck. Move on.

    Male Narrator: For WebMD, I'm Damon Meharg.
