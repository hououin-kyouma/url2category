Why Work-Life Balance Is Important for Nurses

March 9, 2017

Nurses are rarely off duty. Even after the shift is over, the work of a nurse often continues as friends and loved ones seek your advice. Nurses are community heroes, so it is understandable that the lines between work and daily life can blur. However, it is important that nurses take time off for themselves by deliberately turning off nurse-mode and enjoying some down time.

What Is Work-Life Balance?

The term “work-life balance” refers to a state of well-being in which you find a healthy way to separate your work responsibilities from your personal life. As social beings, it is natural for people to socialize with co-workers after the workday is done, and it is also common for people to gravitate toward others who are interested in the same things. However, this makes it easy for one’s work life to cross into one’s personal life.

While it is not necessarily unhealthy to socialize with co-workers or absorb work-related information outside of work, it can make it easy for a nurse’s work to overwhelm many other aspects of life. It is important that not all of your time and energy is absorbed by just one facet of who you are. Rather, in order to have work-life balance, you need to make a conscious effort to disengage from your work mindset while you are at home, out with friends or enjoying hobbies.

Why Is Work-Life Balance so Important?

Without work-life balance, you can become overwhelmed with the constant responsibilities of being a nurse. If you think and act like a nurse even when you are off duty, it can begin to feel as though you are never off the clock. This can lead to burnout.

According to the article titled “Demographics and personality factors associated with burnout among nurses in a Singapore tertiary hospital,” burnout is “a syndrome of feelings of emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and reduced personal accomplishment.” For nurses, this means that when you are burned out you feel unable to connect with patients on an emotional level. You may feel apathetic, disinterested, or detached from patients’ suffering or their requests. You may be unable to communicate effectively or you may not notice small details that could be important. In general, burnout looks like a lack of caring. However, if you are feeling burned out, it is more likely that you are simply overworked and you are not giving yourself enough time to unwind from the stresses of work.

The same article notes that younger nurses are more likely to experience burnout than more experienced nurses who have worked in the field for several years. This is likely because it takes time to develop a healthy work-life balance. Nurses who work late shifts that interfere with normal sleep patterns are also more likely to experience burnout. Also, individuals with certain personality traits experience burnout at different rates. For example, research shows that nurses who score high on extraversion, openness, agreeableness, and conscientiousness had less burnout than those who had high scores on neuroticism.

Nursing is a demanding job that requires scientific, interpersonal and emotional intelligence. Using multiple skills to help numerous patients each day, while also caring for family and friends at home, can take a serious toll on a nurse’s faculties. To avoid burnout, nurses must be able to establish boundaries within the different areas of their lives.

What Can Nurses Do to Create Work-Life Balance?

The first step is to assess which areas of your job are the most demanding. If you work the night shift and getting enough sleep is a challenge for you, then you should pay special attention to getting enough rest and relaxation when you are not at work. If you find yourself doing a great deal of heavy lifting or you perform the same movements repeatedly, you should focus your off hours on caring for your body physically to prevent repetitive stress injuries.

Once you have identified the area or areas that make you most vulnerable to burnout, you can begin to create healthy habits that counteract the toils of work. Because nursing is a physically demanding job, it is important that you get enough sleep, eat healthy meals, drink plenty of water and exercise at least 30 minutes each day, even if it is just a walk around the park.

Taking care of your physical being is important, but burnout is largely an emotional problem. Researchers suggest voicing your concerns and stressors in order to process them fully. This can mean talking with a friend or family member about any stressors, seeing a counselor on a regular basis, or keeping a journal. People who are open and honest about the problems in their life are less likely to experience burnout.

Another way you can protect yourself against burnout is by strengthening the personality traits that make you more resilient. For instance, nurses who score high on agreeableness are less likely to experience burnout. To increase your agreeableness, practice being more flexible and accepting of things that are out of your control. It is common for high achievers to want to be perfect, but perfection is impossible. Accept what you cannot change, take a deep breath and be willing to amend your plans to fit any given situation.

As you grow in your understanding of yourself as a person and as a nurse, you will begin to establish a healthy work-life balance that allows you the freedom to recover from a hard day of work. As a nurse, you are responsible for providing your patients with the best possible care. That means you must take care of yourself before you can help anyone else.
