Allhands: Can't find work-life balance? Here's how employers can help

Joanna Allhands, The Republic | azcentral.com Published 5:09 p.m. MT March 26, 2017 | Updated 7:09 a.m. MT April 13, 2017

It's tough to find work-life balance on our own.

Sometimes, we need help from our employers to free up time for important things besides work. But how willing are companies to pitch in?

As we noted in the first part of this series, many businesses are asking more of their employees. We're working longer hours and handling more responsibilities.

Yet more than half of workers don't use all of their vacation days, largely because there's no one to fill in when we're gone. Taking time off requires extra work before we leave and when we return.

MORE: How you can find work-life balance

It's in a company's best long-term interest to ensure its workers are finding time to rejuvenate. Research shows that rested, empowered employees are far more productive than those that are overworked and disengaged.

But the drive to meet quarterly earnings forecasts often makes it tough for businesses to take the long view, especially in industries where workers are plentiful. Companies' short-term bottom line doesn't take a hit if employees get burned out and leave, because they can easily find someone else to fill the slot.

How, then, can employees find a workload and schedule that suits them?

(Story continues below graphic)

Mandated hours? Don't count on it

Some have suggested that government step in to mandate certain things of employers. They point to Europe, which requires maximum work weeks and mandated vacations for workers. France recently passed a law requiring employers to set hours when email cannot be sent.

That may sound heavenly, but don't hold your breath for mandated time off to happen here. Even European counties are under pressure to walk back these traditions because they make it difficult to compete with the U.S. and Asia, which work far longer hours.

"So it becomes a race to the bottom," said Arizona State University professor Blake Ashforth, who has spent years researching the workplace.

More likely: Paid family leave

President Trump supports offering six weeks paid leave after a baby is born.  (Photo: vadimguzhva, Getty Images/iStockphoto)

The more likely place for government to step in is with paid sick time and maternity and paternity leave, which advocates say are necessary to help workers perform at their best.

Arizona voters recently approved up to five days of paid sick time as part of Proposition 206, and some of the state's major employers have voluntarily extended paid family leave to 16 weeks, though they are in higher-skilled industries, such as finance. There has been little movement to offer paid time to poorer workers, who arguably would benefit from it most.

The Trump administration has said it supports offering six weeks paid family leave, and the benefit may apply to fathers as well as mothers, though not at full pay. Congress appears to be warming to the idea.

So, hey, that's a start.

What we need: Flexibility

But government can't fix every problem we have in finding work-life balance. Mandates tend to be one-size-fits-all, notes Rachel Greszler, a senior policy analyst for the Heritage Foundation, and that doesn't match how many of us work these days.

According to a recent CareerBuilder survey, nearly half of workers say they work outside the typical 9-to-5. Half check and answer email at home. A growing number of workers – now roughly one in four – also are regularly working outside the office, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

Many employees say they need that flexibility to juggle other priorities, such as child care or volunteering.

The business case for flex time

Flex time also can benefit employers.

According to Gallup's most recent State of the American Workplace report, a majority of workers are disengaged, meaning they neither like nor dislike their jobs – they're just there. Making them care has been shown to lower absenteeism, accidents and material losses, and it can even make companies more profitable.

Companies have spent billions of dollars trying to reengage their employees with things like tuition reimbursements and gym memberships, but many of those perks don't even register on employee surveys as priorities.

Not surprisingly, health insurance, retirement savings and paid time off rise to the top. But so does flexible scheduling and the ability to work from home. Gallup found a majority of workers would leave their company to get flex time, and more than a third would do so to work from home.

(Story continues below graphic)

A caveat about flexibility

Does that mean we all should work from home and at unconventional hours? Of course not. It's difficult for people in service industries to phone in from home, for example. And despite recent research that suggests those who do work from home are more productive, not all workers are wired to work outside the office because they need its structure and face-to-face interaction.

Others end up working much more than necessary at home because they don't draw appropriate boundaries between the job and the rest of their lives.

Ideally, flexibility in the workplace should be a conversation: Employees should feel comfortable talking to their bosses about their needs, and bosses should be trained, empowered and encouraged to make concessions.

That's how it worked when I was pregnant two years ago. I asked my boss if I could work from home once the baby was born, and that has made all the difference.

I work 12-hour days, often in fits and spurts, but I get the job done without inconveniencing my coworkers. I can avoid the two-hour commute each day. And I have time to start a roast, put laundry in the dryer or read a book to my kiddo.

My life has far better balance than it would be if I were sitting in my cubicle.

That, ultimately, is what work in 2017 should be about.
