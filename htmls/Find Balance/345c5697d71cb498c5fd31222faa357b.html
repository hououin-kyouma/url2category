How to Reduce Cortisol, the Stress Hormone

By Deane Alban

Views: 140,766

Chronic stress can lead to an elevated cortisol level resulting in mental, emotional, and physical health issues. Learn how to reduce cortisol naturally.

If it’s been awhile since you’ve felt truly relaxed and happy, you may be suffering from a common modern malady — excess cortisol.

Cortisol is a major stress hormone that contributes to mental distress and a wide range of physical illnesses.

If you are concerned that stress is ruining your life and possibly damaging your health, here are five things you can do to reduce cortisol naturally.

Why It’s Critical to Reduce Excess Cortisol

Before we look at how to lower cortisol, let’s take a look at why a high level is so hazardous to your health and mental well-being.

When you face a potential threat or danger, your body’s stress response — often called the “fight or flight” response — is triggered.

First, the stress hormones norepinephrine and epinephrine are released in a quick burst.

That is followed by the release of cortisol.

When your life is (or seems to be) one crisis after another, your body continuously pours out cortisol.

This leads to a chronically elevated cortisol level, and this has some serious health implications.

Chronically elevated cortisol is linked to lowered immune function, obesity, high blood pressure, insomnia, and heart disease. (1)

It also contributes to brain fog, anxiety, depression, mood swings, memory loss, concentration problems, and mental disorders of all kinds. (2, 3, 4, 5)

Stress negatively impacts every cognitive function. (6)

Alarmingly, cortisol kills existing brain cells by literally stimulating them to death. (7)

It also reduces the number of new brain cells being created by decreasing the production of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) — a protein that’s integral in stimulating new brain cell formation.

So, now you know how elevated cortisol is bad for your health.

Here are the steps you can take to lower your cortisol level and production.

1. Reduce Cortisol with Diet

You can make major strides in reducing cortisol levels by eating a diet based on whole, unprocessed foods.

Be sure to include these foods which have a well-deserved reputation as brain foods that nourish and protect the brain and reduce cortisol levels:

  • wild-caught salmon (8)
  • dark chocolate (9)
  • berries (10)
  • garlic (11)
  • olive oil (12)
  • turmeric (13)
  • green tea (14)
  • chamomile tea (15)

Also consider eating prebiotic foods.

These are foods that encourage the growth of gut bacteria which are now known to be integrally connected to, and supportive of, brain health. (16)

Somewhat surprisingly, eating prebiotic foods can reduce cortisol and improve your mood. (17)

Prebiotic foods include asparagus, bananas, barley, leeks, garlic, jicama, lentils, mustard greens, onions, and tomatoes. (18, 19)

Dark chocolate, cocoa powder, and cacao nibs also act as prebiotics. (20)

Since most of these foods are delicious, adding them to your diet should be easy.

However, it won’t be so easy to reduce or remove two popular substances known to increase cortisol — sugar and caffeine.

Ironically, many people eat sugary foods to relax, but sugar triggers a boost of cortisol and adrenaline that can last up to five hours. (21, 22)

The caffeine jolt you get from coffee, energy drinks, and soda stimulates the adrenal glands to pump out more cortisol.

A habit of regular caffeine consumption can more than double your blood level of this stress hormone. (23)

According to Dr. Shawn Talbott, author of The Cortisol Connection Diet, l-theanine is a cortisol-controlling amino acid that counteracts the cortisol-increasing tendency of caffeine.

3. Lower Cortisol Levels with Physical Exercise

Regular physical exercise enhances mental functions and protects your brain from the effects of aging. (40, 41)

It will help you increase your energy and relax more easily.

One of the ways it does this is by reducing cortisol.

✓Exercise Motivator Feel compelled to keep yourself fit with self-hypnosis

Interestingly, you don’t need to exercise strenuously to experience stress reduction.

In fact, exercising too much or too hard actually increases cortisol. (42)

Running a marathon, for example, can initiate the stress response — your brain gets the message that you are fleeing from danger! (43)

But twenty to thirty minutes of light aerobic activity like walking or biking will consume excess cortisol.

Mind-body exercises like yoga, tai chi and qi gong are excellent for stress relief as well. (44, 45)

4. Breathe Deeply for Stress Relief

You’ve probably noticed that when you’re under stress, your chest gets tight and your breathing becomes fast and shallow.

These are automatic reactions that are part of the stress response.

Unfortunately, most adults now breathe this way most of the time.

This in turn creates a feedback loop, perpetuating the stress response.

You can learn to reduce your physical reaction to stress by consciously breathing from your diaphragm.

A few minutes of diaphragmatic breathing, also known as abdominal or belly breathing, will lower levels of cortisol and stimulate the parasympathetic nervous system to induce a state of calm. (46)

To breathe from your diaphragm, focus on keeping your chest still while expanding your stomach.

You can place one hand on your chest and one on your stomach to help monitor your breaths.

By learning stress-relieving breathing, you can turn the mundane act of breathing into a cortisol reduction technique.

5. Use Proven Stress Reduction Techniques

Most stress reduction techniques work, in part, by lowering excess cortisol.

Here are a few evidence-based approaches you should consider:

Change Your Thinking

One of the most insidious sources of stress is your own mental chatter.

The average brain has 50,000 thoughts every day and, in most people, up to 70% of them are negative. (47, 48)

✓Eliminate Stress Train your brain to relieve the tension with self-hypnosis

The negative impact of stress is not due to the events in your life so much as it is due to your thoughts — your automatic negative reactions and cognitive distortions — about these events.

Positive self-talk and thinking can effectively reduce cortisol. (49)

Meditation

One of the best ways to gain more control of your thoughts is through meditation.

✓Zen12 Meditation Feel more relaxed, focused and creative in 12 minutes a day

Over 18,000 studies support the use of mindfulness meditation as one of the best daily habits for improving mental health and reducing stress. (50)

Meditation reduces cortisol while increasing feel-good brain chemicals like serotonin, GABA, and endorphins. (51, 52, 53)

EFT Tapping

Another mind-body stress relief technique is Emotional Freedom Technique, usually referred to as tapping.

It works on the same principles as acupuncture, except acupressure points are tapped with fingertips instead of inserting needles.

This is a simple technique that anyone can learn to do on their own.

Amazingly, just a single session of tapping can significantly reduce lower cortisol levels. (54)

Get Outside

Another easy way to reduce stress is to spend time outdoors.

Spending your lunch hour in a park is fantastic, but getting outside for even a few minutes can lower cortisol, blood pressure, and pulse rate. (55)

Power Nap

Taking a power nap in the afternoon is one of the easiest ways to decrease cortisol. (56)

It also offers one of the best returns on your investment of time.

A 20-minute nap can keep your mood, energy, creativity, and productivity high all day long and works even better than caffeine. (57)

And More …

Engaging in music (listening or actually playing) or making art (of any kind) can boost your mood and reduce chronic stress by lowering cortisol. (58, 59)

Lastly, stop multitasking and start focusing on doing one thing at a time.

Research confirms that trying to multitask is stressful and actually makes you less productive. (60) A lose-lose proposition.

Even the seemingly innocuous habit of frequently checking emails increases heart rate and cortisol levels. (61, 62)

How to Reduce Cortisol: The Bottom Line

Chronic stress leads to the continual release of the stress hormone cortisol.

And a chronically elevated level of cortisol is very harmful to your mental, cognitive, emotional and physical health and well-being.

Fortunately, you can reduce excess cortisol production by making stress reduction a priority.

Eating the right foods, taking the appropriate supplements, getting moderate physical exercise, learning to breathe properly, and employing stress reduction techniques like meditation and EFT can offset the damaging effects of cortisol and decrease its production.
