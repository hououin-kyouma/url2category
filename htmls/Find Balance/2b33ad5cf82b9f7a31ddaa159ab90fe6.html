Can I have a heart attack even if I live a healthy lifestyle?

SCAI

You might say to yourself, “Could I have a heart attack? I’ve never been sick. I eat a healthy diet. And I do not smoke.” While you are on the right track to heart health by making sound lifestyle choices, unfortunately you could still have a heart attack. People who have very few lifestyle risk factors for cardiovascular disease (CVD) sometimes have heart attacks because of factors that are beyond their control, such as a genetic predisposition for high cholesterol levels. That is why it is important to have regular check-ups with your physician. Identifying hereditary risk factors for cardiovascular disease early can help you and your physician decide together on a prevention plan, which might include, for example, medicines to lower LDL (or “bad”) cholesterol levels.

 

Deb Cordes on behalf of Honor Society of Nursing (STTI)

There are several risk factors for developing heart disease. Some of these risk factors you can reduce by lifestyle changes and some of the risk factors you cannot change. The risk factors that you can change or reduce the risk for development of a heart attack are: High cholesterol, high blood pressure, high blood sugar, smoking, lack of physical activity leading to obesity. These risk factors can be reduced by watching your diet and taking your medications for high cholesterol, high blood pressure and high blood sugar. Stop smoking if your a current smoker. Start to increase your physical activity and try to reduce your weight if you are overweight. Risk factors that you cannot change that could lead to a heart attack are age, gender, and race. As we age we increase our risk for heart disease this cannot be changed. If you are male you are more likely to develop heart disease at an earlier age. However, women who develop heart disease at an older age has a greater risk of dying. Family history of heart disease increases your chance of developing heart disease even if you live a healthy lifestyle. You cannot change your family history but you can continue to live a healthy lifestyle which will minimize the other risk factors.

Carolyn Thomas on behalf of Heart Sisters
I'm a heart attack survivor who was also a healthy distance runner for 19 years. I didn't smoke, didn't have issues with diabetes or cholesterol or family history or other major cardiac risk factors. But what I DID have was a pregnancy complication called "pre-eclampsia" while pregnant with my first baby decades before my heart attack struck.
We know that heart disease doesn't hit overnight, and that most cardiac events are 20-30 years in the making. We also now know that women with pregnancy complications like pre-eclampsia are 2-3 times more likely to develop heart disease, often years down the road.
What I had as well was a 30+ year high-stress, high-profile career in public relations, which (as most PR folks readily admit) means juggling multiple deadlines, all due yesterday, all while tap-dancing through life with that smiley face firmly pasted on! While I thrived on this relentless pace, and was good at what I did, living with chronic stress can wreak havoc on our coronary arteries. As Mayo Clinic cardiologists remind us: 
"The long-term activation of the stress-response system — and the subsequent overexposure to cortisol and other stress hormones — can disrupt almost all your body's processes. This puts you at increased risk of numerous health problems including heart disease."
While there are no magic diagnostic tools to determine categorically what did or did not cause heart attacks, it's reasonable to assume that these two particular risk factors did indeed affect my chances of one day having a cardiac event.
I believe, however, that my otherwise healthy lifestyle - particularly exercising! - helped me survive what many do not.

Heart Attack

Heart attack (myocardial infarction (MI), is the leading cause of death among Americans. It often results from coronary artery disease, the most common form of heart disease to affect adults. See your doctor immediately if you feel pressure or a squeezing sensation in your chest, neck, jaw, shoulders, back or arms, especially if it’s accompanied by sweating, nausea, vomiting, or shortness of breath.
