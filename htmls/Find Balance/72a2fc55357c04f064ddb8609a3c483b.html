How do I find work-life balance?

Straightforward answer: you don’t find it. You create it.

This whole work-life balance question is such a hot topic everywhere. And more than figuring out the answer, the most important question is why we are making such a big deal of it in the first place. And why is that? Because life happens. It happens every day, and then the next, and the day after that. Time doesn’t stop for anyone, and things start piling up. For example, we start a new job and want to get better at it. We work hard. And we do get better. But we find ourselves working late into the night, we don’t have time to prepare dinner so we eat out, and we start closing the door to those relationships in our life that do matter in the long run. And then there’s our health. Little sleep, junk food, a lot of stress that comes with big life changes, all of that adds up. Not a pretty picture! But really, like I said before, the answer is in your hands.

If this question bothers you at this point in your life and you really want to do something about it, consider these 3 ideas to create your own work-life balance.

And remember: it’s a personal thing. You might like one idea better than others, or maybe you’ll find another idea a real challenge to implement in your life. The key is to start with one thing and practice is long enough so it becomes a habit. That one thing can be all the difference you may need.

Here are 3 ideas to create your own work-life balance.

IDEA #1. Redesign your mornings.

Why is this important?

For many people, this time of day usually gets overlooked. We tend to hit the snooze button on the alarm until the only thing left is to jump out of bed. Then we rush to get ready, and rush some more to work or school, which adds up to a hectic commute. As we hurry out of the house, we might even forget to bring what we need, such as a packed lunch or healthy snack for energy. Instead, we get busy scrolling through Facebook, Twitter, or Instagram to check what other people are doing with their day. So it’s no surprise we end up feeling overwhelmed and stressed before the day even begins. Too much input, too little time to focus on what’s important!

How can you do this?

  • Reset your brain with simple meditation. It can help you deal better with the thousands of random thoughts that occupy you throughout the day and may contribute to your feeling stressed, rushed, and overwhelmed. Think of it as mental hygiene that can have long-term positive effects on how your brain functions. You can download the Headspace app and practice for only 10 minutes; it’s an easy way to make this mini habit stick.
  • Get moving. Pick a super short and targeted workout to help your body wake up and prepare for the work ahead. Here are some ideas for a 15 minute wake-up session: a morning yoga routine, a 15 minute bootcamp session, a set of hindu pushups, a set of sun salutation poses, or a 15 minute HIIT training session.
  • Have breakfast to jumpstart your day. A well-balanced breakfast will fill you up, give you energy, and improve cognitive function:
    • Oatmeal. Mix oats with 1 tablespoon flaxseeds, 1 teaspoon peanut butter, sliced banana or other fresh fruit, and some walnuts or almonds on top. Flaxseeds are an excellent source of alpha-linolenic acid (ALA), a healthy fat that boosts cerebral cortex function.
    • A parfait. Layer 1/2 cup of yogurt, 1 tablespoon granola, 1 cup fresh fruit (sliced or diced), and a spoonful of nuts such as walnuts and almonds. Almonds are beneficial for increased attention and awareness necessary for learning, as well as restoring memory and cognitive function.
    • Eggs. They are are a powerful mix of B vitamins (they help nerve cells to burn glucose), antioxidants (they protect neurons against damage), and omega-3 fatty acids (they keep nerve cells functioning at optimal speed).

IDEA #2. Turn your lunchtime into some “me time.”

Why is this important?

This time of day is also underestimated for most of us. It’s just a 30–60 minute break from work or school which we end up wasting on running to get lunch, waiting in line, and eating our food almost mechanically and absent-mindedly as we talk with colleagues or classmates. Then, before we know it, it’s time to get back to the classroom or office. Nothing special, right? But actually - that’s the problem. If we don’t take a real break from work, we won’t really feel like we are spending any time on ourselves; it will just be a continuation of what we’re focused on at the office or what we’re studying in school.

How can you do this?

  • Pack your own lunch. It’s easy to pack some leftovers from your dinner with a few fresh vegetables on the side that you can dress up with some olive oil and lemon juice, a dressing you like, or just have as a snack (think carrots, tomatoes, cucumber, lettuce, kale, olives, etc.). Or make a sandwich. Then find a place to eat, maybe while sitting on a bench in a park nearby, or somewhere where you can enjoy your meal and eat it mindfully. You will be focused on what you are eating, and you will feel like you’ve eaten to get more energy and not just to feel full.
  • Go for a walk. It's good to keep moving after a meal. It can help you digest your food, give your brain some much needed time to rest, and can also boost your energy levels. Aim for 20–30 minutes. It’s easier to fit into your lunch break, will energize you, and still leave you enough time to get back to your desk without rushing. Use a pedometer or one of the many apps you can download on your phone; most phones already come with a built in health app that automatically logs your steps daily. You will see how easily you can log the time and make progress every single day.
  • Feed your mind. Select something interesting to listen to on your walk. It can be an audio book or a podcast. Here are some podcast ideas:
    • Optimize with Brian Johnson: this podcast feels like getting an education in how to live smarter. It’s about gaining more wisdom in less time to help you live your greatest life. Brian condenses big ideas from the best books on optimal living and micro classes on how to apply these ideas. Look for the episode called The Power of WOOP, based on brain training research by Gabriele Oettingen, PhD. Then check out his micro classes on a variety of topics, from overcoming procrastination to how to become a straight A student.
    • The Model Health Show with Shawn Stevenson: author, nutritionist, and coach Shawn Stevenson leads a fun and entertaining show on many interesting topics related to health, fitness, and personal growth. Look for episodes on how to learn faster and increase focus with memory expert Jim Kwik (#197), how to embrace change and become emotionally agile with Dr. Susan David (#185), and how to stop the stress cycle with Dr. Pedram Shojai (#142).
    • The James Altucher Show: entrepreneur James Altucher leads conversations with writers and entrepreneurs. Look for episodes with Michael Singer, author of The Surrender Experiment (#119), the artist Jewel and writer of Never Broken: Songs Are Only Half the Story (#186), and Tony Robbins, personal growth expert, philanthropist, and author of many books —the latest is Unshakeable— on how to master your mind (#217).

IDEA #3. Carve out some time in the evenings to work on becoming the best version of yourself.

Why is this important?

Because becoming the best version of yourself should be a lifestyle, not an end goal. In other words, everything you do that is dedicated to your own self-improvement should be your priority. This does not mean that you should ignore your responsibilities, whether that’s devoting time to your family, finishing up homework for school, or making progress on a work project with a fast-approaching deadline. But it does mean you should find ways to be more strategic with your time. The key idea here is to devote some time every day to yourself. It doesn’t have to be three hours of learning a new skill each night. In fact, to make this habit stick, it’s best if you start with just 30 minutes in the evening, maybe after dinner or before bedtime when things are more quiet.

How can you do this?

  • Start by first identifying an important goal. What would you like to accomplish at this point in your life? Why is this goal important to you? Making a personal connection with what you want to be successful in is critical in maintaining motivation. Be honest with yourself as to why this goal is something very personal to you, and not because other people think it “should” be important to you - remember, this exercise is meant to give you a greater balance of your work and your life.
  • Next, devote some time to thinking about your core strengths. Which skills do you possess right now, what do you excel at, what needs more work, which skills would you like to develop but haven’t yet started working on? Being realistic about your strengths is an excellent starting point, so that you can figure out what needs more work in order to become better.
  • Finally, move towards your goal in small steps each evening. Now that you’ve identified a goal and pinpointed the core skills you either possess or would like to work on, it’s time to put the plan in motion. Start by thinking about the following things.
    • How can you combine your goal with your skill set? For example, can you attend an online course or watch a YouTube tutorial to learn how to do something?
    • Where can you find books to read about a topic or subject related to your goal? Go to the library or the nearest bookstore and give yourself a few hours to do some preliminary research.
    • Is there someone you know who is a master in your field of interest, who could be your mentor? Reach out to them with a specific question to ask for help.

Thinking through these questions will give you more focus, help you stay on track, and provide you with a greater work-life balance simply because you are giving yourself the permission to make small steps, every day, towards your future. And that, right there, is where you actually begin to make change.

Originally Answered: What are the best ways to maintain a true work/life balance?
The first question to ask is what true work/life balance looks like to you. Does it mean that when you leave the office you don't think about work until the next day? Does it mean your job and your home life are integrated and indiscernible?

No scenario is wrong as long as you and your employer are happy. Everyone has their own expectations and preferences around what a balanced and satisfied life looks like.

The next step is to find a career and employer that fit your idea of balance. Perhaps it's a company with a generous time off policy that discourages people from staying late. Perhaps it's a career that allows you to work from home, or a workplace that encourages socialization outside the office and invites friends and loved ones into the company dynamic. Do some research into what it's really like to work at a company (Quora is a great place to do that kind of research!)

Once you've found a company and career that fit your life, the rest is up to you. Here are some tips:

  • Get organized. The demands of work and life can add up quickly, especially if you have a family, an active social life, or are self-employed. Keep a calendar that includes both personal and professional engagements so you can keep track of everything. And don't forget to schedule some me-time in there.
  • Prioritize. No matter where you work, there will be times when events overlap, demands pile up, and you find you just can't do it all. That's when your priorities come into play (and your ability to say "no"). Part of having a balanced life is knowing how to balance your own wants and needs. This isn't about making others happy. If you don't prioritize yourself, who will?
  • Know when to walk away. Sometimes, you find your dream job at your dream company, only to find yourself working 60-hour work weeks, missing major family milestones, and struggling to keep up with the stress. If this is the case, it's time to revisit some of your priorities and see what steps you can take to regain balance. Maybe this means having a conversation with your boss, re-organizing your calendar, or even striking out on a new career. The important part is that you don't stay put in an unhealthy work environment.

The pursuit work/life balance never ends. It's something we strive for throughout our lives, as personal and professional demands change, our priorities change, and our world changes. What's important is that you recognize what makes you feel fulfilled and satisfied at the end of each day, and go after those things whenever possible.
