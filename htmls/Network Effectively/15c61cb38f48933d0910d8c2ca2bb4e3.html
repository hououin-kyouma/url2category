Graduation is coming – Networking like a Pro

Discovering who you know and maximizing those relationships is key to finding an engaging (post college) role.

If I could go back 20 years and tell my early-graduate-self one thing, I would say, “Build and maintain meaningful relationships as you start looking for your first job.” I think most people miss (myself included) the importance of professional relationships when looking for something new.  It’s this network that’s really going to help you make that transition into the real world.

I’ve found many graduates struggle with a couple key components to building a professional network.  First, students don’t even know where to start and second, they’re not sure how to maximize the relationship once a connection is made.

Thank goodness for Engaged Pursuit, because we’ve created some tips and tricks to tackle both these difficult questions.

 Where do I even start?

Maximize your university resources.  There’s tons of resources usually at your fingertips from your Career Center and/or Alumni Network.  See if your school has a dedicated Linked-In or Facebook page (more on Social Media below). Also, don’t be afraid to ask your professors for help. If you’re completing a major like Business or Finance, these folks usually have great contacts for you to consider.  This group wants you to be successful, so usually they are super helpful.

Ask your parents for help.  I know, I know.  Who wants to ask them for help?? But it’s really important as you start making the transition out of school.  Mom and Dad may have awesome relationships for you to leverage as you’re thinking about what’s next. Maybe even ask to sit with them and review their Linked-In network if they’re well connected – it can’t hurt!

Check-out social (and professional) networks.  Facebook and Linked-In are amazing resources for research.  If you don’t have a profile on Linked In yet, don’t worry – we’ll cover this next week.  Start by creating a “shell” account and start researching companies – at least you’ll see if any alumni are working at companies that interest you.  That’s a huge start.

Go to local events.  If your school is hosting a Career Fair (even if it’s small) – GO!  You’ll be able to get a sense of who’s hiring now and start practicing your introduction to complete strangers.  Even if you’re a total introvert (like me) it’s important that you start putting yourself out there professionally! And don’t forget to grab business cards as you network!

How do I maximize this new relationship?

Don’t think the relationship has to be perfect. You’re not going to match with everyone, so don’t stress trying to come across perfect. Professionals know you are new to this and most are willing to help.  You are going to find people you click with and others who you don’t – that’s all part of the process.

Come prepared.  While you shouldn’t stress about the relationship, that doesn’t mean you shouldn’t come prepared.  Have an agenda prior and make sure you’re on-time.  And keep any initial conversation to 45 mins or less.

Clarify how you want the individual to help.  Usually, someone will ask “So how can I help?” at the end of a conversation – make sure you have an answer. You could ask for a referral, ask for insight into the hiring process, or simply ask for visibility into new opportunities. Keep it simple.

Ask for their feedback/advice. What an awesome resource right in front of you!  Ask them for feedback on your approach, your resume, and/or what path they took out of school.  Again, most folks want to help – remember that!

Say Thank You!  A written thank you (email is fine) is crucial as you’re building new relationships. Do it.

Your homework this week: Go through the “Where do I even start?” section as see if you can build-out a list of potential new contacts from each of the areas highlighted. We’ll want to have a list of at least 15 people available as you start to think about your career plan.
