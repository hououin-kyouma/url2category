How to Network Like a Pro (Without Feeling Awkward)

May 13, 2016 1:27 PM
by Marissa Miller
Photographed by Toni Frissell, Vogue , February 1938

When Judy Robinett walks into a room, she saunters around its perimeter, making sure to address everyone within five feet of her. There’s always a joke in her mental Rolodex, because she learned firsthand that’s your “in” to solidifying a genuine networking contact. That’s why it’s surprising to learn that Robinett—author of How to Be a Power Connector and an expert on networking—used to be painfully shy.

“We’ve all been trained that strangers are danger,” she says. In truth, strangers are your most valuable career assets. They can introduce you to new opportunities, show you the tricks of the trade, and act as sounding boards for new ideas.

But combine social anxiety with the pervasive belief that there’s something inherently wrong with you if you’re an introvert and suddenly networking sounds easier in theory than in practice. Worried about coming out of the shy closet? Nearly half of adults identify as shy, so you’re not alone.

Here’s how to crack the code to overcoming your worst networking fears.

Talk Yourself Up
We all subconsciously emit signals of insecurity, Robinett says, and those little tics can inadvertently hinder your networking prowess. Whether you’re about to get onstage for a big presentation or if you’re feeling daunted by the thought of something as minute as surviving industry happy hour, she suggests repeating a mantra like: “I’m strong and powerful. Feelings of insecurity aren’t facts.”

Robinett also suggests asking your best friends to dish out three or four of your top qualities. From this simple (and ego-stroking) exercise, she notes “everyone else sees things and we’re the last to know.” So what are you waiting for? Rally the troops for the highest form of flattery.

Make a Memorable First Move
Stroll around the room. If someone is five feet away from you, you have to acknowledge them, says Robinett. Don’t feel pressured to be extroverted if that isn’t who you are. First, smile and say, “Hello, how are you?” Then woo them with a fun story about your pet or child or where you grew up. Networking is a lot like courtship, in that if you get them laughing, the connection has a lot more potential than it would if you blandly listed your résumé.

Steal Robinett’s genius trick for staying cool as a cucumber: “I pretend the person is a best friend. If I knew them already, how would I act toward them?”

Reframe the Idea of Networking
The word networking itself feels mechanical, clouding our perspective of how we should be approaching it. Think about every important person in your network who was once completely foreign to you, from your best friend to your spouse. If you humanize an industry professional in your mind, it’ll take loads of the edge off—no social lubrication required. And that’s a good thing: Research from the University of Michigan found holding a drink not only makes you appear less intelligent, it can reduce your cognition.

If It’s Not Going Well
In a perfect world, approaching strangers would lead to new business ventures left, right, and center. But often, the compatibility isn’t palpable right off the bat. If someone is coming off as aloof, panicking won’t solve your case. “Don’t internalize it. It has nothing to do with you. They could be having diarrhea or going through a breakup. Everyone has problems. When I was younger, I thought everyone had the perfect life, but that’s not true,” Robinett says. If you’re worried about your approach, consider practicing on people you run into in line at Sweetgreen or in the bulk section at Whole Foods. You get to learn all about their quinoa purchases, they get treated to a cheerful conversation. Win-win.

Have a Buddy System
You’d take a wingwoman to the bar with you, so why not to your next networking event? If you’re shy, tag along with someone you deem a “power connector” to break the ice. Before the event, talk to your friend about having her stick around for the first couple minutes, and then have her slyly speak to someone else. This way, you’re not relying on anyone as a clutch. And just as two girls mingling at a bar might be a deterrent for others to approach you, be mindful of your body language when you’re interacting with your friend one-on-one. You don’t want to ward off potential contacts by seeming closed off.

Reach Out Online
Forging meaningful connections can be tricky while you’re both sitting behind screens, but that initial nonthreatening exchange can be your foot in the door.

Say said future contact belongs to an animal rights community on LinkedIn, a passion of yours since you were young. “It’s an honest bond and common ground,” Robinett says. By steering the dialogue—initially, at least—in a non-work-related direction, they won’t get the sense you’re trying to use them strictly for your career.

No one likes to spend their workday chatting aimlessly, so find a way to elegantly segue into career talk. Try “I’m so impressed you did X, Y, and Z, and we share this in common. Congratulations on your success. I’d love to connect.” You can also move the digital relationship to in-person by inquiring about any upcoming industry events or inviting them for coffee.

Battle Impostor Syndrome
Research shows this phenomenon of feeling like a fraud is most common among high-achieving women (that’s you, duh!). But the negative self-talk isn’t founded in reality. The big-deal exec you’re afraid to approach? They were afraid to make the first move toward someone they admired at some point in their career, too. Once you acknowledge that you are your own worst enemy (as in, no one is saying harmful things about yourself other than you!), you’ll regain control, and maybe even a valuable new industry contact.
