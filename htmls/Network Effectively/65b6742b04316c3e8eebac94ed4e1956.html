5 Innovative Ways to Improve Your Business Network

  • Posted by Rebecca Gray on April 10, 2014 at 11:36am
  • View Blog

Before the rise of the Internet and social media, business networking generally involved boring business-card exchange parties that so many people considered a necessary evil. While networking meetings still abound, these days the word “network” is just as likely to bring to mind thoughts of social networking sites such as Linkedin, and the latest networking apps for mobile devices. One thing hasn’t changed, however: if you want to increase your business and expand your client base, it’s important to optimize your business network. The best way to do that involves using the “old” ways as well as embracing the new opportunities for networking.

1. Optimize your social media engagement. Facebook and Twitter have their place in building a business network, furnishing efficient ways to communicate directly with customers and others interested in your goods and services. But professional networking sites such as Linkedin are also critical. Linkedin provides an optimal platform for communicating with colleagues and vendors (as well as potential clients, in some cases), and enhancing your standing in the business community. It’s important that once you’ve set up a Linkedin profile, you keep it updated and engage regularly. Join relevant forums and/or start your own, and participate on a regular basis. While social media aren’t everything, they’re important pieces of the business network puzzle.

 2. Get out in the “real world.” At some point you have to move away from the computer and go bravely into the “real world” – which may mean attending networking meetings, trade shows, and other industry events. Social media and emailing are good ways to make initial contact, but to really develop professional relationships with colleagues, vendors, or clients; you eventually need to meet them in person.

 As you build your network, savor the adventure of meeting new people and thinking about what you can offer them. Listen a lot, ask questions, and allow yourself to become thoroughly engaged in interactions with others. Have an elevator pitch at the ready, if it helps you under pressure.  And practice it in front of a mirror until it comes naturally.

3. Be inclusive in building your network. Whether you’re doing it “old-school” or “new-school,” setting your networking sights on only the highest level executives or the wealthiest clients may be a disservice to your effort. This isn’t to say that you should be indiscriminate, but you should be as inclusive as possible. You never know where contacts will prove valuable, so don’t miss opportunities by being overly selective.

4. Stay in touch in the way that works best for you and the other party. Making the effort to reach out to people is futile without follow-up. Do whatever you are most comfortable doing and whatever seems appropriate – a phone call, a friendly email, a handwritten note, a casual visit to the person’s office, or a private message through Linkedin or Facebook. And make it clear that your outreach efforts are not conditional, that you’re not asking for or expecting favors. The key point is to convey to the other party that you felt your interaction, however brief, was worthy of a follow-up.

 5. Use technology to bolster your networking and follow-up efforts. Too many people go to networking events and make mutual promises to keep in touch, but nothing ever comes of it. And in most cases it’s not due to laziness or indifference, but rather to the normal distractions of busy modern life. Fortunately there are numerous apps and mobile tools that can make your networking and follow-up efforts a breeze. Plan ahead by setting schedules for contacting people.  Enter their contact info and set an alarm, so you know when your follow-up is due. And as a matter of course, as soon as possible after meeting a promising contact, enter his or her information into your contact list, so you'll never lose a business card or networking opportunity.

One thing to keep in mind about networking is that it’s a two-way street. You aren’t just in the game to benefit yourself, but also to support colleagues and business contacts. In other words, it’s as important to give as it is to receive.

Networking alone won’t grow your business. You also need to make sure your products and services are the best they can be, that you treat your employees as well as your customers (with care and respect), and that you’re advertising effectively. But if your business is to grow and improve, you must never let up on networking efforts.

Rebecca Gray writes for Backgroundchecks.org.
