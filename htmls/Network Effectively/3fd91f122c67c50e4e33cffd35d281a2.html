10 ways to network like a pro at app developer conferences

Like death, taxes, and having to upgrade to the latest iOS version, networking at an app developer conference can’t be avoided. However, just because networking can be unpleasant, doesn’t mean you should give up on it. Networking at developer conferences can yield big wins for your app, including new users, valuable feedback, bug identification, and press coverage, if you’re prepared. Thus, the key to successful networking at developer conferences is to take time to prepare your networking “plan of attack” before the show. So, let’s get started!

The methods below can quickly improve your networking chops (and also make things less awkward), yet only require a little research and practice. The Adscend Media team has personally practiced these methods at shows like Mobile World Congress, Apps World, and GDC with strong results. With these methods, along with your passion and intimate knowledge of your app, you should be able to go from networking Padawan to Jedi in no time!

10 Ways to Network Like a Pro at App Developer Conferences

Find YOUR Party: Before a conference, do a quick search on Meetup.com, Eventbrite, or by using the conference hashtag for parties and events. The variety of events at developer conferences is incredible, including meetups at barcades, karaoke, sightseeing outings, and sticker swaps. It’s not just cheesy nightclubs. There really is something for everyone, and you will be amazed about how much better networking feels in an environment that you’re more comfortable in.

Research the Venue: While you’re searching for conference-related events, make sure to research each venue. Not only will this allow you to gauge the distance to the venue, it also helps you decide on whether a networking event is worth your time (maybe you aren’t a fan of San Francisco’s Best EDM Nightclub). For example, networking parties at Mobile World Congress in Barcelona last year were spread out, requiring 20–30 min cab rides. Thus, you can save valuable time by making sure the event is worth the trip.

Schedule Meetings: Securing meetings with aligned attendees, such as attendees from app review blogs, industry press and associations, and advertising solutions ensures a better conference experience. Starting a month out from the conference, begin searching the conference attendee list, social groups, hashtags, and other conference channels for valuable attendees, speakers, and exhibitors (developer shows require a longer lead time, as international attendees often book their scheduled out in advance). These channels should yield you a decent amount of meeting targets. Most conferences also list their social channels on their site, e.g. Mobile World Congress Social Media. However, if you aren’t finding decent contacts, LinkedIn often yields useful groups like this AnDevCon group or this WWDC group.

Once you’ve found a few folks who you would like to meet with, it’s time to reach out to schedule a meeting. When requesting a meeting, remember Ben Franklin’s advice in that “Honesty is the best policy.” There’s no need to be slick, simply give a little info about your app, explain how a meeting would benefit the attendee, and ask for their availability.

Build a Better Business Card: When designing cards for the show, make sure your card includes your app store URL, website, a brief synopsis or slogan that describes your app (e.g. OCR Business Card Scanner, The Premier 1920’s Zombie RPG), and a logo/image. Essentially, you need enough info to quickly jog the memory of anyone you met with, or risk getting your card discarded. It also helps to have a unique card, as the app industry revolves around creativity. If you’re looking to create something truly different, check out 50 Unique Business Cards That Will Make Your Mind Explode for some inspiration.

Look for Nonverbal Cues: Whether at the conference itself, or at a conference networking party, knowing who is in the mood to hear about your app can be a challenge. Therefore, before you strike up a conversation, give the room a quick scan for nonverbal cues. For example, two people facing each other, chatting closely, probably aren’t receptive to conversation. However, that guy smiling, quietly sipping his Coors Light, is definitely ready to chat (or maybe drunk?). The Wall Street Journal does a great job breaking down these nonverbal cues (with pictures!).

Use Conversation Starters: Starting a conversation with, “My app is…” probably isn’t going to be received well and makes you look like Joe or Jane Salesperson. The goal is to relate, not to sell. Thus, an easy remedy to “break the ice” when networking is to use a conversation starter. We’re not talking anything too wordy, or too rehearsed here, just a quick question, statement, or observation. As you will be able to see from this Daily Muse article, even a simple comment about the temperature of the venue can get a conversation started.

Listen: It’s just human nature to talk about ourselves. Or, in this case, talk about our apps. However, actually taking the time to listen to the person you talking with (as opposed to just waiting for a convo break to talk more about your app) can pay huge dividends. Listening allows you to see how you can help the other person achieve his or her conference objectives, which actually increases the chances of that person helping you achieve your conference objectives. Note that you may not be able to help each other, but you may gain feedback about your app, or at least make a new connection just by listening.

Wear it Proud: Make sure you wear your conference badge whenever you are inside your hotel lobby, Starbucks, an Irish pub, or anywhere close to the conference. Sure, wearing your conference badge makes you look like a newb–but it also signals that you are open to conversation and gives you a nice “in” with fellow badge wearers. For example, last year in San Francisco at GDC, the Chipotle next to the Moscone Center became a nice networking hub, with communal tables helping badge wearers easily connect over burrito bowls.

Bring Sweet Merch: Want people to remember your app? Bribe them with some merch (merchandise)! Being that you’re attending a mobile-focused show, obviously power banks, covers, stands, or anything for a mobile device will tie nicely into the event’s theme. However, you don’t have to go the mobile route for your merch. Your goal is to ensure that your promo items will be used often, or are unique enough, that they can’t be thrown away. Check out 5 Tips for Choosing the Right Promotional Product for help finding a promo item that will keep your app top of mind after the conference.

Give a Reward: Your app may sound interesting, but there are millions of apps available and in term of frequent use, most people only use about three apps. It’s going to take a little extra to get you someone to try your app. As seen in recent trends with app ads, and with the success of monetization solutions like offer walls, users prefer to be rewarded for their time. Therefore, if you want people to spend a few minutes with your app, you need to give them something in return. This reward doesn’t have to be much, but it should have some sort of immediate perceived value. For example, a code for a free premium app feature works well, such as a new weapon for a shooter game or a few free filters in a photo app.

TLDR Summary

Networking at app developer conferences is essential to grow your app. These shows feature thousands of attendees from around the world, with an attendee profile spanning from user to industry press. Thus, by using a few smart strategies (and enduring some awkward conversations), your time will definitely be well spent. Hopefully, this article makes networking easier and will help you get value at your next conference. If you still have networking questions, however, feel free to leave a comment below!
