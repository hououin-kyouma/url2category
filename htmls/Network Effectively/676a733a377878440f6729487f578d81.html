Three Ways to Network Like a Pro

Since arriving at The Washington Center (TWC), I've heard the word "networking" used repeatedly. It's a buzzword. Everyone talks about networking as an opportunity to meet new people, make business connections and have a professional presence in the D.C. community. But until last week, I had not had an opportunity to try it out for myself.

On Thursday, I attended a networking event hosted by the local chapter of the National Lesbian & Gay Journalists Association welcoming NPR's Joshua Johnson to the city. At the event, I was able to talk to several different professionals in my field and learn a thing or two about networking along the way. Here are a few of guidelines I stuck to that helped make the evening a success.


Pictured: (Left to Right) Event organizers, NLGJA National Office staff, NPR's Joshua Johnson

 

1. Show genuine interest in other people

We all know when someone is completely uninterested in what you're saying. Inability to keep eye contact, the classic glazed-over look and constant fidgeting indicate that the person we're talking to would rather be somewhere else. Showing interest in what others are saying, however, does not apply only to friends and acquaintances. It is an essential part of networking. Faking it is not enough. Be interested in what others are saying, and they will be more likely to listen to what you're saying. It also means you will be remembered as a good listener and a polite person.

 

2. Be ready to talk about your goals and interests

Unlike a party at a friend's house or dinner with acquaintances, there is often a limited amount of time to talk to the other attendees at a networking event. Make the most of the few minutes you spend talking to each person. This does not mean rushing around from person to person handing out business cards (don't do that.) It means being intentional about the conversation. Small talk is great, but don't forget why you came.

 

3. Don't expect something from everyone

The majority of people at a networking event have little to offer you in the short term. That does not mean building a relationship with that person is not important. By not expecting something of every person you speak to, you'll feel much less pressure to turn conversations into business transactions. Instead, you'll be able to relax and enjoy yourself. It will make the event much more fun and will make you more approachable.


As the semester progresses at TWC, I look forward to taking advantage of other professional events and opportunities to meet people in my field. And I hope to continue learning to be more confident and engaging when I interact with professionals. Best of luck on your networking adventures!

-T
