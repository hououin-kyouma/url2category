Learn from My Mistakes: Improve Your Networking Skills

Anne Handley-FierceAugust 1, 2014
Comments
Share
Share
Share
Email
I admit it: I’m not the most natural networker. In the past, I’ve come away from events having spoken to only the people I already knew and the waitstaff. Shyness, social anxiety, and not knowing exactly what to say have prevented me from making friends and useful contacts. But, dear reader, I’ve gotten better. I’ve also come to the realization that most people feel exactly the same way about networking that I do, even if they don’t show it. I’m here to give you the benefit of my experience. Improve your networking skills by learning from my mistakes—and avoiding them.

Find Meetups

I spent years ignoring meetups and events because I didn’t know what to do at them. I would see an event for writers, publishing industry people, or the children’s literature community and just ignore it. Or I wouldn’t even know it was happening because I didn’t make any effort to find out what was going on in my community. Luckily, fellow writers J.D. Ho and Anna Staniszewski started dragging me to events and helping me improve my networking skills. If not for them, I would probably have far fewer writing friends and professional connections.

What you should do: Start searching. Boston has an organization called Bookbuilders of Boston, which holds networking events. Find out if there’s something similar in your town. Search meetup boards and look for conferences in your area. If you’re not sure whether the event is right for you, go anyway.

Go with Your Friends…

I can’t remember the last event I went to without knowing at least one friend would be there. While it would be great if I could muster up the courage to brazenly go to conferences and meetups alone, I know that’s not going to happen.

What you should do: Call your friends. Reach out to your LinkedIn connections, or post on Facebook. Ask if anyone is going. Don’t assume that your friends are going to ask you. They might be waiting for you to ask them.

…But Don’t Spend All Night with Them

Last winter, I spent most of a meetup talking to three friends that I see every week. While it was great to hang out with them, we can do that any time. How many new people did I meet that night? One. And while I’m happy to have met and connected with her, there were at least 30 additional people that I ignored. Oops.

What you should do: Split up from your friends. Or if that’s too scary, approach people in pairs. Try to ensure that you spend at least 75 percent of the evening talking to people that you don’t already know.

Make Conversation

Recently, I went up to two people at a networking event and said, “Hi, I’m Anne.” There was dead silence. Apart from the fact that the people I approached were less than friendly, I also left it up to them to start the conversation. I said nothing about myself, and I asked nothing about them. It was an awkward minute or two, and then I went to look for a drink. Not a successful endeavor.

What you should do: This is probably the hardest of all the networking skills. What are you supposed to say to people? Honestly, the best answer is, “kind of anything.” If you’re near the food, crack a joke about the strange cheese selection. Talk about the conference or meetup. Is it their first time coming to one? Ask what they do for a living. Offer your own information. Don’t limit your conversation to your career and your industry. Remember this: Everyone at the event came to meet people and make new connections. Some people will be social butterflies, but most will feel just as unsure as you do. If they’re like me, they’ll be grateful that someone else did the work of introducing themselves, and they’ll be happy to chat.

Lauren MacLeod of the Strothman Agency recommends making a human connection first. Get to know people as people in addition to as professionals. Remember, you use your networking skills to make connections and widen your professional network. Who would you rather introduce to your other contacts—the person who joked with you about the strange music selection at the café and sent you a link to a great website? Or the person who spoke to you for 10 minutes about their accomplishments and didn’t show any interest in you?

Bring Business Cards

Reader, I must confess, up until 15 minutes before writing this article, I didn’t have business cards. When I went to meet people at events, I had nothing. Once, a friend showed me her personal cards and suggested I get some. This was about eight years ago.

Instead, when I was lucky, the person I was talking to had cards and I could follow up later by emailing or following them on Twitter. When I was unlucky, I wrote my email on scraps of paper, wrote my information on the other person’s card (hoping that they didn’t then give it to someone else), or Facebook-added someone on my phone. These methods are terrible, and really unprofessional.

What you should do: Get business cards. They’re available online at a lot of price points, and they come quickly. At minimum, include your name, email, Twitter, and personal website or portfolio. If you can’t give people your information, they can’t contact you later.

Remember, reader, that networking goes both ways, You hope to meet contacts that can introduce you to people, suggest opportunities, or even hire you for a writing gig. At the same time, you should be helping your connections in the same way.
