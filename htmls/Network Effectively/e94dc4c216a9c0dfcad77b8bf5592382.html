How to Network Your Way to the Job

From constantly having to apply for tons of openings, to going to interview after interview, searching for a new career can feel like a full-time job of its own, minus the salary. Add networking to the picture and it can sometimes be a bit overwhelming. Where exactly should you be networking? What should you be prepared to say? How can you do it all and build genuine relationships? Don’t let it stress you out. With these few tips and tricks, you can improve your networking skills and get hired.

Find the right events for you.

Deciding which networking events are right for you, or where to even begin looking for them can sometimes be a difficult task, but it doesn’t have to be. Here are a few options that might help:

  • Join professional associations that hold networking events. A quick Google search can point you in the right direction of industry-specific networking organizations. Once you join, you will be looped into newsletters and invited to events that give you a chance to meet people who share common interests and professions, and can help propel your career.

  • Sign up for networking sites. Websites and apps offer a quick and easy way to connect right from your cell phone. Whether you want to discuss politics, books, games or more, these apps and websites are a great option to bring like-minded people together, and offer perfect opportunities for networking.

  • Build your own networking group. Start by inviting some of your friends to your home or a coffeehouse and tell them to invite a few friends of their own. Those friends can reach out to their associates and so on. It’s one way to organically build genuine connections within your circle, and if a career opportunity comes up within that circle, you can be top-of-mind.

Focus on your approach.

Your attitude going into a networking event plays a big part in how the people you meet perceive you and the connections you do or do not make. Instead of attending an event with the intention of making a ton of professional contacts and focusing on what you can gain from the individuals you meet, look at it as an opportunity to make genuine connections. Be fully present while the people you meet are speaking, and think about what you can bring to the table. This is not an interview, but instead a chance to build a lasting bond with people, so be sincere. They will be more inclined to take you seriously and to move forward with building a relationship with you if they think you genuinely want to get to know them.

Be prepared with an elevator pitch.

When you’re at different networking events, one of the first questions you will be asked is, “What do you do?”  It’s important to be able to answer this question with a concise, couple-sentence response that explains who you are, what your position is within an organization, what that position entails, and why you’re passionate about what you do. Keep it to 20-30 seconds, so you don’t monopolize the conversation or bore the other person. A great elevator pitch is one way to make a lasting first impression.  

Make sure to follow up.

Once you’ve attended a networking event and made some connections, it’s essential to follow up. While there, ask people how they prefer to stay in touch. Should you email, send a LinkedIn request, or follow them on Twitter? Find out and act accordingly, preferably about 24 hours after the event. If you wait too long, he or she might not remember you, but you also don’t want to bombard the individual by following up directly after the event. Staying in touch once you make that initial connection is a crucial part of networking and building a bond that encourages people to keep you in mind when career opportunities come up.

 

Sources: Kathryn Minshew, The Muse; Marie Forleo, marieforleo.com
