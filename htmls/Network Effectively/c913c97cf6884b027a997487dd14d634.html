Professional networking

Professional networking

‌Networking is about making connections and building enduring, mutually beneficial relationships.

These connections are potentially the key to a future career. People do business with those they like and trust.

Your network can become a great resource giving you access to the hidden jobs market and great industry insights that will be really helpful when making applications and doing interviews.

Face to face

Networking events are a great way to meet new people. The Careers Service runs a wide range of events allowing you to meet people from different sectors. External events can also provide great opportunities to meet people in the industry you want to work in.


Get advice from alumni

The best people to give you advice about your job search are those who have been through the same experience.

Glasgow Careers Alumni Network connects you online and in person with our global alumni community. 

  • Attend events with graduates who are industry experts
  • Sign up to The Network, our exclusive platform that connects you with 100s of alumni who could be your future employers

Jobseeking using LinkedIn

LinkedIn

Join the University of Glasgow Careers Network Group

Many networking sites serve the dual purpose of facilitating communication within both social and professional networks. LinkedIn is strictly a professional networking site.

LinkedIn’s popularity is easy to understand given its usefulness as a careers and recruitment resource. As a job seeker you can:

  • Search for jobs and internships
  • Receive items of news by joining professional interest groups
  • Build your professional network by connecting with fellow group members and alumni
  • Get yourself noticed by employers through your contributions to interest groups
  • Discover the career paths followed by those currently in jobs you would like to do
  • Research employers
  • Identify employers that have recruited University of Glasgow graduates in selected countries

Tips for an effective LinkedIn profile 

LinkedIn have produced a great series of videos specifically for students on how to produce an effective profile, and how to use LinkedIn successfuly.

There’s no such thing as the perfect profile. What is right for one employer might not be perfect for another.

You have a lot of flexibility in how to write your profile, but it's vital that your profile is easy to find and easy to read.  This means it has to be complete - so you will appear near the top of their searches. It also needs to be concise so that they will find your key messages with the minimum of time and effort.

Tips on being concise when writing for LinkedIn

Eliminate redundancies

Find and remove parts of your writing that offer repeat information, e.g. multiple claims of the same transferable skills.

Use the active voice

Using the active voice emphasises the actor rather than the action, e.g.

Passive:  The planning process required me to liaise with members of parliament

Active:  Liaised with members of parliament

Drop the personal pronouns

Using personal pronouns on CVs and LinkedIn profiles leads to lengthy and repetitive text. Compare the two entries below:

With personal pronouns:  I have held various positions in a variety of organizations and societies as a student, which have expanded my knowledge, built my confidence and improved my communication skills, developed my leadership and teamwork skills.

Without personal pronouns: Held various positions in student organisations and societies, which have expanded knowledge, built confidence, improved communication, leadership, and teamwork skills.

Avoid wordy phrases

Be careful not use whole phrases where single words would do, e.g.

…on the basis that… → since

Despite the fact that… → although


Job seeking using Twitter

Twitter

Follow GUcareers on Twitter

‌Twitter is one of the top ten most-visited sites on the internet. This level of popularity means that recruiters can promote their job opportunities to a global audience, free of charge.

Job seekers can access a world of opportunities when they sign in and it’s important to keep your account professional when using Twitter for job searching. 

Tips for using Twitter

Create a professional profile

It’s important to present a professional image in your profile. Choose a professional-looking photograph and include a job pitch in your bio. Twitter profiles are very brief, so it also makes sense to include a link to your LinkedIn profile.

Stay up to date in your chosen sector by following key individuals and employers

To make sure you see information that is relevant to your job search, it’s important to follow the right users. Find key individuals and organisations in your field and also find out who they follow. The list of users you follow can say a lot about your interests and motivations, so be sure to choose well if you want to impress employers.

Follow job feeds and use search tools

Use the Twitter search box to find job adverts or Twitter accounts that deliver job feeds. Lots of employers have dedicated accounts for job adverts – try searching for the employer’s name and “jobs”. Many third-party agents, such as newspapers and recruitment sites, also maintain their own job feeds.

Use hashtags to increase industry knowledge and find jobs

Take notice of hashtags that people in your industry are using to find discussions on the sector’s hot topics. Graduate recruiters are also likely to use hashtags in tweets to advertise jobs. Using the same hashtags can help you in your job search. If your tweet is interesting and well-written, other users could retweet your post to their own followers.

Raise your profile

Employers use social networking sites to headhunt suitable candidates for opportunities that may not be advertised openly. The more contributions you make on Twitter, the higher your chances of being approached will become.

Get involved with relevant online discussions, retweet interesting news items or quotes and post questions for recruiters. If others see with your contribution you can gain new industry followers.


Protecting your online identity

Social networking can be a powerful tool for learning more about prospective employers, but beware - it's a two way street. Recruiters are increasingly turning to the web to learn more about their applicants, so you should put your best face forward.

Google yourself

Doing a search for your own name in a search engine like Google will tell you a lot of about whether your privacy settings on different social networks are appropriate. 

Having a publicly visible profile is not necessarily a bad thing, but you need to make sure that what others are seeing represents you fairly and professionally. For example, publicly available YouTube videos or personal blogs are fine in theory, but if the content may be seen as rude or unprofessional it is best to make it private or delete it. Would you want a recruiter to see it?

Use different networks for different purposes

Each social network has a slightly different audience, purpose and feel. You’ll get the most out of social networking if you stick with some typical conventions about what belongs where:

Facebook is usually for family and friends. You should lock the privacy settings down tight, but still be careful about what you post. You might also consider sharing with your friends and family that you are looking for a job.

Twitter is used for just about everything, but remember: All tweets are public by default. You may wish to make separate personal and professional accounts – keep your professional account public and change the privacy settings of your personal account so that only approved friends and family can view it.

LinkedIn’s entire purpose is to facilitate professional networking for employment, so keep your profile strictly business. Don’t just replicate your Facebook network; only connect to your friends if it has professional value.

Check your privacy settings

By default, social networks profiles are as publicly accessible as possible - it makes it easier for friends and family (and marketing companies) to find you. However, it can also expose areas of your life that you'd rather not share with potential employers.

You may be surprised to realise just how much of your profile is wide open for anyone to see. For example, your public Facebook data might reveal:

  • Your photos: A tasteful profile picture is fine, but do you really want prospective employers to see photos of nights out and holidays with friends?
  • Your likes: It's okay to tell the world you like playing football or watching TV, but beware - you've probably joined some 'hilarious' groups over the years that could prove embarrassing.
  • Your posts and activity: It's not just what you say - if you already have a job, what might an employer think if they spot that you regularly post during the day while at work?

The easiest way to view your social media profiles as a third party would see it is to log out and view it as unregistered user. Note what information is available - if you're not happy with anything that is being shared, check your privacy settings and find out how to make that bit of information private.

Remember - sharing online isn't wrong, but you need to think about how the information you present may be (mis)interpreted. What are you happy for everyone to see, and what do you want to keep to just friends and family?

Share selectively

You don't want to be entirely invisible, particularly on professional social networks like LinkedIn. You should share:

  • Your name: Use your proper name only - no nicknames.
  • Your photo: Use a sensible photo - it doesn't have to be a serious passport-style photo, but stay away from crazy faces or comedy outfits. On Facebook, bear in mind your timeline cover photo will also be public, so keep this sensible as well.
  • Your location: This can be useful for employers – it makes them more confident they’ve found the right person if there are many other people with the same name as you.
  • Your occupational history: This is useful if you plan for your social profile to act as an informal CV of sorts - just remember to keep the information accurate and up-to-date.

Sharing other sections such as your likes and groups can give your profile a bit more personality and show employers another side of you - just remember to check their contents for suitability before making them public!

Delete questionable content

Anyone checking up on your social media accounts won't stop after the first post or two - in some cases employers have been known to dig back years into your social media history.

You should take a look back through your activity and remove (or make private) anything you think might reflect poorly upon your professional identity. Tools exist to help you automatically clean up your social media history.

Simplewa.sh connects with your Twitter and Facebook accounts and scans them for questionable content. You can then simply choose what to keep and what to delete from its list of results.

Tweet Delete is a particularly useful tweet erasing service. While there are many alternatives for mass deletion of tweets, Tweet Delete is unique in that you can set it to automatically delete your tweets after a certain length of time - weeks, months or years.

Don't risk something you said years ago in the past jeopardising your opportunities in the future!
