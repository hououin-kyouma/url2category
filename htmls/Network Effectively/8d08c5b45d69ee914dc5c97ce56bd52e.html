4 ways to improve your medical sales networking

Tweet Share Share
on January 10, 2014 in Social Networking

In any industry, but especially the medical sales industry, building a solid network is a must. With a wide array of people you can talk to about your field and about jobs, hunting for a new position can be much less scary than it may seem. However, to have your network you need to follow the right processes to build it – and there are four simple tips that can especially help out job seekers.

Diversify your networking efforts
It’s true what they say about a secret job market. Up to 80 percent of all new hires are made without the job they apply for ever being formally posted to the public, according to Forbes. But gaining access to this secret network can be easier than it seems, and changing and expanding your networking searches is a main part of the search.

Networking, first, needs to be a habit you undergo throughout your everyday life, not just when you’re in need of a job. Stay in regular contact with your friends and colleagues on websites like LinkedIn and make new connections as often as possible – that way, your network will always be one click away. Offering services in return for services is always a plus, as well – it builds relationships instead of connections, personal investment instead of favors. While you should advertise your need for a new job openly, it is important that you make it simple for someone to help you out – discuss what you’re looking for as specifically as possible.

Contact employers directly
If you decide you want to work for a specific company – for instance, you find the perfect pharmaceutical sales career for your needs – simply contact it if you don’t see the position you’re looking for listed in job postings. It may be difficult or imposing, at first, but many managers will be more than willing to meet with qualified professionals, especially those that will put money back into their budgets. Building a quality cover letter and resume, then finding a hiring manager and contacting them directly, can get your resume to the top of the list if you’re successful. Even if you don’t get anywhere with one employer, your experience will help you gain the confidence to try, and try again.

Attend a conference
Traveling to a local or national conference in your field of choice will give you a chance to talk to professionals, build your network up further and, most importantly, get you inside information about potential openings. Meeting the right person can help you get a reference or interview, provide access to experts who can advise you of your next step or match you up with employers looking to expand their resources. With the various advantages possible at a conference, including in some cases an actual job offer for the very lucky, there’s no reason to not attend at least one.

Take daily opportunities
The right time may come when you least expect it, so it’s good advice to always be on your guard no matter your situation in case you meet the right person, according to the Coloradoan. Even engaging with random people during your daily routine – for instance, discussing apps with someone in front of you in line using a smartphone, or asking about someone’s career when you both have a few minutes – can provide you with experience, leaving you better prepared for future discussions. If things go well, or they may even have a connection to medical sales, asking for a business card or a social media connection on LinkedIn can bolster your hiring resources. It’s a simple, effective way to land the right connection.
