How to make friends in college

EXISTENTIAL BLABBER By Kara Ortiga  (The Philippine Star) | Updated June 13, 2014 - 12:00am
Blonde ambition: Despite being a popular sorority girl, Elle Woods eventually had trouble finding friends once she went to law school.

College isn’t just about getting schooled in the classroom, it’s also a chance to venture into finding a new set of friends.

I started to think that you couldn’t, really. This I learned as a perky college freshman in UP Diliman, overeager, idealistic, and out to prove people wrong. “You won’t make any friends there,” my parents told me, suggesting that my all-girl-private-school background would repel people and rub them the wrong way. I mean, I could see where the prejudice came from, but what an offensive thing for them to say about me! So for the first few weeks of college I had only one goal: to make friends.

Coming from high school and having the same solid barkada for years made the thought of finding new friends daunting. But all my girls had gone down different paths, and because we were more ambitious than sentimental at that point, I didn’t mind being a lone wolf in university. I was brimming with energy, excited to finally get out into the world. I was ready to meet new people, try new things, go to far places, and build a new identity. I didn’t want to just stick to the same cliques I had made in high school, as this was the chance to really meet new people. I made conversation with anybody who looked like they were open to talking (and maybe even those who weren’t).

I talked to a girl who sat beside me during enrollment, the first time I roamed the campus of the university. We agreed to finish enrolling together (because it was such a pain in the ass), we exchanged numbers, and had lunch. I even offered her a ride. I thought to myself, “Oh my gosh, my first friend!” But a few hours later, I realized we came from totally different backgrounds and had absolutely nothing in common — except that we were both looking for friends. Our short-lived relationship ended that very same afternoon that we met.

I struck up conversations with blockmates. They were a noisy, fun bunch that proclaimed themselves the cool kids. I thought they could be my group and I imagined afternoons hanging out with them in the lobby. It didn’t really take long before I realized that their jokes weren’t very funny, and I wasn’t too keen on being a part of their newly formed clique (although they warmly welcomed me).

But the funny thing is, I met real friends in a girl I had met in the line during enrollment period. A week later I saw her again at the freshmen orientation. We recognized each other so I sat at the empty chair beside her; I had no one else to sit with anyway. She was a journalism major who moved to Manila from Iloilo. She introduced me to the girl beside her, someone she had also just met, another journalism major from Cagayan de Oro. I thought they were genuinely cool. We laughed at the same jokes, were comfortable about being ourselves with each other, and aspired for similar things. At first it was all fun, and then there was trust, and then a real friendship was born.

I don’t think there is a fixed formula to making friends. You have to consciously build and maintain friendships. But to find cool people you can hang with, in a sea of strangers is almost like finding a boyfriend. You can sit and wait for them, or you could go out and aggressively look for them yourselves.

Almost seven years since my first day of college, I am happy to report that the girls I made small talk with during freshmen orientation have become incredibly close friends of mine today. We grew up together, got lost together, and found ourselves together. We still laugh about the context of how we met today. How to make friends? Just smile and say hello.
