Friendship for men in middle-age? Here's what you told us about how to find it

Finding friends in middle-age can be difficult for some men. But you had some suggestions for how to do it.

Tom Merton/Getty Images

"You can't stay in your corner of the Forest waiting for others to come to you. You have to go to them sometimes." ~ A. A. Milne

It seems many of you share the sentiments of Winnie-the-Pooh when it comes to friendship - you have to be willing to join in.

We recently looked at the issue of friendship, and how many middle-aged men are missing out on the social, emotional and physical health benefits that come from having good mates.

The figures are confronting. One study found up to 25 per cent of 30 to 65-year-old men had no-one outside their immediate family they felt they could rely on and 37 per cent weren't satisfied with the quality of their relationships, often feeling they were not emotionally connected or supported.

So we decided to crowd-source some ideas on how men (and women) can make and maintain friendships in middle-age (or any other time of their life).

We received lots of different responses, from joining sports teams to volunteering at a local aged care facility, but there's been a constant theme - join in.

Here are some of your ideas.

Games aren't just for kids...

Men missing out on friends in middle age

Middle-aged men missing out on friendship

Studies show middle-aged men who miss out on friendship can face greater physical and mental health risks.

Board games groups and events. A great way to work through anxiety and depression — you can choose to chat and interact with the other participants or focus on the games, whatever suits you. Great way for introverts or socially anxious men to gain social support.

— Brice via Facebook.

Video games are a great way for men to meet and interact with other men and there are many in their 40s, 50s and older now playing on consoles, they allow what is called party chat. I have no doubt party chats have actually saved lives.

— Marcel via Facebook

Piers, who wrote on Facebook, recommended table tennis because you could play, and learn to play, at any age.

Maintain or join some sort of social interest like table tennis or weekly game like cards etc, learn to use social media, and try connecting with people further away via penpal sites.

— Piers, via Facebook

Piers said "online pen pal sites are a bit of a coin toss due to people using them as dating/bride sites". But he said if you chose your pals wisely, you could have friends for life.

... and neither is education

If one has a curious mind or respect for books and learning - try University of the Third Age (U3A). You can learn almost ANY subject for a minimal fee ... If you're not interested in learning — get into a soft martial art (eg yoga or tai chi).

— Dave via Facebook

Play a sport

Joining a sports club helps. I probably know 40 or more at my sports club. We chat and joke. I socialise with several of them outside the club, but the point is, I didn't know any of them before I joined.

— Roy via Facebook

My husband joined the over 60s cricket club. He joined at 58 and was involved in umpiring and social events, and was able to, play once he turned 60. Best thing for him — a cricket player all his life, but osteoarthritis in his knees in his mid 40s put an end to it. But the over 60's rules are modified, and he played competitively even after two knee replacements, and six cardiac bypass grafts when he was 41.

— Angela, via Facebook

Or you could get a dog

Get a dog! Gets you out of the house. You meet other dog walkers. There's no awkwardness, the reason for being there is at the end of the leash.

— Nicholas, via Facebook

Secrets of a 70-year friendship

Secrets of a 70-year friendship

Dave Daniels and Ted Broadhurst met in 1946 and have have been mates for about 70 years.

Nicholas said he joined an early morning walk on the beach every day with a group of people who all brought along their dogs. They don't go every day and people drop in and out, but he said everyone was welcome and there was no pressure to talk or do anything "other than laugh at the mad bunch of pooches".

He said members of the group kept an eye out for each other and when someone had not been seen for a while, "a posse is sent to investigate".

"We care without intruding. The message is there though: You are not alone. For many, this is enough. It's a win-win."

And if dogs aren't your thing, Morgan suggested walking a cat.

I moved interstate recently and didn't know a soul. I don't have a garden in my new home and I have a beautiful little cat, so I started to take him for walks on a lead. This was a great intro card and before long I had become great friends with lots of my neighbours. My life is so much better now that I have some lovely friends nearby!

— Morgan, via Facebook

Volunteering

Many people told us that volunteering had helped them to connect with others. There are plenty of places you can volunteer — community gardens, animal rescue shelters, community groups or even local community services.

Keith Richardson, who wrote to us via email, is a Unit Commander with Marine Rescue NSW. He said was a great way to meet people of all ages, as well as "save lives at sea".

Spending time with elderly can also be rewarding, according to Kevin, who got in touch on Facebook.

Locally we have a group who visit the elderly who also are lonely, just for a hour or so a week. The volunteers don't require any special skills just be willing to keep someone company, I believe a number of church groups do something similar. It doesn't always work but when it does it can start a great friendship both the volunteer and the visitee.

— Kevin, via Facebook

Friendship tips from the experts

Friendship tips from the experts:

  • Explore hobbies and activities you find engaging, positive and productive, as friendships can flourish through shared interests
  • Think quality not quantity. It may be catching up with just one friend, once a month
  • Online is fine too. Supplement face-to-face catch-ups with digital communication — a text or Facebook message. Small steps over time help develop friendships
  • Make friends with women too, or other couples
  • Avoid friendships that revolve around drinking, drugs or uncalculated risk taking

Renee Leary from north-east Victoria wrote to tell us of a pilot project she's involved with that aims to reduce social isolation in people with mental illness by having trained volunteers go into the homes of those with a mental illness.

"Some may just want a reason to get out of bed and having a volunteer visitor to sit with them and have a cuppa is all they need," she said.

"A lot of the volunteers are putting there hand up to be a part of this program as they feel volunteering will too give them a sense of purpose and allow them to feel more engaged in their community," she said.

"It is only very early stages in rolling out this initiative but can only hope for positive outcomes."

'The shed saved my dad's life'

mens sheds

Men and their sheds

If you or someone you know is looking to connect and make friends with men of a similar age, a Men's Shed could be the way to go.

The Men's Sheds movement was formed and decade ago and since then has taken off across Australia, providing a powerful avenue for men to connect and socialise.

That same opportunity to make friends and share skills is now available via your preferred device at The Shed Online, a virtual community for men.

Founded by beyondblue, The Movember Foundation and the Australian Men's Shed Association, the Shed Online is a space for men to talk and interact with other men.

It also provides men with information on health and wellbeing.

(Photo: ABC South West/Anthony Pancia)

We were also inundated with stories of how men's sheds helped to turn people's lives around.

[It's] a place where men can go learn something, teach something and make something with other like-minded blokes. Woodwork, welding. fixing things and having something in common to meet new mates. A similar group near me rebuild a car that was donated and gave it to a needing family. Good blokes doing good things together always makes mates.

— David, via Facebook

Kay wrote to us on Facebook to tell us about her father's experience.

She said a men's shed really helped him after her mother passed away, and the mates he had made were the most important health support he had received.

"He goes to the shed three times a week and even if he is sick and unwell, his mates will come and visit at home or in hospital. They go to outings together, they support each other, they fundraise together. They are a brotherhood," she says.

"The shed saved my dad's life."

Find a group that suits you and join in

Dean Craig, on Facebook, summed it up, encouraging men to join clubs or classes that suited their own personal interests — whatever they may be.

The range is endless be it golf, bushwalking, model aircraft, bridge, dancing, 4WD off-roading, whatever. Many clubs will also involve physical activity with attendant health benefits.

— Dean Craig, via Facebook
