How to Still Have a Social Life in College Without Joining a Sorority

The possibilities are truly endless.

Courtney Lindstrand Aug 20, 2015 12:12PM EDT

Sometimes, it feels like the golden ticket to having a social life in college is handed to you at the end of the Greek rush process. If you've ever scrolled through Instagram posts of bid day or a sorority formal event, you know what we're talking about. Something about a picture with 15 girls crammed into the frame, grinning from ear to ear, can make you feel like the only way to have real fun in college is to rush a sorority.

While it's undeniable that Greek organizations definitely have their social perks and can foster really tight bonds between girls very quickly, joining a sorority isn't for everyone. There's plenty of things to consider before rushing and if you decide (for whatever reason) that it's not for you, you may feel a little bit scared about how your social life in college might pan out. But never fear! There are tons of ways to meet new people and make new friends in college (more than any other phase of life, actually). Here, a few simple suggestions for how to develop a thriving social life outside of the greek scene:

Try Out for a Sports Team
If you were involved in sports in high school, joining the college version of that team is a surefire way to form a solid group of buds. Another plus? Colleges usually have different levels of competition, from just-for-fun co-ed intramural leagues all the way up to the more competitive varsity teams. Meeting up for practice every week will make forming friendships feel natural — and that euphoric feeling you'll share when you win a championship game won't hurt either. They don't call it team bonding for nothing.

Join an Academic Organization or Club
Despite how nerdy the moniker "academic club" may sound, there are tons of education-based clubs out there that are loads of fun. You may find your best friends working late nights writing up articles for the school newspaper or organizing a huge event that benefits a local charity. An added bonus? These activities will also help beef up your resume — so the social aspect is really just the cherry on top of an awesome learning experience.

Apply for a Work-Study Job
Not only will securing a work-study job earn you some extra cash, these jobs can also be a way to form bonds with your coworkers. Whether you're shelving books in the campus bookstore or working the front desk at the student gym, there's a good chance you'll be paired up with someone who's fun to pass the time with. Making dough while also making lasting friendships? Sounds like a win-win to us.

Nab a Cool Internship
Sounds counterintuitive, but you can even look outside of campus to make friends with fellow students. Oftentimes, local businesses (or corporations, if you're in a larger city) are looking for extra help in the form of interns. While internships obviously look amazing on your resume and their main purpose is to help you gain practical experience for a future career, they can also be a great way to meet new people. Companies often hire more than one intern at a time, so you could be grouped with students that share your interests and who may even end up being your colleagues somewhere down the road.

Explore Classes That Are Notorious for Assigning Group Projects
While you may cringe at the thought of a group project, hear us out. Group assignments in college often pan out much differently than they did in high school. Since everyone is motivated to do well and has a lot of free time between classes, coordinating schedules to work on the task is a lot easier than it was back in the day. Plus, some of the group projects you might be assigned are even (dare we say it?) fun. Someone you meet while working on a community outreach project for your Sociology 101 class could very well end up being your BFF.

Go On a Service-Based Trip
Once the holiday season and summer break roll around, you'll probably start seeing a ton of flyers advertising service trips in far-flung locales. A lot of organizations recruit college kids to volunteer in less fortunate communities during university breaks. Whether it's teaching English in South America or helping potential business owners secure loans in Africa, there are tons of really cool volunteer opportunities abroad that may apply to what you're studying. As far as making a social splash, these trips usually recruit dozens of students — sometimes from multiple schools across the country — to carry out the task. That means dozens of opportunities to make friends that you'll explore a foreign country with (the ultimate bonding activity) while you're also making a global impact.
