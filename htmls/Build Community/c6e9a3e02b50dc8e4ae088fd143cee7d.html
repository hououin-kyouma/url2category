A Heart for Giving: Finding the Right Charity
November 20th, 2013 by Hannah Doyle

Artful knots twist and turn around a crochet hook, although not as fast as they used to. Naomi Nelson’s hands dance a routine while her thoughts drift elsewhere. She has been doing this since she was 3 years old.

Naomi Nelson, left, with Operation Christmas Child staff members Mary Damron, center, and Susanne Price. While crocheting each pair of shoes, Naomi prays for the child who will receive them.
Photo courtesy of Colleen Ruehl

Now 84, Naomi sits in her recliner at her home in Tallahassee, unable to leave because of her health.

“It takes patience to crochet, a lot of patience,” Naomi says. “These people all over Florida send me yarn.”

Naomi hasn’t paid for yarn since 2002. She crochets nearly 300 shoes a year for Operation Christmas Child, a charity of Samaritan’s Purse.
The nationwide charity collects shoeboxes filled with goods such as toys, washcloths and clothes, and gives them to children in developing countries.

Each pair of shoes Naomi makes is picked up from her home and put into a shoebox. The boxes then go to a regional processing center and are inspected for unapproved items such as sharp objects and liquids. Then the boxes are shipped through customs and land by the crate full in one of the 101 countries OCC serves.

Tom Dedricks spent a week in Uganda handing out shoeboxes at churches, schools and orphanages.

“I just love the fact that I can make ’em and somebody else can give ’em out,” Naomi says, “and I feel like I’m a link in the chain.”

On the other end of that chain are people such as Tom Dedricks. Last year, Tom, OCC Jacksonville area coordinator, opened up crates of shoeboxes and handed them to Ugandan boys and girls.

“So many have never received a gift and clutched it close to their heart,” Tom says. “The smiles on their faces made it so worthwhile.”

Tom was one of 12 from the Florida and Puerto Rico region who handed out shoeboxes at orphanages and schools with 45 to 200 children.

“To me, (OCC) is the greatest program to bring Christianity to those willing to accept Christianity,” Tom says. “You’re touching the most vulnerable of children.”

For Tom, volunteering with OCC provides a way to give and show his faith.

Samaritan’s Purse is one of thousands of charities through which people can give, and the number seems to double during the holidays. How do you choose the charity that is right for you?

Navigating the maze of options is daunting and discerning is difficult, but follow this guide to keep it simple without becoming Scrooge.

Donation Determinants

Assess your preferences to narrow your charities from nationwide to nearby countryside. What do you care about? Is someone close to you sick or struggling with a disease? Does a particular cause speak to you?

Where there is an interest, there is a need. Besides the charities involving sickness, children or animals, charities are based around cooking, painting, building and even motorcycles.

Some ask for monetary donations, others ask for both money and time. Charities can be entirely local or entirely global. Some may be global with local offices. It all depends how you want to give and make a difference.

Naomi was introduced to OCC through her church.

Before searching online, talk to friends, family or people you know who are involved with a charity. That can offer built-in legitimacy and a peek inside the workings of the charity before opening your wallet or day planner.

Marks of Merit

Unfortunately, the likelihood of knowing someone who is involved in every charity you are interested in is slim. Fortunately, once you have specific charities in mind, a number of websites do most of the legwork for you.

CharityWatch.org, CharityNavigator.org and Givewell.org are useful tools. Each site breaks down different charities by how they use donations and run their programs.

All of the websites rate and rank the charities, but each site has different charities scoring the highest. That is because each site has different criteria and different values assigned to the criteria.

GiveWell considers skills such as the strength of impact on people’s lives and the amount of room a charity has for additional funding, while CharityNavigator.org bases its ratings on financial health and reporting results.

However, all well-operated charities share common traits.

Without spending hours reading the fine print of charity websites, you can gauge if a charity is worth investing your time and money by being aware of the following characteristics:

• Transparency. The more open and accessible a charity is about its operations, the better. You should be able to access as much data as needed to understand where your donations are going and get a sense of their impact. Most charities make accessible everything from their CEO’s salary to their annual report.

• Accountability. Having charity information readily available is important, but so are the explanations for the actions they take. You should be able to ask questions about what the charity is doing to progress toward its goal and why.

• Low overhead. Low administration costs usually mean more money is spent on the charity’s mission. The American Institute of Philanthropy says charities should use at least 60 percent of their funds toward their program.

Just because the charity is frugal on its administration expenses does not mean it is effective in carrying out the cause it supports. Look at all the factors holistically to evaluate the quality of the charity.

You can find this information online using the aforementioned websites or by calling the charity directly.

The Way to Will

One or multiple charities may meet your standards. Once you land on where you want to give, you have to establish how.

Consider the best way to give money based on the charity. If it is a project-based cause, a one-time donation may be best. If the cause is ongoing, you either can donate all at once or you may want to setup an account with the charity and have a specific amount regularly deducted from your bank account.

Find the sweet spot between your preferences and the charity.

Money is a welcomed gift in obvious ways, but the gift of time is equally as valuable. Virtually no charity will turn down volunteers who offer hands and feet toward their efforts.

Many need help with delivery, assembly, maintenance or just taking notes.

The work involved in running a charity is endless. Help is needed in almost every area. If you are a craftsman, try repairing broken rocking chairs or building a table. Your gift of giving will help others and yourself if you use your skills, as Naomi Nelson does.

“I’m not able to get out of the house, so I said what can I do for God? I can crochet,” says Naomi, who has been making shoes 11 years for OCC.
Naomi lives by herself, and is committed to living by necessity rather than excessively, putting others before herself.

“I live as cheaply as I can,” she says.

No matter how you give—whether dropping a coin in the Salvation Army red kettle, donating online or by packing up a shoebox—a charitable contribution will last longer than any gift placed under the Christmas tree.

“That’s my philosophy,” Naomi says. “Just do what I can for others, and I get blessed every time.”
