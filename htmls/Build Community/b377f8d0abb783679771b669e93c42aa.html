Why It’s So Hard To Make Friends Freshman Year

September 11, 2015 by Julie Zeilinger
8 1

I remember the exact moment I decided to come to terms with never again having friendships as close the ones I had with my best friends from childhood. I was sitting in my dorm room, studying for midterms on a Friday night — not because I had to but because it legitimately felt like the best option available. “Well,” I may or may not have thought to myself, “You got stupid lucky in high school and somehow ended up having a group of best friends who are some of the greatest people in the world. This place is swarming with complete strangers who are nice and smart but will probably never be your people.” I then stared off into the dark abyss of my socially isolated future, masochistically reveling in self-pity.

Of course, I would soon realize that feeling socially frustrated is a pretty common first semester freshman year experience and not an actual prediction of the next four years. While partying in college may be relatively straightforward, the process of making new, authentic friends is uniquely challenging in a way that nobody really prepares you for. Being thrown into a group of strangers is legitimately scary and by no means easy to navigate, and yet because all freshmen are trying to make friends and are hyperaware of how they’re coming off, they tend to pretend like they’re not struggling. Nobody wants anybody to think they’re pathetic or admit that they’re lonely so instead everybody pretends that they’re having the best time ever and that people they met a few weeks ago in English class are their friend soul mates.

The key thing to remember is that most freshmen struggle to make new friends, albeit to varying degrees. But it is possible to avoid graduating a social leper, especially with these tips in mind:

Patience is a virtue: real connections take time.

My tolerance for small talk is virtually nonexistent so wading through the endless superficial flow of “What’s your name / where are you from / what is your major” that is Freshman Orientation felt akin to nails on a chalkboard every single time. I could only take so many awkward silences and overcompensating fake laughs without longing for the easy banter I had with my high school friends. But I soon realized everybody I was talking to was probably thinking the same thing and I was likely coming off in much the same way to them as they were to me. It’s important not to dismiss people based on first impressions – especially in a high-pressure time of transition like first semester freshman year. I am willing to expose my inner bitch and admit that I totally judged some of the girls who ended up being the closest friends I’ve made in college for stupid, inconsequential crap. But had I not continued to hang out with them, I wouldn’t have made it past the freshman posturing insecurity to the authentic friendships I have today.

Be yourself. 

No this isn’t a Very Special / Self Evident Episode of Barney, although this information is frantically imparted to toddlers by wiser/regretful adults for a reason. First of all, you will form the strongest, most enduring bonds with those who know the real you. It may sound obvious but if we’re all going to be honest with ourselves, we have to admit that while most of us are not pathological liars with delusions of grandeur (those are apparently just the people assigned to be our roommates) we can probably all think of little things about ourselves we try to hide or play up to seem more desirable to others. But the truth is, the people who reject you for those things ultimately aren’t worth having in your life anyway. You want people who will take the good and the bad – and, importantly, you must remember to do the same in return.

Your friendships from home will change, too.

I probably idealized my high school friendships my first semester and self-destructively convinced myself nobody new could ever measure up, which, of course, turned out to be bullshit. Just because I was in a new place with new people who hadn’t witnessed the full spectrum of my awkward, insecure adolescence didn’t mean I was doomed to never connect with anybody – I just had to put in the time and effort (see Tip 1). Additionally, when the holidays came around and my high school friends and I got together over Thanksgiving and Winter break, I realized that our bond was already starting to change as an inevitable consequence of time and distance. More than anything, it was a matter of practicality: we were all embedded in different cultures, surrounded by different people and other influential factors and all changed in ways that created an unprecedented gap. Of course, we enjoyed (and still enjoy) spending time together and remain in each other lives, but old friendships will change. As you travel through the college experience it’s natural to make room for both new friends and a new, growing version of yourself.

At the end of the day, when it comes to making true friends your freshman year, the biggest favor you can do for yourself and others is to embrace vulnerability instead of pretending like you have it figured out and have SUPER AWESOME plans all the time. Put yourself out there. Admit to your roommate or hallmate that you’re trying to meet new people and would love to be included in their plans. Start a conversation with whoever is sitting next to you in class. Be yourself, be open to new people and trust me, you’ll find your people.
