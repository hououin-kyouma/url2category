10 Surefire Ways to Make Friends in College

Megan McLachlan
on December 8, 2014 at 3:30 pm


Whether you’re thinking about college or are in the throes of it, the idea of making friends and socializing during your undergraduate years can be intimidating.

With so many people and so many opportunities, where do you start in cultivating a group of friends? As many will tell you, you won’t always be best friends with your roommate (which can be a good thing). Here are some strategies to make friends in college:

1. Get Involved
Don’t just sit in your dorm room or apartment. One really good—and direct—way to make friends is to be involved in activities. Some common college extracurriculars where friends can be made include clubs, volunteering, intramurals and other athletic teams, and Greek life.

When it comes to joining college groups, a good strategy for getting involved and making friends is also to participate in something you have an interest in. If you’re engaged in an activity that interests you, it’s all the more likely you’ll meet like-minded people.

2. Secure a Campus Job
There are work-study jobs for every personality, and finding one may introduce you to some characters you’ll get along with. Working on campus allows you to meet and work with a variety of people and can be beneficial to both your bank and Facebook accounts.

3. Use Social Media
Speaking of Facebook, don’t be afraid to be on it, or other similar networks, when it comes to making friends in college. Social media can be a useful tool when it comes to posting events, creating class pages, and more.

4. Participate in Hall Activities
Resident assistants are often required to have hall events, and these are good opportunities for hallmates to meet each other and socialize. If your RA is having an event, check it out. You may leave with some new friends who live down the hall from you.

5. Start a Study Group
Struggling in one particular class? You may not be alone. Pulling classmates together to form a study group may kill two items off your to-do list with one No. 2 pencil: Make friends and make the grade.

6. Study in Public Places
Consider going to coffee shops and even the library to study. You may strike up a conversation with fellow coffee drinkers or spot someone in your class. However, if you find you get more work done in your room, leave your door open. This makes you a bit more approachable to people that pass by.

7. Attend Parties
College is filled with get-togethers and parties of many different styles. Find one that’s right for you. If you prefer dance parties, you may find similar extroverted individuals there. On the other hand, if a chill jam session in an off-campus apartment sounds better to you, you’ll want to hit this one up. Either way, going where a lot of people go affords you the opportunity to encounter unique temperaments.

8. Do Some Networking
Is Mom pushing for you to get in touch with her best friend from high school’s son or daughter? Do it. You have nothing to lose. You may not become the best of friends with this person, but they could introduce you to people you’ll get along with. Think of every experience as an opportunity to meet someone new.

9. Don’t Always Be on Your Phone
Instead of walking to class with your head down on your phone or scrolling through Twitter while waiting for the bus, put your phone away. People are more likely to approach someone if they aren’t on their phone,. Follow the “Hello Walk” traditions at University of Idaho and Purdue University, which encourage students to say hello to each other around campus.

10. Take a Non-Traditional Class
Sometimes classes don’t always afford the opportunities to meet people, depending on the format. On the other hand, unique classes like Tai Chi, guitar, or yoga may nurture more casual conversation and friendships.

The Bottom Line
To make friends in college, the best rule of thumb is to put yourself out there and be approachable and friendly. Even if you’re a senior and feel like you need more (or maybe new) friends, it’s never too late to start over. While you’ll take what you learn in college with you into your career, sometimes it’s the relationships and friendships you look back on most fondly.
