Charity Watch Web Quest

To Give or Not to Give

The purpose of this web quest is to teach you how get the most out of your donations. We will show you a handful of useful (and some not) web sites that will enable you to find charities and projects, evaluate them for their worth, and ultimately make a decision based on research and reasoning as to whether or not you (or anyone for that matter) should donate to the charity of project. With so many different groups of people asking for donations nowadays, there is a lot to learn. So let's get started.

It is important to know these three terms as they are often used interchangeably. However, there are some slight differences. For this web quest, we will use the term "charity" when discussing organizations that attempt to do good int he world since it seems to be the most specific. 

There are millions of charities around the world (1.5 million in the USA alone) all with different budgets, sizes, missions, and reputations. Finding the right charity is not easy. A good place to start is the Worldwide NGO Directory (WANGO). To help familiarize yourself with this site, answer this question:

Watchdog Sites

While WANGO can help you find charities around the world, it can’t really help you find good ones, and unfortunately, there are just as many bad charities as good ones. It’s important to remember that popular does not mean “good.” Just because you have heard of an charity or see one in a commercial, it does not mean that the charity uses its money well or runs its organization efficiently. Luckily for many of the larger NGOs, there are  watchdog sites. These sites help rate and rank charity and help people decide which charities they might want to help.  Obviously, these  watchdog sites cannot assess every charity (there are just too many), so it focuses mostly on the large, well known ones that people are most likely to donate to. To learn more about these  watchdog sites, answer the following ten questions (5-21).  Let's start with the most well known  watchdog site:  Charity Navigator. 

Charity Navigator has a lot to offer interested donors. It is much more than just a watchdog site. For example, from this site you can find tips on how to choose organizations and what questions to ask them. You can find information on how to donate your car, how to get organizations to stop calling you, how to calculate your tax breaks based on your donations, and, perhaps most importantly, how they rate the different charities. There are also some pretty interesting  Top 10 lists. Let’s start with the Top 10 lists (Hint: the link is towards the top of the page).

Charity Hunters

By now, you should be pretty familiar with the concept of watchdog sites. While these sites are essential to charity research, they are not without their flaws. First, they can be  quite cumbersome and time consuming. Second, they limit their ratings to large scale charities. A second type of web site -- we'll call them  charity hunters  -- try to solve both of these problems. These web sites  cut to the chase and identify only a handful of good charities, sometimes including on their list some smaller, lesser known charities that deserve our attention. Whenever you are using  charity hunter  web sites, you must be careful to check for biases (this is their major flaw). If biases are limited, however, chances are you have found a gold mine of good charities, or at the very least, a great starting point (be sure to always cross-reference any charity using the watchdog sites).

Crowdfunding Sites

​While charity hunters are great for  quickly locating some good charities, they too have their flaws: 1) their lists are pretty limited in number, and 2) they don't really dig deep to find the small, grassroots charities that are doing great work in the lesser known corners of the world.  So how can we find those hidden gems? If we are serious about sustainable development, avoiding paternalism, and truly helping the global community, we need to become better at finding these less-known, albeit amazing, charities that are supporting specific communities around the world. 

One way to find these types of charities is by researching on crowdfunding sites. These sites offer an interesting, bottom-up approach to finding small charities. Crowdfunding sites allow individual people, groups, or charities to post specific projects to their site. Donors then visit these crowdfunding sites,  search for projects, and donate to these specific projects. The crowdfunding site will then take a percentage of the donation for their own profit (it's usually pretty small, but be sure to check before you donate), and the rest goes directly toward funding the specific project. While this plan is not fool-proof (there are still many cases of fake projects), it does seem to work remarkably well for a few reasons:
  1. It connects donors to little known charities that are doing some amazing works,
  2. These small charities get a large percentage of the donation (remember, some large charities take up to 25% of donations for themselves), thus ensuring donors more bang for their buck, and
  3. It supports a bottom-up, grassroots approach to aid and development which is essential to sustainability and long lasting changes. 

Now that you know a little bit about crowdfunding sites, let's explore some of them. Before we begin, it is important to remember that crowdfunding sites can vary significantly in purpose. Some sites look to fund individuals ( gofundme.com), others might focus on inventions ( kickstarter.com), while others  focus a lot of their attention on providing small  charities an international platform to raise money for much needed projects. These will be the types of crowdfunding sites that we visit.

When All Else Fails

Ultimately, the best way to find these little known charities is through personal experience. That's right, get out, see the world, and form some real partnerships with some really awesome grassroots charities. This method, as you can imagine, has a couple pros and  cons.
PROS:
  1. By personally visiting the charity, you can be assured that the small organization is actually reputable.
  2. By personally visiting the charity, you can form a lasting relationship with the people who run it. Relationships are essential to good aid. 
CONS:
  1. It costs a lot of money to visit a small charity in another country.
  2. It is pretty time consuming to visit a small charity in another country.

If you have time and you have money one day, using it to form a connection with a small, grassroots charity in a remote corner of the world is a pretty exceptional use of resources. Not only will you learn a lot, but if your relationship is real, you will become more than a donor; you will become a partner. This is the best type of helping. 

Using this method while I was living in Ghana allowed me to locate a handful of small charities that do amazing work in their respective communities. You can find these charities by visiting  Project Wisdom's  Partners page. The global charities are listed on the left-hand column.
