Making Friends in College

Share via Facebook or Email

August 8, 2016
Posted by Lindsay Bressman
0 comments
Learning & Resources

A huge fear of a surprisingly large number of incoming college freshmen is how they will find new friends. 

This makes a great deal of sense, actually, considering that the last time many college freshmen made new friends was in middle school, seven years earlier.  Since almost everyone is in the same position (some just hide their fears better), one would presume that the problem simply works itself out. For many kids, it does. But for a substantial amount of students, friend-making – a critical step to feeling truly engaged on campus – remains difficult.

Having spoken to countless upperclassmen, almost all of whom went through this very issue, I’ve learned that there are a few tactics freshmen can use to speed up the process of fitting in and finding friends.

Orientation

Colleges are invested in students sticking around, prompting the freshman orientation (and sometimes even pre-orientation) phase.  Take advantage of these opportunities, as everyone with whom you’ll interact is in exactly your shoes.  My close friend from college met his future wife at orientation; you don’t need to do that to be successful, but even a modicum of friendliness will yield you huge rewards.

Just say “Hi”

“What do I even say?” to other kids is a common question asked by nervous incoming freshmen.  Suggestion:  Just say “Hi” and introduce yourself.  At orientation…in class…on those long, winding college paths…in the dining hall…and especially in your dorm. Remember that most of the people you’ll be meeting feel as awkward about this as you do. Just start with the basics!

Clubs

The more actively engaged you are in any of the loads of clubs and groups available on every college campus, the more the friend base (and future network) expands.  The best part about this is meeting kids who come from different towns or countries, or had a disparate upbringing, but realizing a shared interest in music, sports, literature, politics or even dining hall cuisine!

Finally, don’t worry about latching on too tightly with your first friend group. College is not high school; if this crowd gets you through month one, that’s great.  As you meet other people in future classes and activities, friend groups evolve and most likely, students will  make new friends and ultimately establish more secure friendships with people who are a better fit.

Randy Shain

Founder, One on One Mentors, LLC
