How to Make New, Legit Friends After College

Andrea Syrtash
March 29, 2016 10:20 am

As kids, we think we'll be best friends forever with the people we spend the most time with. When we get older, we realize that friendships happen by choice—not by circumstance.

The reality is that just as we evolve, so does our circle of friends. Some friends move far away or we drift apart because of different interests; and other friends may still be close to us, but in different chapters of their lives.

While you love your friend who now has a husband and baby, understandably her schedule has changed so she can no longer drop everything to meet you for an emergency style session or drink after work.

So, if you're looking to expand your social network and pick up new friends, here are some ideas:

Try a Meetup

Almost every city has events and Meetups available based on a variety of interests. Search for your city and your interest (e.g., "Film Lovers Club, Chicago") to find the next gathering and commit to going to one of their events if meeting new people is a priority.

The reason to sign up for these outings is two-fold: First, you'll meet like-minded people to potentially cultivate a friendship with and second, if you plan an event around something you enjoy doing, you'll enjoy yourself whether or not you connect with someone new.

Be Social on Social Media

These days, many of us are connected to people on social media who we don't really know beyond their posts. Enjoying someone's Instagram pictures or funny tweets isn't the basis for a friendship, of course, but having the digital connection is a great way to break the ice.

If someone on one of your networks piques your interest and lives locally, send her a direct message inviting her to an event or asking if she'd want to meet over a cup of coffee. Not everyone wants to take their digital relationships offline, but it's worth looking over your friends' list to see if you may be able to meet IRL.

Make the First Move

The next time you're at an event or party, consider picking up people for friendship. Rather than waiting to be approached, make the first move and approach someone with a genuine smile and interest.

Etiquette expert Thomas P. Farley suggests that you consider arriving at an event or party on time, not late, if you're looking to meet new people. He says, "This will give you an opportunity to meet people before they have "hooked up" (friendship-wise) with other party guests. Having a few new acquaintances in the room will give you the courage and the wherewithal to expand from there, meeting more folks as you go and even to be being an 'introducer' for new arrivals."

Find Common Ground

No need to play 20 questions when you meet someone new, but being genuinely curious about someone and asking questions about her interests, background, and/or where she grew up will lead to finding out if you have commonalities.

Staying curious and open-minded is an important part of any kind of networking, whether it's for business, love, or friendship. Just because someone may not look like your "type," it doesn't mean that you won't realize you have a connection once you learn more.

Be Enthusiastic, Not Needy

While you want to show genuine interest in learning more about a new potential friend, you don't want to come on too strong so it seems like she is your only social option. Trying to book multiple plans immediately or acting close before you even know each other may create unnecessary pressure.

Book a coffee meeting or invite your new friend to an upcoming event. Farley says, "Start small…meeting up for coffee is generally a safe bet and also comes with a reduced chance of your interest being interpreted as a romantic one as opposed to a friendship one." He also recommends connecting on social media after the fact—just limit it to one channel, so you don't look overeager.

Keep Up the Momentum

These days we have so many things on our plate that it can be easy to lose track of new connections that we were initially excited about. For this reason, it's a good idea to be proactive in terms of making plans.

According to Farley, "Don't be flaky. If you've parted ways expressing mutual interest in seeing one another again, waiting too long to get back in touch sends the signal you were not genuinely interested."

You can send a simple note with something like, "It was great to see you the other night! I'd love to continue the conversation we had. How does your schedule look in the next week or two after work for a drink or coffee?"

When meeting new people consider Dale Carnegie's famous book, How to Win Friends and Influence People : Worry less about impressing others and let others impress you. By approaching people with genuine curiosity and confidence, you'll be picking up new friends in no time.
